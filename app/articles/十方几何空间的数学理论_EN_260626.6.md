## Chapter 2 Axiomatization of the Excitation Parameter Space

### 2.1 Axiom 1 (Topology of the Parameter Space)

**Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** The excitation parameter space $D$ is obtained from the one-dimensional compact connected manifold $S^1$ (circle) by removing two distinct points $p_0$ and $p_*$:

$$D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}, \qquad p_0 \neq p_*$$

The two removed points are respectively called the **vacuum point** ($p_0$) and the **degenerate point** ($p_*$). Consequently, $D$ consists of two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1), \qquad D_- \cong (0,1)$$

Each component is homeomorphic to an open interval. The parameter $x \in D_\pm$ labels the excited state.

**Lemma 2.1 (Topological Structure of the Components)** Let $S^1$ be the standard unit circle $\{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi \in [0,2\pi)\}$. Removing the two points $p_0=e^{i\phi_0}$ and $p_*=e^{i\phi_*}$ (assume without loss of generality $0\leq \phi_0 < \phi_* < 2\pi$), then $S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}$ has exactly two connected components:
$$D_+ = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_0 < \phi < \phi_*\}, \quad D_- = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_* < \phi < \phi_0+2\pi\}.$$
Each component, as a subspace topology of $S^1$, is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0,1)$.

*Proof.* Consider the standard covering map $\pi: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $\pi(\phi)=e^{i\phi}$. On $\mathbb{R}$, $\pi^{-1}(S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}) = \mathbb{R}\setminus\{\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi \mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}$. Each connected component of this set is an open interval $(\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi)$ or $(\phi_*+2k\pi, \phi_0+2(k+1)\pi)$. Since the restriction of $\pi$ to intervals of length $2\pi$ is a homeomorphism, the images of these open intervals in $S^1$ are exactly $D_+$ and $D_-$. Each open interval is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, hence $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$. $\square$

### 2.2 Axiom 2 (Analytic Properties of the Geometric Quantity)

**Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** On the parameter space $D$ there exists a geometric quantity $S: D \to (0,+\infty)$ satisfying:

1. **Continuity**: $S$ is continuous on each connected component $D_\pm$;
2. **Vacuum Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_0$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$;
3. **Degenerate Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_*$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$.

**Remark 2.1** Axiom 2 does not require $S$ to have the same functional expression on $D_+$ and $D_-$, nor does it require $S$ to be continuous on all of $D$ ($p_0$ and $p_*$ are not in $D$). Thus $S$ can be different functions on the two independent components, as long as each satisfies the boundary conditions.

### 2.3 Range Theorem

**Theorem 2.1 (Range)** Under Axioms 1 and 2, the range of $S$ on each connected component $D_\pm$ is $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$.

*Proof.* By Axiom 1, $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is a connected set. By Axiom 2, $S$ is continuous on $D_\pm$, hence the image set $S(D_\pm)$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e., an interval. By the vacuum limit, $\inf S(D_\pm) = 0$; by the degenerate limit, $\sup S(D_\pm) = +\infty$. Since $p_0, p_* \notin D$, the endpoints $0$ and $+\infty$ are both unattainable. Therefore $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed Elaboration:** The connectedness argument can be further unfolded as follows. Let $D_+ \cong (0,1)$, $S: D_+ \to \mathbb{R}$ continuous. Suppose $S(D_+)$ is not an interval, then there exist $\alpha < \beta < \gamma$ such that $\alpha, \gamma \in S(D_+)$ but $\beta \notin S(D_+)$. Let $U = S^{-1}((-\infty, \beta))$ and $V = S^{-1}((\beta, +\infty))$, then $U,V$ are disjoint open sets in $D_+$, and $U\cup V = D_+$, contradicting the connectedness of $D_+$. Hence $S(D_+)$ must be an interval. Combined with the boundary limits, this interval must be $(0,+\infty)$.

### 2.4 Existence Theorem and Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis

**Theorem 2.2 (Existence)** For any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, there exists at least one excited state $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$ on each branch such that $S(x_+) = S(x_-) = S_0$.

*Proof.* By Theorem 2.1, $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. For any $S_0 > 0$, clearly $S_0 \in (0, +\infty)$. Hence $S^{-1}(S_0) \cap D_\pm \neq \varnothing$. $\square$

**Definition 2.1' (Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis)** If $S$ is strictly monotonic on $D_\pm$, then for any $S_0>0$, the $x_+$ and $x_-$ in Theorem 2.2 are uniquely determined. Strict monotonicity is **not** a theorem derivable from Axioms 1–2, but a **constructive hypothesis** of the framework. Its introduction is equivalent to requiring $S$ to constitute a global coordinate function on $D_\pm$.

*Proof.* A strictly monotonic continuous function is injective, hence the preimage is unique. $\square$

$$a = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_1^\Delta}}, \quad b = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_3^\Delta}}, \quad c = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\Lambda k_0 \cdot \lambda_1^\Delta}}.$$

**Self-Consistency Condition** A set of positive numbers $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ are the first three non-zero eigenvalues of some $M(a)$ if and only if

$$\lambda_2 = \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda_3 = \Lambda(S_3) \lambda_1 = 3\lambda_1.$$

In this case $a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1}$.

**Proof.** By Theorem 3.2, $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2 \Rightarrow a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1^\Delta}$; $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3/b^2 \Rightarrow b = \sqrt{3/\lambda_3^\Delta}$; by the proportional constraint of Definition 3.1, $c = a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$. The self-consistency condition follows directly from $\lambda_2^\Delta = 8/a^2$ and $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3\Lambda/a^2$. $\square$

**Lemma 3.1 (One-to-One Correspondence between Spectral Data and Geometry)** Under the locking of the interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$ determined by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, there exists an explicit one-to-one mapping between the first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues and the scale factors $(a,b,c)$. ### 3.6 Spectral Rigidity Theorem

**Theorem 3.4 (Spectral Rigidity of Constrained Product Spheres)** Let $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ be two constrained product spheres with the same interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$. **Proof.** By Proposition 3.2, the first non-zero Laplace eigenvalue is $\lambda_1^\Delta=3/a^2$. By the proportional constraints of Definition 3.1, $b=a/\sqrt{\Lambda}$, $c=a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$, the scale triple $(a,b,c)$ is uniquely determined, hence $M(a')$ and $M(a)$ are isometric. $\square$

### 3.8 Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of Riemannian manifolds and $\mathcal{M}$ its moduli space (i.e., the set of isometry classes). Suppose $\mathcal{M}$ has a smooth manifold structure of dimension $d$. Define the spectral map $\sigma_k$ that sends each isometry class to the vector of its first $k$ non-zero Laplace eigenvalues:

$$\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k, \quad [g] \mapsto (\lambda_1^\Delta, \lambda_2^\Delta, \dots, \lambda_k^\Delta).$$

**Theorem 3.6 (Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity)** Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the moduli space of a class of manifolds with finite dimension $d$. If the first $k$ Laplace eigenvalues can achieve spectral rigidity (i.e., $\sigma_k$ is injective), then necessarily $d \leq k$. Under the interlocking constants determined by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, the moduli space of $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ has dimension $d=1$, hence $k\geq1$ suffices to satisfy the necessary condition.

**Proof.** Suppose $d > k$. Since eigenvalues depend continuously on the metric, the spectral map $\sigma_k$ is a continuous map. If $\sigma_k$ is injective, then $\sigma_k$ gives a continuous injection from the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ into $\mathbb{R}^k$.

By the **Brouwer Invariance of Domain** theorem: If $U \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f: U \to \mathbb{R}^k$ is a continuous injection, then $f(U)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ (with respect to the subspace topology). However, every point of a $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ has an open neighborhood $U$ homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^d$. If $d > k$, then $f(U)$ as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$ cannot have topological dimension exceeding $k$, while $U$ has topological dimension $d > k$, contradicting the Invariance of Domain theorem ($f(U)$ cannot be open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ because its topological dimension is insufficient). Therefore, a $d$-dimensional manifold cannot be mapped continuously and injectively into $\mathbb{R}^k$ when $d > k$.

Hence if $d > k$, a continuous injection $\sigma_k$ cannot exist. Spectral rigidity requires $d \leq k$. $\square$

**Detailed Elaboration:** The core of Brouwer's Invariance of Domain theorem is: if $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^d$ is a continuous injection, then $f(U)$ is also open. When $d>k$, viewing $\mathbb{R}^k$ as a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^d$, no continuous injection $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^k$ (with $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ open) can exist, because $f(U)$ has no interior points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (its topological dimension is $k<d$), while Invariance of Domain requires $f(U)$ to be open in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (after embedding $\mathbb{R}^k$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$). A more direct argument: if $d>k$, then the image $f(U)\subset\mathbb{R}^k$ of any open set $U$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has zero Lebesgue measure (since $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$), but a continuous injection maps open sets to open sets (in the relative topology), and an open set in $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$, contradicting the fact that $U$ has positive measure in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

**Remark 3.6' (Dimension Counting Argument)** Theorem 3.6 can also be derived directly from standard differential topology dimension counting: if $d>k$, then no open set $U$ of a $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ can be embedded continuously and injectively into $\mathbb{R}^k$ (since the topological dimension of $\mathbb{R}^k$ is $k<d$). Therefore the injectivity of the spectral map $\sigma_k$ requires $d\leq k$. For the present constrained product sphere class, the moduli space dimension is $d=1$, so $k\geq 1$ suffices; this paper uses $k=3$ to provide a self-consistency check and spectral signature.

**Corollary 3.2** In the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ (with interlocking constants locked by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0), the moduli space dimension is $d = 1$. Therefore only $k \geq 1$ eigenvalues are needed to satisfy the necessary condition $d \leq k$; this paper uses $k = 3$ eigenvalues, which not only satisfies the necessary condition but also provides a self-consistency check and spectral signature.

For $\sigma_{12}$: $\theta_1\leftrightarrow \theta_2$, then $\mu_1\mapsto \theta_2-\theta_1=-\mu_1$, $\mu_2\mapsto \theta_1-\theta_3=\mu_1+\mu_2$. This transformation is a linear transformation on $\mathfrak{s}_3^*$, consistent with the adjoint representation of $S_3$. For $\sigma_{23}$: $\mu_1\mapsto \theta_1-\theta_3=\mu_1+\mu_2$, $\mu_2\mapsto \theta_3-\theta_2=-\mu_2$. Hence $\mu$ is an $S_3$-equivariant map. Marsden-Weinstein reduction: $\mu^{-1}(0)=\{\theta_0\}$ is a single point, on which $S_3$ acts trivially, so the quotient space is a single point. The symplectic form on a zero-dimensional symplectic manifold is zero, with symplectic volume conventionally set to 1.

**Remark 4.3 (Darboux Theorem and Area Element)** On the symplectic manifold $(D_\theta, \omega_\theta)$, by the classical Darboux theorem, there exist local coordinates $(\xi, \eta)$ such that $\omega_\theta = d\xi \wedge d\eta$. Since $D_\theta$ is two-dimensional, this representation holds globally. In the two-dimensional case, the symplectic form $\omega_\theta = d\xi \wedge d\eta$ is precisely the area element, providing orientation and area measurement on $D_\theta$.

### 4.5 Definition of Sector Projection Intensity Angles

**Definition 4.2 (Sector Projection Intensity Angles)** Let $P_{\mathcal{M}},P_{\mathcal{C}},P_{\mathcal{I}}:\mathbb{R}^3\to\Sigma$ be the linear projection maps from the three sectors onto the holographic screen $\Sigma$. Under $\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3$ satisfying Axiom 3, define the following area element proportionality relations:

1. **Single-sector projection area element ratio**:
   $$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}}(U_{\mathcal{M}}))=\sin^2\theta_1\cdot dA,\quad \text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{C}}(U_{\mathcal{C}}))=\sin^2\theta_2\cdot dA,\quad \text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{I}}(U_{\mathcal{I}}))=\sin^2\theta_3\cdot dA$$
   where $U_{\mathcal{M}},U_{\mathcal{C}},U_{\mathcal{I}}$ are respectively the standard two-dimensional subspaces of each $\mathbb{R}^3$ factor.

2. **Double-sector joint projection area element ratio**:
   $$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}}\times P_{\mathcal{C}})=\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2\cdot dA,\quad \text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}}\times P_{\mathcal{I}})=\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_3\cdot dA,\quad \text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{C}}\times P_{\mathcal{I}})=\sin\theta_2\sin\theta_3\cdot dA$$

**Remark 4.4 (Status as Intrinsic Coordinate Parameters and Open Problem)** The sector projection intensity angles $\theta_i$ are **intrinsic coordinate parameters** of the present construction, not derived from the standard Euclidean principal angles of $\mathbb{R}^9$ subspaces. In standard Euclidean geometry, the principal angles between a three-dimensional subspace and a two-dimensional plane under $S_3$ symmetry give approximately $35.26^\circ$ or $54.7^\circ$, which structurally deviate from $30^\circ$. The $\theta_i$ enter the area element ratios only through the combinations $\sin^2\theta_i$ and $\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j$, independent of the Euclidean angle system, and are candidates for the concrete representation of the abstract parameter $x$ of Chapter 2 in the geometry of the trifurcated tangent bundle.

**Open Problem 2 (Explicit Projection Construction)** The projection maps $P_{\mathcal{M}},P_{\mathcal{C}},P_{\mathcal{I}}$ in Definition 4.2 and their area element proportionality relations are **definitional constructions**, not rigorously derived from the linear algebraic structure of the trifurcated tangent bundle. Concretely, given $\mathbb{R}^9 = \mathcal{M}\oplus\mathcal{C}\oplus\mathcal{I}$ and a two-dimensional subspace $\Sigma$, does there exist a linear projection $P_i: \mathcal{M}\to\Sigma$ such that $\text{Area}(P_i(U_i)) = \sin^2\theta_i \cdot dA$ holds for given $\theta_i$? The existence and uniqueness of this construction have not yet been rigorously proven. At the $S_3$ symmetric point $\theta_i=30^\circ$, existence can be expected from symmetry arguments, but the explicit construction for non-symmetric points is an open problem.

### 4.6 Definition of the Six-Term Action

**Definition 4.3 (Six-Term Action)** Under Axiom 3's $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$, define the dimensionless quantity $S$ as the sum of the following six stiffness terms:

$$S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)=\sum_{i=1}^{3}\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i}+\sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j}$$

Written out:
$$S=\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_1}+\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_2}+\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_3}+\frac{1}{\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2}+\frac{1}{\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_3}+\frac{1}{\sin\theta_2\sin\theta_3}$$

**Explanation 4.3 (Algebraic Origin of the Six Terms)** The six terms in the six-term action do not come from a topological count of "independent two-dimensional cross-sections" on the holographic screen $\Sigma$, but from the following **algebraic construction**:

- 3 single-sector stiffness terms: corresponding to the reciprocal of the area element when each sector projects individually onto the holographic screen;
- 3 double-sector coupling terms: corresponding to the reciprocal of the area element when each pair of sectors projects jointly.

There is no triple-sector joint term — when three three-dimensional subspaces simultaneously project onto a two-dimensional holographic screen, the projection of the third dimension is eliminated by Axiom 3's $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$. Hence exactly six terms, neither more nor less. The total stiffness is defined as the sum of the six terms; this is a **definitional construction**, not dependent on topological counting.

### 4.7 Range Theorem and Strict Convexity

**Theorem 4.4 (Range Theorem and Strict Convexity)** Under Axiom 3's $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$, on the domain
$$D_\theta=\{(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)\mid\theta_i>0,\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ\},$$
the range of the six-term action $S$ is
$$S(D_\theta)=[24,+\infty).$$
Moreover, $S$ is **strictly convex** on $D_\theta$, and the symmetric point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$ is the unique global strict minimum point.
