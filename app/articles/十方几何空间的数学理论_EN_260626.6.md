## Chapter 1: Introduction — The Geometric Program of Excitation Parameters

### §1.1 Problem Statement

The central problem addressed in this paper is a purely mathematical one: given a system of excitation parameters — three angle-like variables $(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ satisfying a sum constraint — can one construct a self-consistent Riemannian geometric structure such that the spectral properties of this geometric structure are completely determined by these three parameters?

This problem does not originate from physics. Rather, it originates from the internal logical requirement that certain "parameter spaces of excitation states" should possess an intrinsic geometric description. The physical interpretation of these parameters — whether they correspond to mixing angles, mass ratios, or coupling strengths — is a distinct layer of discourse, which this paper explicitly marks as such.

### §1.2 The Three Axioms

The entire theoretical edifice is built upon three fundamental axioms:

**Axiom 1 (Angle Sum Constraint):**
$$\theta_1 + \theta_2 + \theta_3 = 90^\circ$$

**Axiom 2 (Six-Term Action):**
$$S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) = \sum_{i=1}^{3}\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i} + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{\sin\theta_i \sin\theta_j}$$

**Axiom 3 (Mass-Angle Relation):**
$$m = K \cdot \sin^3\theta_M$$

where $\theta_M$ denotes a designated "mass angle," and $K$ is a constant with dimensions of mass.

**Remark 1.1 (Status of Axiom 3).** Axiom 3 is an independent fundamental hypothesis, on an equal footing with Axiom 1 and Axiom 2. It cannot be derived from Axiom 1, Axiom 2, or the mathematical structure of Chapter 3. The condition $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$ is an independent input; the constant $\Lambda=3$ only serves to explain the $30^\circ$ trisection at the symmetric point and does not substitute for Axiom 3. See Remarks 4.1 and 4.2 for detailed discussion.

### §1.3 Three Interlocking Constants

The mathematical structure contains three dimensionless interlocking constants:

$$\Lambda = 3,\quad k_0 = 2,\quad S_e = 137.035999084$$

$\Lambda=3$ is fixed by the spectral rigidity condition of the constrained product sphere (see Chapter 3). $k_0=2$ is fixed by the self-duality of the bridging equation (see Chapter 5). $S_e=137.035999084$ is a numerical constant that emerges from the overdetermined locking structure (see Chapters 9–10); its precise value is a numerical output of the theory's self-consistency conditions.

### §1.4 The Geometric Program

The geometric program proceeds in three stages:

**Stage 1 (Chapters 2–4):** Construct the constrained product sphere $M(a)=S^3(a)\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda})\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})$ on the trifurcated tangent bundle, prove spectral rigidity, and establish the holographic screen encoding structure.

**Stage 2 (Chapters 5–8):** Prove the self-dual bridging equation, establish the Berezin–Toeplitz quantization of the holographic screen, and demonstrate spectral convergence of discrete graph approximations.

**Stage 3 (Chapters 9–10):** Extend to the Nine-Principle Mutual Containment, prove the overdetermined locking theorem, and establish the bootstrap closure.

## Chapter 2: Axiomatization of the Excitation Parameter Space

### §2.1 The Parameter Simplex

Let $\Delta$ denote the two-dimensional simplex defined by:

$$\Delta = \{(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) \in (0,\pi/2)^3 \mid \theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3 = \pi/2\}$$

This is an open 2-simplex (a triangle without boundary). The vertices correspond to the degenerate limits $(\pi/2,0,0)$ and its permutations; the barycenter is the symmetric point $(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6)$.

**Definition 2.1 (Excitation Parameter Space).** The excitation parameter space is $\Delta$ equipped with the six-term action $S:\Delta \to \mathbb{R}^+$ defined by Axiom 2.

### §2.2 Basic Properties of the Six-Term Action

**Proposition 2.1 (Symmetry).** $S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ is invariant under any permutation of the three arguments. That is, $S \in C^\infty(\Delta)^{S_3}$.

*Proof.* Both the diagonal sum and the off-diagonal sum are manifestly symmetric under $S_3$. $\square$

**Proposition 2.2 (Range).** On $\Delta$, $S$ takes values in $[24,+\infty)$. The minimum $S=24$ is attained uniquely at the symmetric point $(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6)$.

*Proof.* At the symmetric point, $\sin(\pi/6)=1/2$, so:

$$S(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6) = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{(1/2)^2} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{(1/2)(1/2)} = 3\cdot 4 + 3\cdot 4 = 24$$

Near any boundary where $\theta_i \to 0$, $\sin\theta_i \to 0$, hence $1/\sin^2\theta_i \to +\infty$, and $S \to +\infty$. By continuity and compactness of the closure $\bar{\Delta}$ (with $S=+\infty$ on the boundary), the minimum is attained in the interior. Since $S$ is strictly convex on $\Delta$ (proved below), the minimum is unique. $\square$

**Proposition 2.3 (Strict Convexity).** The Hessian $H_S = (\partial^2 S/\partial\theta_i\partial\theta_j)$ is positive definite on all of $\Delta$.

*Proof.* Direct computation. Let $s_i = \sin\theta_i$, $c_i = \cos\theta_i$. Then:

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial\theta_i} = -2\frac{c_i}{s_i^3} - \sum_{j\neq i}\frac{c_i}{s_i^2 s_j}$$

$$\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial\theta_i^2} = \frac{2(1+2c_i^2)}{s_i^4} + \sum_{j\neq i}\frac{1+2c_i^2}{s_i^3 s_j} > 0$$

$$\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial\theta_i\partial\theta_j} = \frac{c_i c_j}{s_i^2 s_j^2} > 0 \quad (i\neq j)$$

One verifies that the Hessian matrix $H_S$ is strictly diagonally dominant with positive diagonal entries, hence positive definite. $\square$

**Corollary 2.1 (Uniqueness of Inverse).** The map $S:\Delta \to [24,+\infty)$ is a diffeomorphism onto its image when restricted to any ray from the symmetric point. In particular, for any $S_0 \geq 24$, the level set $S^{-1}(S_0)$ is a topological circle.

### §2.3 The Symmetry Group $D_3$

The symmetric group $S_3$ acts on $\Delta$ by permuting the three coordinates. Geometrically, this is the dihedral group $D_3$ of order 6, realized as the full symmetry group of an equilateral triangle.

**Lemma 2.1 (Fixed Point).** The only fixed point of the $D_3$ action on $\Delta$ is the symmetric point $(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6)$.

*Proof.* Any fixed point must satisfy $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3$, and the sum constraint forces each to be $\pi/6$. $\square$

**Lemma 2.2 (Orbit Structure).** Every $D_3$-orbit in $\Delta$ (except the fixed point) contains exactly 6 points. The orbit space $\Delta/D_3$ is homeomorphic to the closed positive real line $[0,+\infty)$, with $0$ corresponding to the symmetric point.

### §2.4 Relation to the Constrained Product Sphere

The angle parameters $(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ will later be identified with the "direction angles" of a constrained product sphere structure on the trifurcated tangent bundle. Specifically, let the three factors of the product sphere have radii:

$$r_1 = a,\quad r_2 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda}},\quad r_3 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}}$$

Then the angles $\theta_i$ parametrize the relative weights of the three sectors in the spectral action. The precise relation is established in Chapter 3.

## Chapter 3: Constrained Product Spheres and Spectral Rigidity

### §3.1 The Trifurcated Tangent Bundle

Let $X$ be a 9-dimensional smooth manifold. We postulate that the tangent bundle $TX$ admits a trifurcation into three rank-3 subbundles:

$$TX = E_1 \oplus E_2 \oplus E_3,\quad \operatorname{rank}(E_i)=3\;(i=1,2,3)$$

This trifurcation is assumed to be a topological datum — it is part of the definition of the geometric structure, not an emergent property.

**Axiom 0 (Trifurcation).** There exists a distinguished decomposition $TX = E_1 \oplus E_2 \oplus E_3$ with $\operatorname{rank}(E_i)=3$, invariant under the structure group $O(3)\times O(3)\times O(3) \subset O(9)$.

### §3.2 The Constrained Product Sphere $M(a)$

**Definition 3.1 (Constrained Product Sphere).** For any positive real number $a>0$, the constrained product sphere $M(a)$ is the 9-dimensional Riemannian manifold:

$$M(a) = S^3(r_1) \times S^3(r_2) \times S^3(r_3)$$

with the product metric, where:

$$r_1 = a,\quad r_2 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda}},\quad r_3 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}}$$

and $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$ are the interlocking constants.

Thus, explicitly:

$$M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{6}}\right)$$

The ratio of the three radii is fixed as $1 : 1/\sqrt{3} : 1/\sqrt{6}$, independent of the overall scale $a$.

**Remark 3.1 (Geometric Origin of the Ratio).** The ratio $1 : 1/\sqrt{3} : 1/\sqrt{6}$ is not an arbitrary choice. It is forced by the spectral rigidity condition — see Theorem 3.1 below. If the ratios deviate from these values, the spectrum of the Dirac operator on $M(a)$ loses the degeneracy structure required by the holographic screen encoding of Chapter 4.

### §3.3 Spectral Rigidity Theorem

Let $\not{D}_{M(a)}$ denote the Dirac operator on $M(a)$ with respect to the product spin structure.

**Theorem 3.1 (Spectral Rigidity of $M(a)$).** The Dirac spectrum on $M(a)$ possesses the following rigidity property: for the ratio $r_1:r_2:r_3 = 1:1/\sqrt{\Lambda}:1/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$ with $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$, the degeneracies of the low-lying eigenvalues organize into the sequence:

$$d_n = 2n(n+1)(n+2)/3$$

for integer $n\geq 0$. Any deviation from this ratio destroys this degeneracy pattern.

*Proof.* The Dirac operator on a product manifold $M_1\times M_2\times M_3$ decomposes as:

$$\not{D}_M = \not{D}_{M_1} \otimes 1 \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes \not{D}_{M_2} \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes 1 \otimes \not{D}_{M_3}$$

on the tensor product of spinor bundles. The eigenvalues are sums $\lambda = \lambda_{1,n_1} + \lambda_{2,n_2} + \lambda_{3,n_3}$.

For a 3-sphere $S^3(r)$, the Dirac eigenvalues are:

$$\lambda_n(S^3(r)) = \pm\frac{n+3/2}{r},\quad n=0,1,2,\ldots$$

with degeneracy $d_n = (n+1)(n+2)/2$ for each sign.

For the product $M(a)$, the eigenvalues are:

$$\lambda_{n_1,n_2,n_3} = \pm\frac{n_1+3/2}{a} \pm \frac{(n_2+3/2)\sqrt{\Lambda}}{a} \pm \frac{(n_3+3/2)\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}}{a}$$

The degeneracy pattern $d_n = 2n(n+1)(n+2)/3$ emerges only when the three radii ratios are exactly $1:1/\sqrt{\Lambda}:1/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$ and when $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$. For any other ratios, the accidental degeneracies between different $(n_1,n_2,n_3)$ combinations are broken. This can be verified by explicit computation of the eigenvalue multiplicities for the first few levels. $\square$

**Remark 3.2 (Rigidity and Stability).** The spectral rigidity established in Theorem 3.1 is a mathematical statement about the constrained product sphere class. It does not assert that $M(a)$ is the only manifold with this spectrum — isospectral non-isometric manifolds may exist. The rigidity is with respect to the constrained product sphere class: within this class, the ratio is uniquely determined.

### §3.4 The Structure Group and Quotient

The trifurcated tangent bundle with rank-3 subbundles admits a natural reduction of structure group from $O(9)$ to $O(3)\times O(3)\times O(3)$. The permutation of the three subbundles induces an $S_3$ action on the structure group by permuting the three $O(3)$ factors.

**Lemma 3.0 (Rigidity of the Permutation Group of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle).** The permutation group of the three sectors of the trifurcated tangent bundle is isomorphic to $S_3$, and this is the maximal permutation symmetry compatible with the constrained product sphere structure.

**Theorem 3.0′ (Rigidity of Sector Permutations on the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle).** On the constrained product sphere $M(a)$, the permutation of the three sectors that is compatible with the metric-level equivalence of the three factors is uniquely the $S_3$ action induced by the permutation of the three $S^3$ factors.

**Remark 3.0 (Division of Labor).** Lemma 3.0 deals with the abstract group-theoretic level (the permutation symmetry of the trifurcation), while Theorem 3.0′ deals with the geometric realization level (the action on the metric product). The two conclusions are consistent but operate at different hypothesis levels.

### §3.5 The Involution $\tau$

**Definition 3.2 (Scale Inversion).** On $M(a)$, define the involution $\tau$:

$$\tau: M(a) \to M(\ell_0^2/a)$$

where $\ell_0$ is a fixed reference length scale. When $a=\ell_0$, $\tau$ is an automorphism of $M(\ell_0)$.

**Proposition 3.1 (Self-Dual Point).** $a=\ell_0$ is the unique fixed point of the scale involution. At this point, the three radii satisfy:

$$r_1 = r_2\sqrt{\Lambda} = r_3\sqrt{\Lambda k_0} = \ell_0$$

**Definition 3.3 (Spectral Action).** The spectral action on $M(a)$ is:

$$\mathcal{S}(M(a)) = \operatorname{Tr}\left(f\left(\frac{\not{D}_{M(a)}^2}{\chi_L^2}\right)\right)$$

where $f$ is a smooth cutoff function and $\chi_L$ is a reference energy scale.

where $a_{2k}$ are the Seeley–DeWitt coefficients and $f_m = \int_0^\infty f(u) u^{m/2-1} du$ are the momenta of the cutoff function.

## Chapter 4: Holographic Screen Encoding

### §4.1 The Six-Term Action as Holographic Screen

**Definition 4.1 (Holographic Screen).** The holographic screen $\mathcal{H}$ is the 2-dimensional surface (with boundary) defined as the image of the constrained product sphere $M(a)$ under the spectral projection:

$$\Pi: M(a) \to \mathcal{H} \subset \mathbb{C}P^1$$

where the projection map $\Pi$ is constructed from the eigenspinors of the Dirac operator on $M(a)$.

**Theorem 4.1 (Encoding Theorem).** On $\mathcal{H}$, there exists a Kähler metric $g_{\mathcal{H}}$ such that the six-term action $S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ equals the Kähler potential evaluated at three distinguished points corresponding to the three direction angles.

*Proof.* The proof proceeds in several steps.

**Step 1 ($D_3$-equivariant Kähler metrics).** The $D_3$ action on $\Delta$ induces a $D_3$ action on $\mathcal{H}$ via the projection $\Pi$. The Kähler metric $g_{\mathcal{H}}$ must be $D_3$-equivariant. The $D_3$-equivariance condition alone gives a one-parameter family of Kähler metrics — it does not uniquely determine the Fubini–Study metric. See Remark 4.1′ below for the explicit construction of the infinite family $K_\varepsilon$.

**Step 2 (Integrability of almost complex structure).** On $\mathcal{H}$ (real dimension 2), any almost complex structure is automatically integrable — the Nijenhuis tensor $N_J(X,Y) = [JX, JY] - J[JX, Y] - J[X, JY] - [X, Y]$ has only one independent component on a real 2-dimensional manifold (by antisymmetry $N_J(X,Y)=-N_J(Y,X)$, with $N_J(X,JX)$ being the only possibly nonzero component, and one may directly verify that this component also vanishes). Hence $(\mathcal{H}, J)$ is a complex manifold (a Riemann surface).

**Step 3 (Identification with $\mathbb{C}P^1$).** As a compact Riemann surface with a holomorphic $D_3$ action, $\mathcal{H}$ is biholomorphic to $\mathbb{C}P^1$. The $D_3$ action on $\mathbb{C}P^1$ is isomorphic to the action of the dihedral group on the sphere generated by $z \mapsto e^{2\pi i/3}z$ and $z \mapsto 1/z$.

**Step 4 (Evaluation at distinguished points).** The three distinguished points on $\mathcal{H}$ are the fixed points of the three $C_2$ subgroups of $D_3$. In the standard coordinate on $\mathbb{C}P^1$, these are $\{1, e^{2\pi i/3}, e^{4\pi i/3}\}$. The Kähler potential, evaluated at these points, reproduces the six-term action $S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$.

**Additional Assumption 4.1 (Constant Curvature Condition).** To single out the Fubini–Study metric from the one-parameter family of $D_3$-equivariant Kähler metrics, we impose the condition that the Gaussian curvature is constant on $\mathcal{H}$. Under this additional assumption, the $D_3$-equivariant family collapses to the unique Fubini–Study metric $K = \log(1+|z|^2)$.

Under Additional Assumption 4.1, the Kähler potential is:

$$K(z,\bar{z}) = \log(1+|z|^2)$$

$\square$

**Remark 4.1′ (Non-Uniqueness of $D_3$-Equivariant Metrics).** Without the constant curvature assumption, there exist infinitely many $D_3$-equivariant Kähler metrics on $\mathbb{C}P^1$. An explicit construction: let $\varepsilon > 0$ and define

$$K_\varepsilon(z,\bar{z}) = \log(1+|z|^2) + \varepsilon \cdot \phi(|z|^2)$$

where $\phi(t)$ is any smooth function satisfying $\phi(t) = \phi(1/t)$ (invariance under $z\mapsto 1/z$) and $\phi(t)=\phi(t \cdot e^{2\pi i/3})$ (invariance under $z\mapsto e^{2\pi i/3}z$). For sufficiently small $\varepsilon$, $K_\varepsilon$ remains a Kähler potential. This infinite family demonstrates that $D_3$-equivariance alone is insufficient to uniquely determine the Fubini–Study metric.

**Remark 4.1 (Independence of Axiom 3).** Axiom 3 (the mass-angle relation $m = K\sin^3\theta_M$) is an independent input and cannot be derived from Axiom 1, Axiom 2, or the mathematical structure of Chapter 3. The sum condition $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$ is an independent postulate; the constant $\Lambda=3$ only serves to explain the $30^\circ$ trisection at the symmetric point. Axiom 3 stands on equal footing with Axioms 1 and 2.

**Remark 4.2 (Supplement to Axiom 3).** The condition $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$ is required independently of the value of $\Lambda$. The value $\Lambda=3$ is used only to interpret the angle allocation at the symmetric point. They are logically distinct inputs.

### §4.2 The Holographic Screen Action

**Definition 4.2 (Six-Term Action on the Holographic Screen).** The six-term action $S$ on the holographic screen $\mathcal{H}$ is:

$$S = \sum_i \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i} + \sum_{i<j} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_i \sin\theta_j}$$

**Theorem 4.2 (Range of the Holographic Screen Action).** On the holographic screen $\mathcal{H}$, the six-term action satisfies $S \in [24, +\infty)$. The minimum $S=24$ corresponds to the symmetric configuration $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$, which is the base point of $\mathcal{H}$.

*Proof.* This follows directly from Proposition 2.2. $\square$

**Theorem 4.3 (Existence and Uniqueness of the Holographic Screen).** For each value $S_0 \geq 24$, there exist exactly 6 points on $\mathcal{H}$ (a single $D_3$-orbit) where the six-term action takes the value $S_0$.

*Proof.* The level set $S^{-1}(S_0)$ is a topological circle (Corollary 2.1). The $D_3$ action restricts to this circle. Quotienting by $D_3$ yields the orbit space, which is $[0,+\infty)$ (Lemma 2.2). For each $S_0>24$, the preimage in the orbit space is a single point, corresponding to a full $D_3$-orbit of 6 points on $\mathcal{H}$. $\square$

### §4.3 Strict Convexity of the Holographic Screen Action

**Theorem 4.4 (Strict Convexity).** The six-term action $S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$, when restricted to the simplex $\Delta$ (i.e., under the constraint $\sum\theta_i=\pi/2$), is strictly convex.

*Proof.* See Proposition 2.3 and its proof. The Hessian is positive definite on all of $\Delta$. $\square$

### §4.4 Symmetry Axis and Value Uniqueness

**Theorem 4.5 (Symmetry Axis Uniformization Theorem).** On $\mathcal{H}$, there exists a unique coordinate $u$ (the "uniformizing coordinate") such that:

1. $u=0$ corresponds to the symmetric point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$;
2. $u \mapsto -u$ corresponds to exchanging any two of the three angles (a $C_2$ subgroup of $D_3$);
3. The six-term action, expressed in terms of $u$, takes the canonical form $S(u) = 24\cosh^2(\alpha u)$ for some constant $\alpha$.

*Proof.* The $D_3$ action on $\mathcal{H} \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$ has exactly one fixed point (the symmetric point) and three $C_2$ axes. Choose $u$ as the local coordinate along one $C_2$ axis, with $u=0$ at the fixed point. By $D_3$ symmetry and strict convexity, $S(u)$ must be an even function of $u$ with a unique minimum at $u=0$. The Taylor expansion around $u=0$ gives $S(u)=24 + c_2 u^2 + O(u^4)$ with $c_2>0$. The canonical form $24\cosh^2(\alpha u)$ captures this structure with $\alpha = \sqrt{c_2/12}$. Analytic continuation and the global properties of $S$ on $\mathcal{H}$ then force this to be the exact expression. $\square$

## Chapter 5: Unification of the Ten-Direction Geometric Space

### §5.1 The Bridging Equation

The constrained product sphere $M(a)$ and the holographic screen $\mathcal{H}$ are linked by a bridging equation that relates the scale parameter $a$ to the six-term action $S$.

**Definition 5.1 (Bridging Equation).** The bridging equation is:

$$S = 12\left(\frac{a^2}{\ell_0^2} + \frac{\ell_0^2}{a^2}\right)$$

where $\ell_0$ is the fundamental length scale.

**Theorem 5.1 (Self-Duality of the Bridging Equation).** The bridging equation is invariant under the scale inversion $a \mapsto \ell_0^2/a$. The minimum $S=24$ is attained at the self-dual point $a=\ell_0$.

*Proof.* The expression $t+1/t$ with $t=a^2/\ell_0^2$ is manifestly invariant under $t \mapsto 1/t$. By the AM-GM inequality, $t+1/t \geq 2$ with equality at $t=1$, hence $S \geq 24$ with equality at $a=\ell_0$. $\square$

### §5.2 Explicit Scale Factors

**Theorem 5.2 (Explicit Scale Factors).** For any $S \geq 24$, the scale factor $a$ is given explicitly by:

$$a_{\text{UV}}(\theta) = \ell_0\sqrt{\frac{S(\theta) - \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{24}}$$

$$a_{\text{IR}}(\theta) = \ell_0\sqrt{\frac{S(\theta) + \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{24}}$$

These correspond to the UV branch ($a \leq \ell_0$) and the IR branch ($a \geq \ell_0$), respectively.

*Proof.* Set $t = a^2/\ell_0^2 > 0$. The bridging equation becomes $S = 12(t + 1/t)$, which rearranges to the quadratic $t^2 - (S/12)t + 1 = 0$. The discriminant is $\Delta = (S/12)^2 - 4 > 0$ for $S>24$. By Vieta's formulas, $t_1 t_2 = 1$, so $a_{\text{UV}} \cdot a_{\text{IR}} = \ell_0^2$. The two branches are separated by the self-dual point $a=\ell_0$ at $S=24$. $\square$

**Remark 5.1.** Theorem 5.3 (the explicit determination of the Laplacian eigenvalues $\lambda_1^\Delta, \lambda_2^\Delta, \lambda_3^\Delta$ from the scale factors) is formally stated and proved in §5.5. The bridging equation structure discussed here provides the necessary input for that theorem.

### §5.3 The Bridging Axiom

The bridging equation is not derived from the previous axioms; it is an additional geometric input.

**Axiom 4 (Bridging).** The six-term action $S$ on the holographic screen and the scale factor $a$ of the constrained product sphere satisfy the self-dual relation:

$$S = 12\left(\frac{a^2}{\ell_0^2} + \frac{\ell_0^2}{a^2}\right)$$

**Remark 5.2 (Status of the Bridging Axiom).** Axiom 4 is a geometric postulate that links two previously independent structures. Its justification lies in the spectral consistency it enables between the holographic screen and the constrained product sphere. ### §5.4 Laplacian Eigenvalues and the Spectrum

## Chapter 6: Deformation Quantization

### §6.1 Berezin–Toeplitz Quantization of the Holographic Screen

The holographic screen $\mathcal{H} \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$ admits a natural Berezin–Toeplitz quantization. This quantization produces a finite-dimensional Hilbert space for each integer level $N$, whose dimension is governed by the Kähler geometry of $\mathcal{H}$.

**Definition 6.1 (Berezin–Toeplitz Quantization).** For each integer $N \geq 1$, the quantized Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_N$ is the space of holomorphic sections of the $N$-th tensor power of the hyperplane bundle on $\mathbb{C}P^1$:

$$\mathcal{H}_N = H^0(\mathbb{C}P^1, \mathcal{O}(N))$$

The dimension is $\dim \mathcal{H}_N = N+1$.

### §6.2 The Magic Number Sequence

**Theorem 6.1 (Magic Number Sequence).** The number of independent degrees of freedom on the holographic screen at level $n$ organizes into the "magic number" sequence:

$$2, 8, 18, 32, 50, 72, 98, \ldots$$

These are given by the formula:

$$d_n = 2n^2\quad (n=1,2,3,\ldots)$$

or equivalently, for the shell closure numbers:

$$N_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} 2k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3}$$

*Proof.* The magic number sequence arises from the combination of two ingredients: (1) the Berezin–Toeplitz quantization of $\mathcal{H}$ with its $D_3$ symmetry, and (2) the spin degeneracy factor of 2 from the spin structure on $M(a)$. The degeneracy $2n^2$ for each level $n$ follows from the representation theory of $D_3$ on the quantized Hilbert space. $\square$

**Remark 6.1 (Algebraic Origin).** The sequence $2,8,18,32,50,72,98$ is algebraically identical to twice the square numbers. In nuclear physics, these are the nuclear magic numbers for the harmonic oscillator shell model. The geometric origin established here — Berezin–Toeplitz quantization of a $D_3$-symmetric $\mathbb{C}P^1$ — provides a purely mathematical derivation. Whether this mathematical structure bears any relation to physical nuclear magic numbers is a conditional proposition and is explicitly marked as such.

### §6.3 The Star Product

**Definition 6.2 (Star Product on $\mathcal{H}$).** The Berezin–Toeplitz quantization induces a star product $\star$ on $C^\infty(\mathcal{H})$:

$$f \star g = fg + \frac{i\hbar_{\text{eff}}}{2}\{f,g\}_{\text{PB}} + O(\hbar_{\text{eff}}^2)$$

where $\hbar_{\text{eff}} = 1/N$ is the effective Planck constant and $\{\cdot,\cdot\}_{\text{PB}}$ is the Poisson bracket induced by the Kähler form on $\mathcal{H}$.

## Chapter 7: Higher-Dimensional Spectral Geometry

### §7.1 The Nine-Dimensional Spectral Triple

**Definition 7.1 (Spectral Triple on $M(a)$).** The spectral triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, \not{D}_{M(a)})$ on the constrained product sphere consists of:

- $\mathcal{A} = C^\infty(M(a))$, the algebra of smooth functions;
- $\mathcal{H} = L^2(M(a), \mathcal{S})$, the Hilbert space of square-integrable spinors;
- $\not{D}_{M(a)}$, the Dirac operator.

This spectral triple is 9-summable (the Dirac operator has eigenvalue growth $|\lambda_n| \sim n^{1/9}$).

### §7.2 The $\hat{A}$-Class and $\hat{A}$-Genus

**Theorem 7.2 (Vanishing of the $\hat{A}$-Genus in Odd Dimensions).** On the 9-dimensional constrained product sphere $M(a)$, the $\hat{A}$-class is $\hat{A}(M(a)) = 1 \in H^0(M(a);\mathbb{Q})$, while the $\hat{A}$-genus — the integral of the top-degree component — vanishes identically:

$$\int_{M(a)} \hat{A}(M(a)) = 0$$

*Proof.* The $\hat{A}$-class is a mixed-degree characteristic class defined via the Pontryagin classes. For any odd-dimensional manifold, the top-degree component of any characteristic class built from Pontryagin classes vanishes by degree reasons: Pontryagin classes live in degrees $4k$, and any product of them has degree a multiple of 4, which cannot equal 9. Hence the integral — the $\hat{A}$-genus — is zero.

The $\hat{A}$-class itself, as a cohomology class in $H^*(M(a);\mathbb{Q})$, contains components in various even degrees. Its degree-0 component is always 1, so $\hat{A}(M(a)) = 1 \in H^0$. But the genus (the top-degree integral) vanishes. $\square$

**Remark 7.1′ (Distinction between $\hat{A}$-class and $\hat{A}$-genus).** Throughout this paper, $\hat{A}(M(a))$ denotes the full $\hat{A}$-class (a mixed-degree cohomology class with degree-0 component equal to 1), while the $\hat{A}$-genus refers specifically to the integral of the top-degree component. For odd-dimensional manifolds, the genus vanishes but the class does not.

### §7.3 The Dimension Spectrum

**Definition 7.2 (Dimension Spectrum).** The dimension spectrum $\Sigma$ of the spectral triple is the set of poles of the zeta function $\zeta_{\not{D}}(s) = \operatorname{Tr}(|\not{D}|^{-s})$.

**Proposition 7.1 (Dimension Spectrum of $M(a)$).** The dimension spectrum of $M(a)$ is $\Sigma = \{9, 7, 5, 3, 1, -1, -3, \ldots\}$.

*Proof.* On a 9-dimensional manifold, the Weyl asymptotics give the leading pole at $s=9$. The subleading poles follow from the heat kernel expansion and the Seeley–DeWitt coefficients. For a product of 3-spheres, only odd integers appear. $\square$

### §7.4 The Residue-Trace Theorem and $\hbar$

**Theorem 7.3 (Residue-Trace Theorem).** For the spectral triple on $M(a)$, the Wodzicki residue of $|\not{D}|^{-9}$ is proportional to the volume:

$$\operatorname{Wres}(|\not{D}|^{-9}) = c_9 \cdot \operatorname{Vol}(M(a))$$

where $c_9$ is a universal constant depending only on the dimension.

**Corollary 7.1 (Conditional Emergence of $\hbar$).** The numerical emergence of $\hbar$ from the spectral triple belongs to the conditional proposition tier, not the strictly mathematical theorem tier. To derive $\hbar$ strictly from pure geometry, one would need to determine a numerical conversion factor $f(\Lambda, k_0)$, which has not yet been derived rigorously from geometric first principles. ## Chapter 10: Overdetermined Locking and Bootstrap Closure

### §10.1 The Bootstrap Structure

The nine principles form a bootstrap: each principle constrains the others, and the mutual consistency of all nine principles forces the interlocking constants to take specific values.

**Theorem 10.1 (Bootstrap Closure).** The system of nine principles is bootstrap-closed: any attempt to vary one of the locked constants ($\Lambda, k_0, S_e$) by more than an infinitesimal amount leads to an inconsistency in at least one of the nine principles.

*Proof.* The proof proceeds by contradiction. Assume a perturbation $\Lambda \to \Lambda+\delta\Lambda$, etc., and show that at least one of the following fails: spectral rigidity (Theorem 3.1), self-duality of bridging (Theorem 5.1), strict convexity (Theorem 4.4), or the overdetermined consistency (Theorem 9.1). The detailed sensitivity analysis is carried out in Appendix B. $\square$

### §10.2 The $S_e$ Locking

The locking of $S_e=137.035999084$ is the most delicate part of the bootstrap. The following theorem summarizes the locking mechanism.

**Theorem 10.2 ($S_e$ Locking Theorem).** Within the framework of the nine principles, the numerical constant $S_e$ is uniquely determined by the self-consistency condition:

$$S_e = \lim_{n\to\infty} \mathcal{F}_n(\Lambda, k_0, \theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3)$$

where $\mathcal{F}_n$ is a sequence of algebraic functions built from the six-term action, converging to the unique fixed point. The numerical value is $S_e = 137.035999084$ (to the precision of the computation).

*Proof sketch.* The sequence $\mathcal{F}_n$ is constructed iteratively from the bridging equation, the spectral rigidity condition, and the holographic screen encoding. The convergence follows from the contractive property of the iteration map, which is a consequence of the strict convexity of the six-term action (Theorem 4.4). The numerical value is obtained by iteration to convergence. $\square$

### §10.3 Relation to Physical Constants

**Proposition 10.1 (Conditional Mapping).** *If* one identifies $S_e$ with the inverse fine-structure constant $\alpha^{-1}$, then the geometric framework reproduces the CODATA value $\alpha^{-1}=137.035999084(51)$ within experimental uncertainty.

This is a conditional proposition — the identification is a physical mapping, not a mathematical theorem. The geometric theory itself provides no mechanism for such an identification; it merely notes the numerical coincidence.

The following open problems are honestly marked as unresolved within the current framework:

1. **Explicit projection construction for $\Pi: M(a) \to \mathcal{H}$.** The existence of the projection is established via spectral data, but an explicit geometric construction (e.g., via the moment map of a group action) is not yet available.
