## Chapter 2: Axiomatization of the Excitation Parameter Space

### ¬ß2.1 Axiom 1 (Topology of the Parameter Space)

**Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** The excitation parameter space $D$ is obtained from the one-dimensional compact connected manifold $S^1$ (the circle) by removing two distinct points $p_0$ and $p_*$:

$$D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}, \qquad p_0 \neq p_*$$

The two removed points are respectively called the **vacuum point** ($p_0$) and the **degenerate point** ($p_*$). Consequently, $D$ consists of two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1), \qquad D_- \cong (0,1)$$

Each component is homeomorphic to an open interval. The parameter $x \in D_\pm$ labels the excitation state.

**Lemma 2.1 (Topological Structure of the Components)** Let $S^1$ be the standard unit circle $\{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi \in [0,2\pi)\}$. Removing two points $p_0=e^{i\phi_0}$ and $p_*=e^{i\phi_*}$ (without loss of generality, $0\leq \phi_0 < \phi_* < 2\pi$), $S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}$ has exactly two connected components:
$$D_+ = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_0 < \phi < \phi_*\}, \quad D_- = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_* < \phi < \phi_0+2\pi\}.$$
Each component, as a subspace of $S^1$, is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0,1)$.

*Proof.* Consider the standard covering map $\pi: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $\pi(\phi)=e^{i\phi}$. On $\mathbb{R}$, $\pi^{-1}(S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}) = \mathbb{R}\setminus\{\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi \mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}$. Each connected component of this set is an open interval $(\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi)$ or $(\phi_*+2k\pi, \phi_0+2(k+1)\pi)$. Since the restriction of $\pi$ to an interval of length $2\pi$ is a homeomorphism, the images of these open intervals in $S^1$ are precisely $D_+$ and $D_-$. Each open interval is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, hence $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$. $\square$

### ¬ß2.2 Axiom 2 (Analytic Properties of the Geometric Quantity)

**Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** On the parameter space $D$ there exists a geometric quantity $S: D \to (0,+\infty)$ satisfying:

1. **Continuity:** $S$ is continuous on each connected component $D_\pm$;
2. **Vacuum Limit:** As $x$ approaches $p_0$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$;
3. **Degenerate Limit:** As $x$ approaches $p_*$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$.

**Remark 2.1** Axiom 2 does not require $S$ to have the same functional expression on $D_+$ and $D_-$, nor does it require $S$ to be continuous on the entirety of $D$ ($p_0$ and $p_*$ are not in $D$). Thus $S$ may be different functions on the two independent components, provided each satisfies the boundary conditions.

### ¬ß2.3 Range Theorem

**Theorem 2.1 (Range)** Under Axioms 1 and 2, the range of $S$ on each connected component $D_\pm$ is $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$.

*Proof.* By Axiom 1, $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is connected. By Axiom 2, $S$ is continuous on $D_\pm$, hence the image $S(D_\pm)$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e., an interval. By the vacuum limit, $\inf S(D_\pm) = 0$; by the degenerate limit, $\sup S(D_\pm) = +\infty$. Since $p_0, p_* \notin D$, the endpoints $0$ and $+\infty$ are not attained. Therefore $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed Expansion: Connectedness Argument.** The connectedness argument can be further unfolded as follows. Let $D_+ \cong (0,1)$ and $S: D_+ \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Suppose $S(D_+)$ is not an interval; then there exist $\alpha < \beta < \gamma$ such that $\alpha, \gamma \in S(D_+)$ but $\beta \notin S(D_+)$. Set $U = S^{-1}((-\infty, \beta))$ and $V = S^{-1}((\beta, +\infty))$; then $U,V$ are disjoint open sets in $D_+$ and $U\cup V = D_+$, contradicting the connectedness of $D_+$. Hence $S(D_+)$ must be an interval. Combined with the boundary limits, that interval must be $(0,+\infty)$.

### ¬ß2.4 Existence Theorem and Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis

**Theorem 2.2 (Existence)** For any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, there exists at least one excitation state on each component $D_+$ and $D_-$, namely $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$, such that $S(x_+) = S(x_-) = S_0$.

*Proof.* By Theorem 2.1, $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. For any $S_0 > 0$, $S_0 \in (0,+\infty) = S(D_\pm)$, hence there exists $x_\pm \in D_\pm$ such that $S(x_\pm) = S_0$. $\square$

**Corollary 2.1 (Strict Monotonicity and Uniqueness ‚Äî Hypothesis)** If we further assume that $S$ is **strictly monotonic** on each component $D_\pm$, then for each $S_0 > 0$, there exists a **unique** excitation state $x_+(S_0)$ on $D_+$ and $x_-(S_0)$ on $D_-$, and $x_- \neq x_+$ holds for all $S_0$.

*Remark 2.2* Strict monotonicity is not forced by Axioms 1‚Äì2 ‚Äî $S$ could oscillate on $(0,1)$ while still satisfying $S\to 0$ (approaching $0$) and $S\to+\infty$ (approaching $1$). Strict monotonicity is an **additional hypothesis** introduced in this paper, whose geometric inevitability is rigorously proved on $D_\theta$ by the strict positive-definiteness of the Hessian in Theorem 4.4 (¬ß4.7), and the bridging axiom of Chapters 3 and 5 transfers this monotonicity from $D_\theta$ back to $D_\pm$.

## Chapter 3: Spectral Geometry of Constrained Product Sphere Classes

### ¬ß3.1 Trifurcated Tangent Bundle Structure and Constrained Product Spheres

**Definition 3.1 (Trifurcated Tangent Bundle)** Let $M(a)$ be a nine-dimensional compact smooth Riemannian manifold, whose tangent bundle $TM(a)$ decomposes as the Whitney sum of three rank-3 subbundles:

$$TM(a) = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$$

The three sectors are named:
- $\mathcal{M}$ (Material sector)
- $\mathcal{C}$ (Causal sector)
- $\mathcal{I}$ (Information sector)

Each sector is equipped with a Riemannian metric, and the three sectors are mutually orthogonal.

**Definition 3.2 (Constrained Product Sphere)** Let $S^3(R)$ be the three-dimensional sphere of radius $R$, equipped with the standard round metric. Define the constrained product sphere

$$M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda}) \times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})$$

where $a>0$ is the reference scale and $\Lambda>0$ and $k_0>0$ are interlocking constants (their precise values are locked group-theoretically by Proposition 3.0 as $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$). The curvature radii of the three factor spheres are:

$$R_1 = a,\qquad R_2 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda}},\qquad R_3 = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}}$$

with corresponding scalar curvatures $6/a^2$, $6\Lambda/a^2$, $6\Lambda k_0/a^2$.

### ¬ß3.2 Permutation Symmetry of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle and Group-Theoretic Rigidity

**Lemma 3.0 (Rigidity of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle Permutation Group)** Let $G$ be a permutation group on $\{1,2,3\}$ satisfying: for every $g\in G$, the sector permutation induced by $g$ is orientation-preserving at the topological level. Then $G$ must be isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_3$. If we further require $G$ to be non-trivial and to contain at least one transposition and at least one 3-cycle, then $G\cong S_3$.

**Theorem 3.0‚Ä≤ (Rigidity of Trifurcated Tangent Bundle Sector Permutations)** If the three sectors $\mathcal{M},\mathcal{C},\mathcal{I}$ are required to be **fully equivalent at the metric level** ‚Äî i.e., any sector permutation induces an isometry of $M(a)$ ‚Äî then the sector permutation group must contain all transpositions and all 3-cycles, and thus is uniquely isomorphic to the symmetric group $S_3$.

**Remark 3.0 (Division of Labor between Lemma 3.0 and Theorem 3.0‚Ä≤)** Lemma 3.0 treats the abstract group-theoretic level: from basic properties of permutation groups one obtains $G\cong S_3$. Theorem 3.0‚Ä≤ treats the geometric realization level: under the stronger hypothesis of "full metric equivalence," one obtains the concrete isometric realization of $S_3$ acting on $M(a)$. **Proposition 3.0 (Group-Theoretic Locking of Interlocking Constants)** The number of conjugacy classes of $S_3$ is $\Lambda(S_3)=|\text{Conj}(S_3)|=3$, and its maximal normal subgroup is $A_3$ (index $[S_3:A_3]=2$), hence $k_0(S_3)=2$.

*Proof.* The conjugacy classes of $S_3$ are: $\{e\}$ (identity), $\{(12),(13),(23)\}$ (transpositions), $\{(123),(132)\}$ (3-cycles), totaling 3 classes. The unique non-trivial normal subgroup of $S_3$ is $A_3=\{(123),(132),e\}$ (index $6/3=2$). $A_3$ is the maximal normal subgroup of $S_3$ (the unique index-2 subgroup), hence $k_0(S_3)=[S_3:A_3]=2$. $\square$

### ¬ß3.3 Laplace Operator and Spectral Invariants

Let $\Delta_{M(a)}$ be the Laplace‚ÄìBeltrami operator on $M(a)$ (positive-definite convention, $\Delta=-g^{ij}\nabla_i\nabla_j$). Eigenvalues are arranged in increasing order: $0=\lambda_0^\Delta < \lambda_1^\Delta \leq \lambda_2^\Delta \leq \cdots$.

**Proposition 3.1 (Laplace Spectrum of Product Manifolds)** For the product manifold $M(a)=S^3(R_1)\times S^3(R_2)\times S^3(R_3)$, its Laplace eigenvalues are the sums of the eigenvalues of the factors:

$$\lambda_{(k_1,k_2,k_3)}^\Delta = \lambda_{k_1}^{S^3(R_1)} + \lambda_{k_2}^{S^3(R_2)} + \lambda_{k_3}^{S^3(R_3)}$$

where $k_i=0,1,2,\dots$ are the spherical harmonic orders on $S^3(R_i)$, and $\lambda_k^{S^3(R)} = k(k+2)/R^2$.

**Proposition 3.2 (Ordering of the First Three Nonzero Eigenvalues)** Under the locking $\Lambda>1$ and $k_0>0$, the first three nonzero Laplace eigenvalues of $M(a)$ are strictly ordered as:

$$\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{3}{a^2} < \lambda_2^\Delta = \frac{8}{a^2} < \lambda_3^\Delta = \frac{3\Lambda}{a^2}$$

The corresponding harmonic modes are respectively the $k=1$ excitation of $S^3(a)$, the $k=2$ excitation of $S^3(a)$, and the $k=1$ excitation of $S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda})$.

*Proof.* From the product manifold Laplace spectrum formula, the first few nonzero eigenvalues come from low-order harmonic combinations of the three factor spheres. For $S^3(R)$, the $k=1$ eigenvalue is $3/R^2$ and the $k=2$ eigenvalue is $8/R^2$. For $M(a)$:
- $(k_1,k_2,k_3)=(1,0,0)$: $\lambda=3/a^2$ (excitation on $S^3(a)$)
- $(k_1,k_2,k_3)=(2,0,0)$: $\lambda=8/a^2$
- $(k_1,k_2,k_3)=(0,1,0)$: $\lambda=3\Lambda/a^2$

Since $\Lambda>1$, $3\Lambda/a^2 > 3/a^2$. If $\Lambda > 8/3$, then $3\Lambda/a^2 > 8/a^2$, and the three are strictly separated. This condition is satisfied when $\Lambda=3$. All other modes are at least as large as these three (e.g., $(0,0,1)$ yields $3\Lambda k_0/a^2$, and the ordering must be ensured in conjunction with the value of $k_0$). $\square$

**Theorem 3.1 (Moduli Space Parameterization)** Under the locking of the interlocking constants $(\Lambda, k_0)$, the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_{(\Lambda,k_0)}$ of isometry isomorphism classes of the constrained product sphere family is completely parameterized by the single scale factor $a\in(0,+\infty)$:

$$\mathcal{M}_{(\Lambda,k_0)} \cong (0,+\infty)_a$$

*Proof.* Given $(\Lambda,k_0)$, the three factor radii are uniquely determined as $(a, a/\sqrt{\Lambda}, a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})$. Any two distinct values of $a$ give distinct scalar curvatures and hence are not isometric. Therefore the moduli space is parameterized one-to-one by $a$. $\square$

*Proof.* If $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric, then their scalar curvatures coincide: $\text{Scal}_{M(a)} = \text{Scal}_{M(a')}$. From the scalar curvature formula $\text{Scal}_{M(a)} = 6/a^2 + 6\Lambda/a^2 + 6\Lambda k_0/a^2$, we obtain $a=a'$. $\square$

$$\frac{1}{\sin^2 30^\circ}=4,\quad\frac{1}{\sin 30^\circ\sin 30^\circ}=4$$

$$S(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)=12+12=24$$

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3,\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i} + \sum_{i<j} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j} - \lambda\left(\sum_{i=1}^3 \theta_i - \frac{\pi}{2}\right).$$

### ¬ß7.2 Dirac Index Theorem

**Theorem 7.2 (The $\hat{A}$-Class and Vanishing of the $\hat{A}$-Genus of $M(a)$).** $M(a)=S^3\times S^3\times S^3$ is a nine-dimensional closed manifold. Its $\hat{A}$-class satisfies:

$$\hat{A}(M(a)) = 1 \in H^0(M(a)).$$

$$\int_{M(a)}\hat{A}(M(a))=0.\tag{7.2}$$

*Proof.* For the odd-dimensional sphere $S^3$, Pontryagin classes $p_i\in H^{4i}(M)$; if $4i>3$ then $p_i=0$. Hence $\hat{A}(S^3)=1\in H^0(S^3)$. By the product formula $\hat{A}(M_1\times M_2)=\hat{A}(M_1)\wedge\hat{A}(M_2)$, we obtain $\hat{A}(M(a))=1\wedge 1\wedge 1=1\in H^0(M(a))$.

On a nine-dimensional manifold, no integer $k$ satisfies $4k=9$, so $\hat{A}$ has no degree-9 component. The integral $\int_M$ is defined in characteristic class theory as the pairing of the top-degree component with the fundamental class $[M]\in H_d(M)$; since the highest non-vanishing component of $\hat{A}(M(a))$ is at most degree 8 (here actually only the degree-0 component is non-vanishing), there is no degree-9 component to pair with the 9-dimensional fundamental class, and the $\hat{A}$-genus is zero by definition. This is a classical result: the $\hat{A}$-genus of any odd-dimensional compact oriented manifold is identically zero. $\square$

**Remark 7.1‚Ä≤‚Ä≤ (Conceptual Distinction between Characteristic Class and Genus)** The $\hat{A}$-**class** is a mixed-degree cohomology class; the value $\hat{A}=1\in H^0$ is trivial. The $\hat{A}$-**genus** is the integral of the top-degree component of the $\hat{A}$-class, and vanishes identically on odd-dimensional manifolds. For $M(a)$ ($\dim=9$), the non-vanishing components of $\hat{A}$ are only $\hat{A}_0=1$ (degree 0), $\hat{A}_4$ (degree 4, from $p_1$, but here $p_1=0$), $\hat{A}_8$ (degree 8, from $p_2, p_1^2$, but here these also vanish). This is consistent with the Atiyah-Singer index theorem: the analytic index of the Dirac operator on an odd-dimensional manifold is identically zero (no chiral decomposition). $\square$
