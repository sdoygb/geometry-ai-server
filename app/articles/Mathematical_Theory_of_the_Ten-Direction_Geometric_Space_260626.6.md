## Chapter 2 Axiomatization of the Excitation Parameter Space

### 2.1 Axiom 1 (Topology of the Parameter Space)

**Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** The excitation parameter space $D$ is obtained by removing two distinct points $p_0$ and $p_*$ from the one-dimensional compact connected manifold $S^1$ (the circle):

$$D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}, \qquad p_0 \neq p_*$$

The two removed points are called the **vacuum point** ($p_0$) and the **degeneration point** ($p_*$), respectively. Consequently, $D$ consists of two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1), \qquad D_- \cong (0,1)$$

Each component is homeomorphic to an open interval. The parameter $x \in D_\pm$ labels an excitation state.

**Lemma 2.1 (Topological Structure of the Components)** Let $S^1$ be the standard unit circle $\{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi \in [0,2\pi)\}$. Removing two points $p_0=e^{i\phi_0}$ and $p_*=e^{i\phi_*}$ (let us set $0\leq \phi_0 < \phi_* < 2\pi$ without loss of generality), $S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}$ has exactly two connected components:
$$D_+ = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_0 < \phi < \phi_*\}, \quad D_- = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_* < \phi < \phi_0+2\pi\}.$$
Each component, as a subspace of $S^1$ under the subspace topology, is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0,1)$.

*Proof.* Consider the standard covering map $\pi: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $\pi(\phi)=e^{i\phi}$. On $\mathbb{R}$, $\pi^{-1}(S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}) = \mathbb{R}\setminus\{\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi \mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}$. Each connected component of this set is an open interval $(\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi)$ or $(\phi_*+2k\pi, \phi_0+2(k+1)\pi)$. Since the restriction of $\pi$ to an interval of length $2\pi$ is a homeomorphism, the images of these open intervals in $S^1$ are precisely $D_+$ and $D_-$. Each open interval is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, hence $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$. $\square$

### 2.2 Axiom 2 (Analytic Properties of the Geometric Quantity)

**Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** On the parameter space $D$, there exists a geometric quantity $S: D \to (0,+\infty)$ satisfying:

1. **Continuity**: $S$ is continuous on each connected component $D_\pm$;
2. **Vacuum Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_0$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$;
3. **Degeneration Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_*$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$.

**Remark 2.1** Axiom 2 does not require $S$ to have the same functional expression on $D_+$ and $D_-$, nor does it require $S$ to be continuous on the whole of $D$ ($p_0$ and $p_*$ are not in $D$). Thus $S$ may be different functions on the two independent branches, as long as each satisfies the boundary conditions.

### 2.3 The Range Theorem

**Theorem 2.1 (Range)** Under Axiom 1 and Axiom 2, the range of $S$ on each connected component $D_\pm$ is $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$.

*Proof.* By Axiom 1, $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is connected. By Axiom 2, $S$ is continuous on $D_\pm$, so the image $S(D_\pm)$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e., an interval. By the vacuum limit, $\inf S(D_\pm) = 0$; by the degeneration limit, $\sup S(D_\pm) = +\infty$. Since $p_0, p_* \notin D$, the endpoints $0$ and $+\infty$ are not attained. Therefore $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed Explanation:** The connectedness argument may be further expanded as follows. Let $D_+ \cong (0,1)$, and $S: D_+ \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Suppose $S(D_+)$ is not an interval; then there exist $\alpha < \beta < \gamma$ such that $\alpha, \gamma \in S(D_+)$ but $\beta \notin S(D_+)$. Set $U = S^{-1}((-\infty, \beta))$ and $V = S^{-1}((\beta, +\infty))$; then $U,V$ are disjoint open sets in $D_+$ with $U\cup V = D_+$, contradicting the connectedness of $D_+$. Hence $S(D_+)$ must be an interval. Together with the boundary limits, this interval must be $(0,+\infty)$.

### 2.4 The Existence Theorem and the Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis

**Theorem 2.2 (Existence)** For any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, there exists at least one excitation state $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$ on each branch, such that $S(x_+) = S(x_-) = S_0$.

*Proof.* By Theorem 2.1, $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. For any $S_0 > 0$, clearly $S_0 \in (0, +\infty)$. Hence $S^{-1}(S_0) \cap D_\pm \neq \varnothing$. $\square$

**Definition 2.1' (Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis)** If $S$ is strictly monotonic on $D_\pm$, then for any $S_0>0$, the $x_+$ and $x_-$ in Theorem 2.2 are uniquely determined. Strict monotonicity is **not** a theorem derivable from Axioms 1–2, but rather a **constructive hypothesis** of the framework. Its introduction is equivalent to requiring that $S$ constitutes a global coordinate function on $D_\pm$.

*Proof.* A strictly monotonic continuous function is injective, hence the preimage is unique. $\square$

$$a = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_1^\Delta}}, \quad b = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_3^\Delta}}, \quad c = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\Lambda k_0 \cdot \lambda_1^\Delta}}.$$

**Self-consistency Condition** A triple of positive numbers $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ are the first three non-zero eigenvalues of some $M(a)$ if and only if

$$\lambda_2 = \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda_3 = \Lambda(S_3) \lambda_1 = 3\lambda_1.$$

In this case, $a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1}$.

**Proof.** By Theorem 3.2, $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2 \Rightarrow a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1^\Delta}$; $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3/b^2 \Rightarrow b = \sqrt{3/\lambda_3^\Delta}$; by the proportionality constraint of Definition 3.1, $c = a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$. The self-consistency condition follows directly from $\lambda_2^\Delta = 8/a^2$ and $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3\Lambda/a^2$. $\square$

**Lemma 3.1 (One-to-One Correspondence between Spectral Data and Geometry)** Under the locking of interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$ determined by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, there is an explicit one-to-one mapping between the first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues and the scale factors $(a,b,c)$. Given $\lambda_1^\Delta$, $\lambda_2^\Delta$, $\lambda_3^\Delta$, the scale factors $(a,b,c)$ can be uniquely inversely solved; conversely, given $(a,b,c)$, the first three eigenvalues are uniquely determined.

### 3.6 The Spectral Rigidity Theorem

**Theorem 3.4 (Spectral Rigidity of Constrained Product Spheres)** Let $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ be two constrained product spheres with the same interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$. **Proof.** By Proposition 3.2, the first non-zero Laplace eigenvalue is $\lambda_1^\Delta=3/a^2$. By the proportionality constraints $b=a/\sqrt{\Lambda}$, $c=a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$ of Definition 3.1, the scale triple $(a,b,c)$ is uniquely determined, so $M(a')$ is isometric to $M(a)$. $\square$

**Remark 3.1** The spectral rigidity in this theorem stems from the moduli space being parametrized by a single scale factor. ### 3.8 The Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of Riemannian manifolds, and $\mathcal{M}$ its moduli space (i.e., the set of isometry classes). Suppose $\mathcal{M}$ has a smooth manifold structure of dimension $d$. Define the spectral mapping $\sigma_k$ that sends each isometry class to the vector of its first $k$ non-zero Laplace eigenvalues:

$$\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k, \quad [g] \mapsto (\lambda_1^\Delta, \lambda_2^\Delta, \dots, \lambda_k^\Delta).$$

**Theorem 3.6 (Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity)** Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the moduli space of some manifold class, with finite dimension $d$. If the first $k$ Laplace eigenvalues can achieve spectral rigidity (i.e., $\sigma_k$ is injective), then necessarily $d \leq k$. Under the interlocking constants determined by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, the moduli space dimension of $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ is $d=1$, so $k\geq1$ already suffices to satisfy the necessary condition.

**Proof.** Suppose $d > k$. Since eigenvalues depend continuously on the metric, the spectral mapping $\sigma_k$ is continuous. If $\sigma_k$ is injective, it gives a continuous injection from the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ into $\mathbb{R}^k$.

By the **Brouwer Invariance of Domain Theorem**: If $U \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f: U \to \mathbb{R}^k$ is a continuous injection, then $f(U)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ (with respect to the subspace topology). However, every point of the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ has an open neighborhood $U$ homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^d$. If $d > k$, $f(U)$, as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$, cannot have topological dimension exceeding $k$, whereas $U$ has topological dimension $d > k$. This contradicts the Invariance of Domain Theorem ($f(U)$ cannot be open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ because its topological dimension is insufficient). Hence a continuous injection from a $d$-dimensional manifold into $\mathbb{R}^k$ cannot exist when $d > k$.

Therefore, if $d > k$, a continuous injective mapping $\sigma_k$ cannot exist. Spectral rigidity requires $d \leq k$. $\square$

**Detailed Explanation:** The core of the Brouwer Invariance of Domain Theorem is: if $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^d$ is a continuous injection, then $f(U)$ is also open. When $d>k$, any continuous injection $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^k$ (with $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ open) cannot exist, because $f(U)$ has no interior points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (its topological dimension is $k<d$), whereas the Invariance of Domain Theorem would require $f(U)$ to be open in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (after embedding $\mathbb{R}^k$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$). A more direct argument: if $d>k$, then the image $f(U)\subset\mathbb{R}^k$ of any open set $U$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has Lebesgue measure zero (since $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$), but a continuous injection maps open sets to open sets (in the relative topology), and an open set in $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$, contradicting the fact that $U$ has positive measure in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

**Remark 3.6' (Dimension Counting Argument)** Theorem 3.6 can also be derived directly from the dimension counting of standard differential topology: if $d>k$, any open set $U$ of a $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ cannot be continuously injectively embedded into $\mathbb{R}^k$ (since the topological dimension of $\mathbb{R}^k$ is $k<d$). Hence injectivity of the spectral mapping $\sigma_k$ requires $d\leq k$. The moduli space of the present constrained product sphere class has dimension $d=1$, so $k\geq 1$ suffices to satisfy the necessary condition; this paper uses $k=3$ to provide self-consistency checks and a spectral signature.

**Corollary 3.2** In the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ (with interlocking constants locked by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0), the moduli space dimension is $d = 1$. Hence $k \geq 1$ eigenvalues already suffice to satisfy the necessary condition $d \leq k$; this paper uses $k = 3$ eigenvalues, which not only satisfies the necessary condition but also provides self-consistency checks and a spectral signature.

### 4.7 The Range Theorem and Strict Convexity

**Theorem 4.4 (Range Theorem and Strict Convexity)** Under $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$ satisfying Axiom 3, on the domain
$$D_\theta=\{(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)\mid\theta_i>0,\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ\},$$
the range of the six-term action $S$ is
$$S(D_\theta)=[24,+\infty),$$
and $S$ is a **strictly convex function** on $D_\theta$, with the symmetric point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$ being the unique global strict minimum.

*Proof.*

**(1) Symmetric point minimum.**
Consider the symmetric point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$. Then $\sin30^\circ=1/2$, $\sin^2 30^\circ=1/4$, so
$$\frac{1}{\sin^2 30^\circ}=4,\quad\frac{1}{\sin 30^\circ\sin 30^\circ}=4.$$
Sum of three single-field terms: $3\times4=12$. Sum of three cross terms: $3\times4=12$. Hence
$$S(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)=12+12=24.$$

**(2) Strict minimum proof — Lagrange multiplier method.**
Under the constraint $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=\pi/2$, use the Lagrange multiplier method to find the extremum of $S$. Set up the Lagrange function
$$\mathcal{L}(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3,\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i} + \sum_{i<j} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j} - \lambda\left(\sum_{i=1}^3 \theta_i - \frac{\pi}{2}\right).$$

Partial derivative with respect to $\theta_k$:
$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_k} = -\frac{2\cos\theta_k}{\sin^3\theta_k} - \sum_{j\neq k} \frac{\cos\theta_k}{\sin^2\theta_k\sin\theta_j} - \lambda = 0.$$

Factor out the common factor $-\cos\theta_k/\sin^2\theta_k$:
$$-\frac{\cos\theta_k}{\sin^2\theta_k}\left(\frac{2}{\sin\theta_k} + \sum_{j\neq k} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_j}\right) = \lambda.$$

By $S_3$ permutation symmetry, if $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=\pi/6$, then the above equation gives the same $\lambda$ for $k=1,2,3$, satisfying the Lagrange equations. Hence $(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6)$ is the unique interior critical point.

**Step 2a: Verification of Hessian positive definiteness.** Compute the Hessian quadratic form of $S$ on the constrained tangent space $T_{\theta}D_\theta = \{v\in\mathbb{R}^3 \mid v_1+v_2+v_3=0\}$. Write $s_i=\sin\theta_i$, $c_i=\cos\theta_i$.

For a single-sector term $f_i=1/s_i^2$, $\partial^2 f_i/\partial \theta_i^2 = (2+4c_i^2)/s_i^4$. At $30^\circ$: $\partial^2 f_i/\partial \theta_i^2 = (2+3)/(1/16) = 80$.

For a dual-sector term $f_{ij}=1/(s_i s_j)$, $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i^2 = (1+c_i^2)/(s_i^3 s_j)$. At $30^\circ$: $s_i=s_j=1/2$, so $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i^2 = (7/4)/(1/16) = 28$.

For mixed partial derivatives, $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i\partial \theta_j = c_i c_j/(s_i^2 s_j^2)$. At $30^\circ$: $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i\partial \theta_j = (3/4)/(1/16) = 12$.

Thus the Hessian matrix entries are:
$$H_{ii} = 80 + 28 + 28 = 136,\quad H_{ij} = 12\quad(i\neq j).$$

For any $v\in T_{\theta}D_\theta$ (i.e., $\sum v_i=0$), the quadratic form
$$v^T H v = 136\sum v_i^2 + 24\sum_{i<j}v_iv_j = 124\sum v_i^2 > 0$$
(using $(\sum v_i)^2=0 \Rightarrow \sum_{i<j}v_iv_j = -\frac{1}{2}\sum v_i^2$). Hence $H$ is positive definite on the constrained tangent space, $S$ is strictly convex on $D_\theta$, and the symmetric point $30^\circ$ is the unique global strict minimum.

**(3) Unboundedness above.**
When any angle tends to $0^\circ$, the corresponding term $1/\sin^2\theta_i\to+\infty$, so $S\to+\infty$. Hence the range is unbounded above.

**(4) Conclusion by continuity.**
$S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ is continuous on $D_\theta$. Combining (2), (3), and the Intermediate Value Theorem, we obtain $S(D_\theta)=[24,+\infty)$. $\square$

### 8.1 Graph Laplacian and $\epsilon$-Net Construction

Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite undirected weighted graph with $|V|=N$. Vertex weights $\mu(x)>0$, edge weights $\omega(x,y)=\omega(y,x)\geq 0$. The normalized graph Laplacian $\Delta_G$ acts on functions $f:V\to\mathbb{R}$ as:

$$\Delta_G f(x)=\frac{1}{\mu(x)}\sum_{y\sim x} \omega(x,y)(f(x)-f(y)).\tag{8.1}$$

Eigenvalues: $0=\lambda_0^{(G)}\leq \lambda_1^{(G)}\leq\dots\leq \lambda_{N-1}^{(G)}\leq 2$. The spectral gap $\lambda_1^{(G)}>0$ if and only if $G$ is connected.

Standard $\epsilon$-net construction:
- **Vertex set**: $V_N$ is an $N$-point $\epsilon$-net on $M$, $\max_x \min_v d_M(x,v)\to 0$;
- **Edge set**: $x\sim y$ when $d_M(x,y)\leq h_N$ ($h_N\to 0$);
- **Weights**: $\omega(x,y)=\text{Vol}(B(x,h_N)\cap B(y,h_N))$ or Gaussian weights.

Consistency requirement: $h_N\to 0$ and $N h_N^d\to\infty$.

### 8.2 The Spectral Convergence Theorem

*Proof.* Based on the $\Gamma$-convergence method of García Trillos–Slepčev. **Step 2: Mosco convergence.** We say $E_{G_N}$ Mosco-converges to $E_M$ if:
(a) For any $u\in L^2(M)$ and any sequence $f_N\in L^2(V_N)$ with $f_N\to u$, we have $E_M(u) \leq \liminf E_{G_N}(f_N)$;

Under (H1)–(H3), one can verify that $E_{G_N}$ Mosco-converges to $E_M$.

$$\lambda_j = \min_{\dim V=j} \max_{u\in V} \frac{E(u)}{\|u\|^2}.$$
Mosco convergence guarantees that the discrete Rayleigh quotients converge to the continuous Rayleigh quotients, hence the eigenvalues converge.
