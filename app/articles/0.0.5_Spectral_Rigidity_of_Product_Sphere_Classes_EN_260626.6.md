## Abstract

This paper studies a class of nine-dimensional constrained product spheres $M(a)=S^3(a)\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda})\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})$ locked by three interlocking constants: $k_0>1$ (the binary compactness constant, the index of the maximal normal subgroup of $S_3$), $\Lambda>8/3$ (the trisection ratio parameter, the number of conjugacy classes of $S_3$), and $\ell_0>0$ (the scale constant). We prove that in this class of manifolds, the first three non-zero Laplace–Beltrami eigenvalues already constitute a complete set of coordinates for the moduli space; if two such manifolds share the same first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues, then they must be isometric. Furthermore, we establish the Dirac index theorem ($\hat{A}(M(a))=1$), the classification of Laplace low-energy eigenmodes, the algebraic relation between the Yang–Mills term in the spectral action and the Hessian soft/hard modes, the normal bundle curvature–Hessian locking theorem, and the algebraic locking of heat kernel coefficients and spectral triple reconstruction. Finally, we give a universal dimensional necessary condition for spectral rigidity, explaining the methodological reason why spectral rigidity fails in general classes yet holds in constrained subclasses.

**Keywords:** spectral rigidity; Laplace–Beltrami operator; Dirac index; Atiyah-Singer index theorem; product sphere; moduli space; dimensional necessary condition; isospectral non-isometric; normal bundle curvature; spectral action; heat kernel expansion

## 1 Introduction: From Two Eigenvalues to Three Eigenvalues

In 1966, Kac posed the famous "Can one hear the shape of a drum?" question: can the spectrum of the Laplace operator uniquely determine the isometry class of a manifold? In 1992, Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert constructed counterexamples of isospectral non-isometric planar domains, proving that spectral rigidity does not hold in general manifold classes.

Previously, if only the "first two non-zero Laplace eigenvalues" were used, in the product sphere model $S^3(a)\times S^3(b)\times S^3(c)$, the first two eigenvalues $3/a^2$ and $3/b^2$ could only determine the ratio of two radii, but could not uniquely determine the third radius $c$; even assuming $c$ is locked by an additional ratio, the first two eigenvalues still do not provide an internal self-consistency check. The correction in this paper: we upgrade "the first two eigenvalues" to "the first three eigenvalues."

In the product sphere model, the first three non-zero eigenvalues correspond to the modes $(1,0,0)$, $(2,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, with explicit expressions

$$\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{3}{a^2}, \quad \lambda_2^\Delta = \frac{8}{a^2}, \quad \lambda_3^\Delta = \frac{3}{b^2} = \frac{3\Lambda}{a^2}$$

where $b^2/a^2 = 1/\Lambda$. From $\lambda_1^\Delta$ and $\lambda_2^\Delta$ one can independently solve for $a$ (with the ratio $\lambda_2^\Delta/\lambda_1^\Delta = 8/3$ serving as an internal self-consistency check), and from $\lambda_3^\Delta$ one solves for $b$; while $c$ is determined by the ratio locking and the fixed constant $k_0$. Hence, the first three eigenvalues completely determine all scale parameters.

The strategy adopted in this paper is: construct a highly constrained manifold subclass whose moduli space is parameterized by a single scale factor, so that spectral rigidity automatically holds. The key to the success of this strategy is: through strict constraints (three $S^3$ factors, ratio locking, triviality of the tangent bundle), we compress the infinite-dimensional "general manifold class" into a one-dimensional constrained subclass, thereby satisfying the dimensional necessary condition for spectral rigidity.

## 4 Explicit Computation of Laplace Eigenvalues

### 4.1 Spectral Separation on Product Manifolds

**Proposition 4.1.** On the constrained product sphere $M(a)$, the eigenvalues of the Laplace–Beltrami operator $\Delta_g$ are given by

$$\lambda_{p,q,r}^\Delta = \frac{p(p+2)}{a^2} + \frac{q(q+2)}{b^2} + \frac{r(r+2)}{c^2}, \quad p,q,r \in \mathbb{N}_0.$$

where $p=q=r=0$ corresponds to the zero eigenvalue (constant function), and non-zero eigenvalues begin with $p+q+r \geq 1$.

*Proof.* On a product manifold, the Laplace operator separates as $\Delta_g = \Delta_1 + \Delta_2 + \Delta_3$, where $\Delta_i$ is the Laplace operator on each factor. It is known that on $S^3(R)$, the eigenvalue corresponding to a spherical harmonic of degree $p$ is $p(p+2)/R^2$, so the total eigenvalue is the sum of the three. $\square$

**Supplementary Remark:** The standard derivation of Laplace eigenvalues on $S^3(R)$ is as follows. Embed $S^3(R)$ into $\mathbb{R}^4$ as $\{x\in\mathbb{R}^4: |x|=R\}$. The restriction of a homogeneous harmonic polynomial of degree $p$ to $S^3(R)$ yields a spherical harmonic. By separation of the Euclidean Laplacian into radial and angular parts, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4} = \partial_r^2 + \frac{3}{r}\partial_r + \frac{1}{r^2}\Delta_{S^3}$. For a homogeneous function of degree $p$, $f(r,\theta)=r^p Y(\theta)$, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4}f=0$ yields $\Delta_{S^3}Y = -p(p+2)Y$. On $S^3(R)$, the metric scaling factor is $R^2$, so the eigenvalue is $p(p+2)/R^2$.

### 4.2 Explicit Expressions for the First Three Eigenvalues

- Second eigenvalue (mode $(2,0,0)$): $\lambda_2^\Delta = 8/a^2$;

*Proof.* From Proposition 4.1, the eigenvalues of each candidate mode are computed as follows:

| Mode (p,q,r) | Eigenvalue Expression | Ratio to $3/a^2$ |
|:---|:---|:---|

| $(2,0,0)$ | $8/a^2$ | $8/3 \approx 2.667$ |

Hence, the first three non-zero eigenvalues are precisely given by the first three rows of the above table. $\square$

## 5 Spectral Rigidity Theorem

### 5.1 Statement of the Theorem

**Theorem 5.1 (Spectral Rigidity of Constrained Product Spheres).** Let $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ be two constrained product spheres with the same interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$. If their Laplace–Beltrami operators share the same first three non-zero eigenvalues, then $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric.

### 5.2 Proof

**Step 1: Parameterization of the moduli space and distinction of isometry classes.**

By Theorem 3.1, with the interlocking constants locked, the moduli space of the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ is parameterized by $a \in (0,\infty)$. If $a \neq a'$, then the first Laplace eigenvalue $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2 \neq 3/a'^2 = \lambda_1^{\Delta'}$, or equivalently, the volume $V(a) = (2\pi^2)^3 a^3 (a/\sqrt{\Lambda})^3 (a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})^3 \neq V(a')$. Isometric manifolds must be isospectral and have equal volumes, so different $a$ correspond to different isometry classes. Therefore, any difference in isometry class between two manifolds of this class can only arise from $a \neq a'$.

**Step 2: Spectral data determine the scales.**

By Theorem 4.2, the first three eigenvalues $\lambda_1^\Delta$, $\lambda_2^\Delta$, $\lambda_3^\Delta$ uniquely determine $a$ and $b$, and $c$ is uniquely determined by $a$ and the fixed $k_0$.

**Step 3: Topological uniqueness.**

$M(a)$ and $M(a')$, as smooth manifolds, are both diffeomorphic to $S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$, independent of the scale parameter $a$.

**Step 4: Conclusion.**

Combining the above, $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ have the same metric (determined by the same $(a,b,c)$) and the same topology (trivial), and hence are isometric. $\square$

**Remark 5.1.** The root of spectral rigidity in this theorem lies in the fact that the moduli space is parameterized by a single scale factor. Since the isometry classes of constrained product spheres satisfying Definition 3.1 with locked interlocking constants are uniquely labeled by $a$, and the first three Laplace eigenvalues (indeed, already the first one suffices) uniquely determine $a$, two manifolds sharing the same first three eigenvalues must belong to the same isometry class.

**Remark 5.2 (Completeness of the First Eigenvalue).** In the single-parameter family $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ with locked interlocking constants, the moduli space is one-dimensional. The first non-zero eigenvalue $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2$ itself already constitutes a complete coordinate for the moduli space (bijective with $a$). The second and third among the first three eigenvalues provide self-consistency checks (they must satisfy the ratios $8/3$ and $\Lambda$) and serve as the spectral signature of the class, for determining whether a given manifold belongs to this class.

### 5.3 Compatibility with Classical Counterexamples

**Theorem 5.2.** Sunada-type isospectral non-isometric counterexamples do not exist in the constrained product sphere class.

*Proof.* The Sunada method requires a non-trivial fundamental group and finite covers. $M(a)$ is the product $S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$. Since $S^3$ is simply connected, the product is also simply connected, with fundamental group zero. Hence the Sunada method is inapplicable in this class. $\square$

## 6 Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity

### 6.1 The Core Problem: Why Spectral Rigidity Fails in General

The isospectral non-isometric counterexamples of Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert (1992) reveal a fundamental fact: in a general manifold class, a finite number of Laplace eigenvalues cannot distinguish different isometry classes. This section provides a universal explanation based on the dimension of the moduli space: for spectral rigidity to hold, the number of eigenvalues used must be no less than the dimension of the moduli space.

### 6.2 The Spectral Map and Moduli Space Embedding

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of Riemannian manifolds, and $\mathcal{M}$ its moduli space (i.e., the set of isometry classes). Assume $\mathcal{M}$ has a smooth manifold structure of dimension $d$ (which may be finite or infinite). Define the spectral map $\sigma_k$ sending each isometry class to the vector formed by its first $k$ non-zero Laplace eigenvalues:

$$\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k, \quad [g] \mapsto (\lambda_1^\Delta, \lambda_2^\Delta, \dots, \lambda_k^\Delta).$$

If spectral rigidity holds for the first $k$ eigenvalues, then $\sigma_k$ must be injective: distinct isometry classes have distinct first $k$ eigenvalues.

### 6.3 The Dimensional Obstruction Theorem

**Theorem 6.1 (Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity).** Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the moduli space of a class of manifolds, having finite dimension $d$. If the first $k$ Laplace eigenvalues can realize spectral rigidity (i.e., $\sigma_k$ is injective), then necessarily $d \leq k$.

*Proof.* Suppose $d > k$. Since eigenvalues depend continuously on the metric (in an appropriate function space topology), the spectral map $\sigma_k$ is a continuous map. If $\sigma_k$ is injective, then $\sigma_k$ gives a continuous injective map from the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ into $\mathbb{R}^k$.

By **Brouwer's Invariance of Domain Theorem**: if $U \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f: U \to \mathbb{R}^k$ is a continuous injective map, then $f(U)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ (with respect to the subspace topology). However, every point of the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ has an open neighborhood $U$ homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^d$. If $d > k$, then $f(U)$, as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$, cannot have topological dimension exceeding $k$, whereas $U$ has topological dimension $d > k$, contradicting the Invariance of Domain Theorem ($f(U)$ cannot be open in $\mathbb{R}^k$ because its topological dimension is insufficient). Hence, a $d$-dimensional manifold cannot be mapped continuously and injectively into $\mathbb{R}^k$ when $d > k$.

Therefore, if $d > k$, a continuous injective map $\sigma_k$ cannot exist. Spectral rigidity requires $d \leq k$. $\square$

**Detailed Explanation:** The core of Brouwer's Invariance of Domain Theorem is: if $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^d$ is a continuous injective map, then $f(U)$ is also open. When $d>k$, viewing $\mathbb{R}^k$ as a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^d$, any continuous injective map $f:U\to\mathbb{R}^k$ (with $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ open) cannot exist, because $f(U)$ has no interior points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (its topological dimension is $k<d$), while the Invariance of Domain Theorem requires $f(U)$ to be open in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (after embedding $\mathbb{R}^k$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$). A more direct argument: if $d>k$, then for any open set $U\subset\mathbb{R}^d$, the image $f(U)\subset\mathbb{R}^k$ has Lebesgue measure zero (since $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$), but a continuous injective map sends open sets to open sets (in the relative topology), and an open set in $\mathbb{R}^k$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^d$, contradicting that $U$ has positive measure in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

**Remark 6.1 (Dimension Counting Argument).** Theorem 6.1 also follows directly from standard differential topology dimension counting: if $d>k$, then any open set $U$ of the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ cannot be continuously and injectively embedded into $\mathbb{R}^k$ (since the topological dimension of $\mathbb{R}^k$ is $k<d$). Hence the injectivity of the spectral map $\sigma_k$ requires $d\leq k$. The moduli space of the present constrained product sphere class has dimension $d=1$, so $k\geq 1$ suffices to satisfy the necessary condition; this paper uses $k=3$ to provide self-consistency checks and a spectral signature.

**Corollary 6.1.** In the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ (with interlocking constants locked by Proposition 2.0), the moduli space dimension is $d = 1$. Hence, only $k \geq 1$ eigenvalues are required to satisfy the necessary condition $d \leq k$; this paper uses $k = 3$ eigenvalues, which not only satisfies the necessary condition but also provides self-consistency checks and a spectral signature.

## 7 Dirac Index and Spectral Action

### 7.1 The Dirac Index Theorem

**Theorem 7.1 (The $\hat{A}$-Class of $M(a)$).** $M(a)=S^3\times S^3\times S^3$ is a nine-dimensional closed manifold. Its $\hat{A}$ characteristic class satisfies:

$$\hat{A}(M(a)) = 1 \in H^0(M(a)).$$

However, as the **$\hat{A}$-genus** of a nine-dimensional manifold, its integral is zero by definition:

$$\int_{M(a)}\hat{A}(M(a))=0.\tag{7.1}$$

*Proof.* For an odd-dimensional manifold $S^3$, the Pontryagin classes satisfy $p_i\in H^{4i}(M)$, and $p_i=0$ whenever $4i>3$. Hence $\hat{A}(S^3)=1\in H^0(S^3)$. By the product formula $\hat{A}(M_1\times M_2)=\hat{A}(M_1)\wedge\hat{A}(M_2)$, we have $\hat{A}(M(a))=1\wedge 1\wedge 1=1\in H^0(M(a))$.

However, the $\hat{A}$ characteristic class is a mixed-degree cohomology class: $\hat{A} = \hat{A}_0 + \hat{A}_4 + \hat{A}_8 + \cdots$, where $\hat{A}_{4k}\in H^{4k}(M)$. On a nine-dimensional manifold, there is no integer $k$ such that $4k=9$, so $\hat{A}$ has no nine-dimensional component. The integral $\int_M$ is defined in characteristic class theory as the pairing of the top-degree component with the fundamental class $[M]\in H_d(M)$; since the highest non-zero component of $\hat{A}(M(a))$ is at most 8-dimensional (and in fact only the 0-dimensional component is present), there is no 9-dimensional component to pair with the 9-dimensional fundamental class, so the $\hat{A}$-genus is zero by definition. This is a classical result: the $\hat{A}$-genus of any odd-dimensional compact oriented manifold is identically zero. $\square$

**Detailed Expansion: Definition of the $\hat{A}$-class.** For a real vector bundle $E$, the $\hat{A}$-class is given by the formal power series $\hat{A}(E)=\prod_i \frac{x_i/2}{\sinh(x_i/2)}$, where $x_i$ are formal Chern roots. For $S^3$, the tangent bundle $TS^3$ is trivial, so all Pontryagin classes $p_i=0$, and $\hat{A}(TS^3)=1\in H^0(S^3)$. By the multiplicative property of the product formula, $\hat{A}(M(a))=1\in H^0(M(a))$.

However, we must note: the integral definition of the $\hat{A}$-genus requires taking the component of degree equal to $\dim M$. For $M(a)$ ($\dim=9$), the non-zero components of $\hat{A}$ are only $\hat{A}_0=1$ (degree 0), $\hat{A}_4$ (degree 4, contributed by $p_1$, but here $p_1=0$), $\hat{A}_8$ (degree 8, contributed by $p_2, p_1^2$, but here also zero). No degree-9 component exists, so the integral must be zero. This is consistent with the Atiyah-Singer index theorem: on odd-dimensional manifolds, the analytic index of the Dirac operator is identically 0 (no chiral decomposition). $\square$

**Remark 7.1'.** Theorem 7.1 has proven that $\hat{A}(M(a)) = 1\in H^0(M(a))$, and that as the $\hat{A}$-genus of a nine-dimensional manifold, $\int_{M(a)}\hat{A}(M(a))=0$. On odd-dimensional manifolds, the top-degree component of the $\hat{A}$-class is automatically zero, so it does not enter the leading heat kernel coefficient $a_0$, nor does it provide a non-trivial topological index. The physical content of the spectral action comes from the heat kernel coefficients $a_{2n}$ (geometric scales) rather than the $\hat{A}$-genus (topological index). For even-dimensional manifolds (such as four-dimensional ones), the $\hat{A}$-class would appear in the index theorem and in higher heat kernel coefficients such as $a_d$, yielding non-trivial topological-geometric correlations.

### 7.4 Normal Bundle Curvature–Hessian Locking

**Theorem 7.5 (Normal Bundle Curvature–Hessian Locking).** Within the framework of Axiom 3 (holographic screen encoding condition), Additional Hypothesis 4.1 (constant curvature condition, see 0.0.6), and Theorem 7.1, let $\Sigma\subset M(a)$ be the geometric constraint section, and $N\Sigma$ its rank $7$ normal bundle. At the stable critical point $\theta_0=(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)$, let $h=\text{diag}(\kappa_1,\kappa_2)$ be the second fundamental form. Then the curvature $2$-form $R^\perp$ of the induced Spin connection $\nabla^\perp$ on the normal bundle $N\Sigma$ satisfies:

$$\text{tr}_{\rho_7}(R^\perp\wedge *R^\perp)=\frac{(\kappa_1-\kappa_2)^2}{2}\,d\text{vol}_\Sigma,\tag{7.5}$$

where $\text{tr}_{\rho_7}$ denotes the trace in the $8$-dimensional spinor representation of $\mathfrak{so}(7)$.

*Proof.* At the stable critical point $\theta_0$, the positive-definiteness of the Hessian guarantees that the second fundamental form $h$, when diagonalized in an appropriate basis of $T\Sigma$, takes the form $\text{diag}(\kappa_1,\kappa_2)$. By the Ricci equation, the normal bundle curvature $2$-form satisfies
$$R^\perp(X,Y) = \bigl(R^{M(a)}(X,Y)\bigr)^\perp + [A_{h(X,\cdot)}, A_{h(Y,\cdot)}],$$
where $A_\xi$ is the shape operator (Weingarten map) along the normal vector $\xi$, and $h(X,\cdot)$ denotes the normal-vector-valued second fundamental form in the tangential direction $X$. At the $S_3$-symmetric point, the product structure of $M(a)$ makes the tangential curvature term $\bigl(R^{M(a)}(X,Y)\bigr)^\perp$ vanish on the $U(1)$ normal subbundle corresponding to the $\mathcal{I}$-sector (by symmetry of the sectional curvatures of the $S^3$ factors), so the normal bundle curvature on that subbundle is entirely contributed by the commutator of the shape operators.

Take $\{e_1, e_2\}$ as the principal direction basis of $h$, with corresponding principal curvatures $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$. For the complex line bundle $L_{\mathcal{I}}\cong\mathcal{O}(1)$ in the normal bundle $N\Sigma$, obtained by complexifying the projection kernel of the $\mathcal{I}$-sector, the corresponding $U(1)$ sub-connection curvature $F$ satisfies: the shape operators $A_{h(e_1,e_1)}$ and $A_{h(e_2,e_2)}$ are $\text{diag}(\kappa_1, \kappa_2)$ and $\text{diag}(\kappa_1, \kappa_2)$ respectively in the $T\Sigma$ basis, but in the $L_{\mathcal{I}}$ direction of the normal bundle, due to the non-isotropy of $h$, the commutator $[A_{h(e_1,\cdot)}, A_{h(e_2,\cdot)}]$ restricted to $L_{\mathcal{I}}$ produces a non-zero curvature $2$-form, whose squared modulus satisfies
$$\text{tr}_{\mathbb{C}}(F\wedge *F) = \frac{(\kappa_1-\kappa_2)^2}{2}\,d\text{vol}_\Sigma,\tag{7.5a}$$
where $\text{tr}_{\mathbb{C}}$ is the standard trace on the complex line bundle. This identity originates from the classical relation between the principal curvature difference and the $U(1)$ curvature of the normal bundle in a two-dimensional surface: on the $U(1)$ sub-connection of $L_{\mathcal{I}}$, the action of $[A_{h(e_1,\cdot)}, A_{h(e_2,\cdot)}]$ is equivalent to the curvature operator $(\kappa_1-\kappa_2)J$, where $J$ is the complex structure on $L_{\mathcal{I}}$, and direct computation yields $|F|^2=(\kappa_1-\kappa_2)^2/2$.

**Step 2: Branching law $\mathfrak{so}(7) \supset \mathfrak{so}(2)$ and normalization.** The $U(1)$ structure of the complex line bundle $L_{\mathcal{I}}$ corresponds to an $\mathfrak{so}(2)$ subalgebra within the normal bundle $N\Sigma$ (real rank 7). Choose a local orthonormal frame $\{\xi_1,\dots,\xi_7\}$ for $N\Sigma$ such that $L_{\mathcal{I}}$ is spanned by $\xi_1+i\xi_2$. Then the $\mathfrak{so}(2)$ subalgebra of $\mathfrak{so}(7)$ preserving this complex structure is generated by
$$J = e_1 \wedge e_2 \in \mathfrak{so}(7),$$
where $e_i$ are the dual basis. The real spinor representation $\rho_7$ (8-dimensional) of $\mathfrak{so}(7)$, when restricted to $\mathfrak{so}(2)$, decomposes by the standard branching law into 4 real 2-dimensional irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{so}(2)$:
$$\rho_7|_{\mathfrak{so}(2)} = 4 \cdot \rho_2^{(\text{real})},$$
where on each $\rho_2^{(\text{real})}$, $J$ acts as $J^2 = -I_2$.

Under the Killing form $B(X,Y) = 2(n-2)\text{tr}(XY) = 10\text{tr}(XY)$ ($n=7$) of $\mathfrak{so}(7)$, the normalization of $J$ satisfies $B(J,J) = 10\text{tr}(J^2) = 10\times(-8) = -80$. Choose the normalized generator $\tilde{J} = J/\sqrt{8}$ for $\mathfrak{so}(2)$, so that $\text{tr}_{\rho_7}(\tilde{J}^2) = -1$. Then the curvature $F = f \cdot \tilde{J}$ satisfies
$$\text{tr}_{\rho_7}(F\wedge *F) = f^2 \text{tr}_{\rho_7}(\tilde{J}\wedge *\tilde{J}) = f^2 \cdot (-1) \cdot (-1) \ d\text{vol}_\Sigma = f^2 \ d\text{vol}_\Sigma,$$
where the normalization of $\tilde{J}\wedge *\tilde{J}$ follows from $\text{tr}_{\rho_7}(\tilde{J}^2)=-1$ and the standard definition of the Hodge star operator. Substituting $f^2 = (\kappa_1-\kappa_2)^2/2$ (from Step 1) yields (7.5). $\square$

**Remark 7.2' (Rigor of the Normalization).** In the above proof, the branching law $\mathfrak{so}(7) \supset \mathfrak{so}(2)$ and the Killing form normalization are standard representation-theoretic results. $\rho_7$, as an irreducible representation of $Cl_{0,7}\cong\mathbb{R}(8)\oplus\mathbb{R}(8)$, restricts to $\mathfrak{so}(2)\subset\mathfrak{so}(7)$ with decomposition $4\cdot\rho_2^{(\text{real})}$, which can be read off directly from the weight diagram of the maximal torus $T^2$ of $Spin(7)$: the weights of $\rho_7$ are $\pm\frac{1}{2}(1,1,1)$ with all odd-sign variations; upon restriction to $\mathfrak{so}(2)$ (generated by $e_1\wedge e_2$), each weight pair contributes one 2-dimensional real representation of $\mathfrak{so}(2)$, totaling 4. The normalization factor $1/\sqrt{8}$ is chosen so that $\text{tr}_{\rho_7}(\tilde{J}^2)=-1$, consistent with the standard conventions of $\mathfrak{so}(2)$ representation theory.

**Detailed Expansion: Full Derivation of the Ricci Equation.** Let $\nabla$ be the Levi-Civita connection on $M(a)$, $\nabla^\Sigma$ the induced connection on $\Sigma$, and $\nabla^\perp$ the normal bundle connection. For tangential vector fields $X,Y,Z\in T\Sigma$ and a normal vector field $\xi\in N\Sigma$:
$$R^{M(a)}(X,Y)Z = R^\Sigma(X,Y)Z + h^\dagger(X, h(Y,Z)) - h^\dagger(Y, h(X,Z))$$
(Gauss equation),
$$R^{M(a)}(X,Y)\xi = R^\perp(X,Y)\xi + h(X, A_\xi Y) - h(Y, A_\xi X)$$
(Ricci equation), where $A_\xi$ is the shape operator and $h(X,Y)=\sum_\alpha g(A_{\xi_\alpha}X, Y)\xi_\alpha$.

At $\theta_0$, $S_3$ symmetry requires the shape operator $A$ to have eigenvalues $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ in some basis of $T\Sigma$. Taking $X,Y$ as principal directions, $A_X=\text{diag}(\kappa_1,\kappa_2)$, $A_Y=\text{diag}(\kappa_1,\kappa_2)$ (in the same basis), the commutator $[A_X,A_Y]$ vanishes on $T\Sigma$ but produces a non-trivial curvature $2$-form $F$ on the $L_{\mathcal{I}}$ subbundle of the normal bundle, whose squared modulus satisfies $|F|^2=(\kappa_1-\kappa_2)^2/2$. ### 7.5 Heat Kernel Coefficients and Spectral Triple Reconstruction

**Theorem 7.6 (Algebraic Locking of Heat Kernel Coefficients and Spectral Triple Reconstruction).** For the odd spectral triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$ on $M(a)$, the Wodzicki residue, Dixmier trace, and heat kernel expansion coefficients satisfy the following explicit relations. Let $V = \text{Vol}(M(a))$ be the volume of the manifold.

1. **Volume-Residue Locking** (standard Wodzicki residue definition):
   Using the standard Wodzicki residue definition (integral over the unit cosphere bundle $S^*M(a)$ with the metric-induced volume element, without the $(2\pi)^{-d}$ normalization factor):
   $$\text{Res}_W(D^{-9}) = \int_{S^*M(a)} \text{tr}(\sigma_{-9}(D^{-9}))\, dx\, d\xi = 2^{[9/2]} \text{Vol}(S^8) \cdot V = 16 \cdot \frac{32\pi^4}{105} \cdot V = \frac{512\pi^4}{105} V.\tag{7.6}$$
   If one adopts the convention in noncommutative geometry where the leading heat kernel coefficient is denoted $a_0 = V$ (i.e., having absorbed the spinor bundle rank $2^{[9/2]}$ and $(4\pi)^{-d/2}$ factors into the definition of $a_0$), then the above can be written as $\text{Res}_W(D^{-9}) = \frac{512\pi^4}{105} a_0$.

2. **Dixmier Trace–Heat Kernel Consistency** (Connes' formula):
   $$\text{Tr}_\omega(|D|^{-9}) = \frac{1}{9(2\pi)^9} \text{Res}_W(D^{-9}) = \frac{V}{945\pi^5} = \frac{a_0}{945\pi^5}.\tag{7.7}$$

3. **Curvature-Energy Scale Ratio**:
   $$\frac{a_2}{a_0} = \frac{1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0}{a^2}.\tag{7.8}$$

*Proof.* For the Dirac operator on a $d$-dimensional compact manifold, the Wodzicki residue $\text{Res}_W(D^{-d})$ equals the spinor bundle rank times the cosphere volume times the manifold volume (under the standard definition). For $d=9$, the spinor bundle rank is $2^{[9/2]}=16$, $\text{Vol}(S^8)=32\pi^4/105$, yielding (7.6). Connes' theorem asserts that $\text{Tr}_\omega(|D|^{-d}) = \frac{1}{d(2\pi)^d} \text{Res}_W(|D|^{-d})$, and substitution yields (7.7). From the heat kernel expansion definition, $a_2/a_0 = \langle R \rangle/6$; for $M(a)$, direct computation gives: the scalar curvature is $R = 6/a^2 + 6\Lambda/a^2 + 6\Lambda k_0/a^2 = 6(1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)/a^2$, thus $a_2 = \frac{1}{6}\int R = a_0(1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)/a^2$, yielding (7.8). $\square$

**Detailed Expansion: Computation of the Scalar Curvature.** For $S^3(R)$, the Ricci curvature is $\text{Ric} = 2/R^2 g$, and the scalar curvature is $R = 6/R^2$. For the product manifold $M(a)=S^3(a)\times S^3(b)\times S^3(c)$, the scalar curvature satisfies additivity:
$$R_M = R_{S^3(a)} + R_{S^3(b)} + R_{S^3(c)} = \frac{6}{a^2} + \frac{6}{b^2} + \frac{6}{c^2}.$$
Substituting $b=a/\sqrt{\Lambda}$, $c=a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$, we obtain
$$R_M = \frac{6}{a^2} + \frac{6\Lambda}{a^2} + \frac{6\Lambda k_0}{a^2} = \frac{6(1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)}{a^2}.$$
The standard formula for the heat kernel coefficient $a_2$ is $a_2 = \frac{1}{6}\int_M R\, d\text{vol}$, hence
$$a_2 = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{6(1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)}{a^2} \cdot V = \frac{(1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)V}{a^2},$$
i.e., $a_2/a_0 = (1+\Lambda+\Lambda k_0)/a^2$.

**Remark 7.3 (Clarification of Normalization Conventions).** In Theorem 7.6, this paper explicitly distinguishes two common conventions:
- **Standard mathematical convention**: The Wodzicki residue $\text{Res}_W$ is defined as the standard integral over the cosphere bundle, without the $(2\pi)^{-d}$ factor; the leading heat kernel coefficient is $a_0^{\text{std}} = (4\pi)^{-d/2} \text{rk}(\mathcal{S}) V$.
