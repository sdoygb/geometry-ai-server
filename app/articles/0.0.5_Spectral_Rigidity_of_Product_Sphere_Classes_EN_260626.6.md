## Abstract

This paper studies a class of nine-dimensional constrained product spheres $M(a)=S^3(a)\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda})\times S^3(a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0})$ locked by three interlocking constants: $k_0>1$ (the binary compactness constant, the index of the maximal normal subgroup of $S_3$), $\Lambda>8/3$ (the trisection ratio parameter, the number of conjugacy classes of $S_3$), and $\ell_0>0$ (the scale constant). We prove that in this class of manifolds, the first three non-zero Laplace–Beltrami eigenvalues already constitute a complete set of coordinates for the moduli space; if two such manifolds share the same first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues, then they must be isometric. Furthermore, we establish the Dirac index theorem ($\hat{A}(M(a))=1$), the classification of Laplace low-energy eigenmodes, the algebraic relation between the Yang–Mills term in the spectral action and the Hessian soft/hard modes, the normal bundle curvature–Hessian locking theorem, and the algebraic locking of heat kernel coefficients and spectral triple reconstruction. Finally, we give a universal dimensional necessary condition for spectral rigidity, explaining the methodological reason why spectral rigidity fails in general classes yet holds in constrained subclasses.

**Keywords:** spectral rigidity; Laplace–Beltrami operator; Dirac index; Atiyah-Singer index theorem; product sphere; moduli space; dimensional necessary condition; isospectral non-isometric; normal bundle curvature; spectral action; heat kernel expansion

## 1 Introduction: From Two Eigenvalues to Three Eigenvalues

In 1966, Kac posed the famous "Can one hear the shape of a drum?" question: can the spectrum of the Laplace operator uniquely determine the isometry class of a manifold? In 1992, Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert constructed counterexamples of isospectral non-isometric planar domains, proving that spectral rigidity does not hold in general manifold classes.

Previously, if only the "first two non-zero Laplace eigenvalues" were used, in the product sphere model $S^3(a)\times S^3(b)\times S^3(c)$, the first two eigenvalues $3/a^2$ and $3/b^2$ could only determine the ratio of two radii, but could not uniquely determine the third radius $c$; even assuming $c$ is locked by an additional ratio, the first two eigenvalues still do not provide an internal self-consistency check. The correction in this paper: we upgrade "the first two eigenvalues" to "the first three eigenvalues."

In the product sphere model, the first three non-zero eigenvalues correspond to the modes $(1,0,0)$, $(2,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, with explicit expressions

$$\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{3}{a^2}, \quad \lambda_2^\Delta = \frac{8}{a^2}, \quad \lambda_3^\Delta = \frac{3}{b^2} = \frac{3\Lambda}{a^2}$$

where $b^2/a^2 = 1/\Lambda$. From $\lambda_1^\Delta$ and $\lambda_2^\Delta$ one can independently solve for $a$ (with the ratio $\lambda_2^\Delta/\lambda_1^\Delta = 8/3$ serving as an internal self-consistency check), and from $\lambda_3^\Delta$ one solves for $b$; while $c$ is determined by the ratio locking and the fixed constant $k_0$. Hence, the first three eigenvalues completely determine all scale parameters.

The strategy adopted in this paper is: construct a highly constrained manifold subclass whose moduli space is parameterized by a single scale factor, so that spectral rigidity automatically holds. The key to the success of this strategy is: through strict constraints (three $S^3$ factors, ratio locking, triviality of the tangent bundle), we compress the infinite-dimensional "general manifold class" into a one-dimensional constrained subclass, thereby satisfying the dimensional necessary condition for spectral rigidity.

## 2 Group-Theoretic Origin of the Interlocking Functions

**Lemma 2.0 (Rigidity of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle Permutation Group).** Let $TM = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$ be the trifurcated tangent bundle, and $G$ its sector permutation symmetry group. If $G$ acts faithfully and transitively on the three sectors and contains all pairwise transpositions, then $G \cong S_3$, and $S_3$ is the unique finite group satisfying these conditions.

*Proof.* A faithful permutation group on three objects must be a subgroup of $S_3$. Transitivity requires orbit size 3, so $|G| \geq 3$. The transitive subgroups of $S_3$ are only $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (cyclic) and $S_3$ itself. If transpositions (odd permutations) are required, then $A_3$ is insufficient, and the only possibility is $S_3$. $\square$

**Theorem 2.0' (Rigidity of Trifurcated Tangent Bundle Sector Permutations).** Let $M = S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$ be equipped with a product metric, and suppose there exists a tangent bundle decomposition $TM = \mathcal{M}\oplus\mathcal{C}\oplus\mathcal{I}$ such that the three sectors are fully equivalent at the metric level (i.e., any permutation induces an isometric automorphism). Then:
1. The sector permutation group $G$ of this decomposition must contain all pairwise transpositions;
2. The finite group satisfying (1) is uniquely isomorphic to $S_3$.

Consequently, $\Lambda(G)=|\text{Conj}(S_3)|=3$, $k_0(G)=[S_3:A_3]=2$ are the necessary outputs of the group-theoretic functions.

*Proof.* (1) If the three sectors are metrically equivalent, then any transposition (e.g., $\mathcal{M}\leftrightarrow\mathcal{C}$) preserves the product metric and thus induces an isometric automorphism. Hence all pairwise transpositions belong to $G$.

(2) By Lemma 2.0, a transitive permutation group containing transpositions can only be $S_3$. Hence $G\cong S_3$ is not an artificial choice, but a necessary consequence of non-trivial permutations under the threefold equivalence structure. $\square$

**Definition 2.0 (Interlocking Function and Compactness Function).** Let $G$ be a finite group. Define:
- **Interlocking Function** $\Lambda(G) := |\text{Conj}(G)|$ (the number of conjugacy classes; by the fundamental theorem of representation theory, equal to the number of irreducible complex representations of $G$);
- **Compactness Function** $k_0(G) := [G : N_{\max}]$ (the index of the maximal normal subgroup).

**Proposition 2.0.** For the permutation symmetry group $G = S_3$ of the trifurcated tangent bundle $TM = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$:
$$\Lambda(S_3) = 3, \quad k_0(S_3) = 2.$$

*Proof.* The conjugacy class decomposition of $S_3$ is: identity $\{e\}$ (1), transpositions $\{(12),(13),(23)\}$ (3), 3-cycles $\{(123),(132)\}$ (2), totaling 3 conjugacy classes, hence $\Lambda(S_3)=|\text{Conj}(S_3)|=3$.

The normal subgroups of $S_3$ are only $\{e\}$ and $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (the alternating subgroup). The maximal normal subgroup is $A_3$, with index $[S_3 : A_3] = 6/3 = 2$, hence $k_0(S_3)=2$. $\square$

**Remark 2.0.** By Lemma 2.0, $S_3$ is the unique choice for the trifurcated tangent bundle permutation symmetry group. The interlocking constants $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ are not freely chosen parameters, but necessary values of the group-theoretic functions evaluated on $S_3$. If the trifurcated tangent bundle had $S_4$ permutation symmetry (a four-sector structure), then $\Lambda(S_4)=5$, $k_0(S_4)=2$, which would be a theory entirely different from Shifang Geometry. The reason Shifang Geometry locks $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$ lies in its group structure being $S_3$ (three sectors), and Lemma 2.0 has already proven the uniqueness of this group choice.

## 3 Definitions and Assumptions

### 3.1 Definition of Constrained Product Spheres

Let $a > 0$ be the overall scale factor. By Proposition 2.0, the group-theoretic function outputs of the trifurcated tangent bundle permutation group $S_3$ are:
$$\Lambda = \Lambda(S_3) = 3 \quad \text{(trisection ratio parameter)}, \quad k_0 = k_0(S_3) = 2 \quad \text{(binary compactness constant)}, \quad \ell_0 > 0 \quad \text{(scale constant, locked by the spectral unit choice theorem and ground state condition)}.$$

**Definition 3.1 (Constrained Product Sphere).** A nine-dimensional closed manifold (compact, without boundary)

$$M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3(a/\sqrt{3}) \times S^3(a/\sqrt{6})$$

equipped with the product metric $g = g_a \oplus g_{a/\sqrt{3}} \oplus g_{a/\sqrt{6}}$ is called a **Constrained Product Sphere** (CPS). Denote

$$b = \frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}, \quad c = \frac{a}{\sqrt{6}}.$$

From $k_0 = 2 > 1$, the scale ordering $a > b > c$ holds strictly ($1 > 1/\sqrt{3} > 1/\sqrt{6}$).

**Remark 3.1.** The choice of parameters $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ makes the squared radius ratios of the second and third factors satisfy $b^2/a^2 = 1/\Lambda$ and $c^2/a^2 = 1/(\Lambda k_0)$. These values are introduced as part of the class definition. **To guarantee strict ordering of the first three non-zero eigenvalues (Proposition 3.2), it is necessary that $\Lambda > 8/3$.** If $\Lambda \leq 8/3$, then the eigenvalue of the mode $(0,1,0)$ would be no greater than that of the mode $(2,0,0)$, causing the correspondence of the first three eigenvalues to change, and the spectral rigidity theorem under this definition would need to be reformulated.

**Remark 3.2 (Properties of the Interlocking Constants).** The constants $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ are computed directly from the $S_3$ group structure via Theorem 2.0' and Proposition 2.0; $\ell_0$ is locked by the self-consistent output of the Nine-Principle Mutual Arrest network. None of the three are freely chosen free parameters: $\Lambda$ and $k_0$ are the necessary values of the group-theoretic functions $|\text{Conj}(S_3)|$ and $[S_3:A_3]$; $\ell_0$ is uniquely determined by the joint constraints of the spectral separation condition, heat kernel coefficient ratio, information hierarchy nesting, and magic number truncation (see subsequent sections). As a positive real parameter, $\ell_0$ is locked by the spectral unit choice theorem and the ground state condition $S_{\min}=24$, providing a numerical anchor for the spectrum-projection bridge, but does not participate in the one-dimensional parameterization of the moduli space.

**Remark 3.3 (Triviality of the Tangent Bundle).** Since $S^3$, as the Lie group $SU(2)$, is parallelizable, its tangent bundle $TS^3$ is trivial. The tangent bundle of the product manifold is the direct sum of the tangent bundles of each factor, so $TM(a)$ is automatically trivial. Hence, triviality of the tangent bundle holds automatically in this class and need not be listed as an additional hypothesis.

### 3.2 Parameterization of the Moduli Space

**Theorem 3.1 (Parameterization of the Moduli Space).** Under the premise of the interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$ determined by Theorem 2.0' and Proposition 2.0, the moduli space of the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0, \infty)$, where $a$ is the unique continuous degree of freedom (the overall scale factor). Different $a$ correspond to different isometry classes.

**Theorem 3.1' (First Eigenvalue as a Coordinate).** Under the premise of locked interlocking constants determined by Theorem 2.0' and Proposition 2.0, the spectral map
$$\sigma_1: (0,\infty) \to (0,\infty), \quad a \mapsto \lambda_1^\Delta(a) = \frac{3}{a^2}$$
is a diffeomorphism. Therefore, the first non-zero Laplace eigenvalue $\lambda_1^\Delta$ itself constitutes a **global coordinate** for the moduli space $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ of the constrained product sphere class, and the spectral signature $(1,\ 8/3,\ \Lambda)$ is independent of $a$.

*Proof.* $\sigma_1$ is continuous, strictly monotonically decreasing, with $\lim_{a\to 0^+}\sigma_1(a)=+\infty$ and $\lim_{a\to+\infty}\sigma_1(a)=0$. Hence $\sigma_1$ is a homeomorphism $(0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)$; moreover, $\sigma_1'(a)=-6/a^3\neq 0$, so the inverse map is smooth, hence it is a diffeomorphism. $\square$

**Remark 3.1' (Status of the Definition Class).** This theorem studies the **definition class** $\{M(a)\}$ — that is, the subset of product spheres satisfying the specific ratio constraints of Definition 3.1 — not "all manifolds diffeomorphic to $S^3\times S^3\times S^3$ whose first three Laplace eigenvalues satisfy the spectral signature." The latter (more general isospectral class) may have a larger moduli space, and this paper does not claim to fully classify it.

*Proof.* By Definition 3.1, each factor metric is spherical, characterized by radii $(a,b,c)$, with the ratios of $b$ and $c$ to $a$ locked by $\Lambda$ and $k_0$. Therefore, with $(k_0, \Lambda, \ell_0)$ fixed, the unique continuous degree of freedom is the overall scale $a$. Since $S^3$ is parallelizable, $TM(a)$ is automatically trivial, and the topology is uniquely determined. Hence the moduli space is fully parameterized by $a \in (0, \infty)$, and different $a$ give different curvature radii, corresponding to different isometry classes. $\square$

**Detailed argument:** The rigor of distinguishing isometry classes can be further explained. Let $a \neq a'$, then the first factor $S^3(a)$ and $S^3(a')$ have different radii. Since the diameter of $S^3(R)$ is $\pi R$ and the volume is $2\pi^2 R^3$, isometric manifolds must have equal diameters and volumes. If $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric, their diameters and volumes must coincide. For the product metric, the diameter formula is
$$\text{diam}(M(a)) = \pi\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2} = \pi a\sqrt{1+\frac{1}{\Lambda}+\frac{1}{\Lambda k_0}},$$
where $b=a/\sqrt{\Lambda}$ and $c=a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$. Since $\Lambda$ and $k_0$ are constants locked by the interlocking constants, $\sqrt{1+1/\Lambda+1/(\Lambda k_0)}$ is a positive constant independent of $a$, so $\text{diam}(M(a))$ is a strictly monotonic function of $a$. Hence $a=a'$, and different $a$ correspond to different isometry classes.

## 4 Explicit Computation of Laplace Eigenvalues

### 4.1 Spectral Separation on Product Manifolds

**Proposition 4.1.** On the constrained product sphere $M(a)$, the eigenvalues of the Laplace–Beltrami operator $\Delta_g$ are given by

$$\lambda_{p,q,r}^\Delta = \frac{p(p+2)}{a^2} + \frac{q(q+2)}{b^2} + \frac{r(r+2)}{c^2}, \quad p,q,r \in \mathbb{N}_0.$$

where $p=q=r=0$ corresponds to the zero eigenvalue (constant function), and non-zero eigenvalues begin with $p+q+r \geq 1$.

*Proof.* On a product manifold, the Laplace operator separates as $\Delta_g = \Delta_1 + \Delta_2 + \Delta_3$, where $\Delta_i$ is the Laplace operator on each factor. It is known that on $S^3(R)$, the eigenvalue corresponding to a spherical harmonic of degree $p$ is $p(p+2)/R^2$, so the total eigenvalue is the sum of the three. $\square$

**Supplementary Remark:** The standard derivation of Laplace eigenvalues on $S^3(R)$ is as follows. Embed $S^3(R)$ into $\mathbb{R}^4$ as $\{x\in\mathbb{R}^4: |x|=R\}$. The restriction of a homogeneous harmonic polynomial of degree $p$ to $S^3(R)$ yields a spherical harmonic. By separation of the Euclidean Laplacian into radial and angular parts, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4} = \partial_r^2 + \frac{3}{r}\partial_r + \frac{1}{r^2}\Delta_{S^3}$. For a homogeneous function of degree $p$, $f(r,\theta)=r^p Y(\theta)$, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4}f=0$ yields $\Delta_{S^3}Y = -p(p+2)Y$. On $S^3(R)$, the metric scaling factor is $R^2$, so the eigenvalue is $p(p+2)/R^2$.
