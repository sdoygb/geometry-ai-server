## 2.2 Direct Sum Decomposition of the Tangent Bundle

### 2.2.1 What is a Tangent Bundle?

First, recall a basic concept. For a manifold (say, a sphere $S^3$), the **tangent bundle** $TS^3$ is obtained by attaching a tangent space $T_pS^3$ (an $\mathbb{R}^3$) at each point $p$, then collecting all these tangent spaces together.

For the product manifold $M(a) = S^3_1 \times S^3_2 \times S^3_3$, the tangent bundle has a beautiful property:

**Tripartite Tangent Bundle Decomposition (GT-1.2.0.1)**

The tangent bundle of the Constrained Product Sphere $M(a)$ can be written as the direct sum of three sub-bundles:

$$TM(a) = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I},$$

where $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$ correspond respectively to the pullbacks of the tangent bundles of the three $S^3$ factors, each fiber being $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Why $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$? These letters represent the three different roles of the sectors:
- **$\mathcal{M}$** (Matter Sector) — the $S^3(a)$ with the **largest radius** among the three spheres
- **$\mathcal{C}$** (Constraint Sector) — the intermediate radius $S^3(a/\sqrt{3})$
- **$\mathcal{I}$** (Information Sector) — the smallest radius $S^3(a/\sqrt{6})$

*Proof:* The tangent bundle of a product manifold equals the direct sum of the tangent bundles of its factors. $M(a)$ is the product of three $S^3$'s, so the conclusion follows immediately. $\square$

This decomposition possesses a very strong symmetry: the three subspaces $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$ are mathematically **completely equivalent** — any permutation of the labels does not alter the structure of the tangent bundle. This $S_3$ symmetry (the permutation group of order 6) is one of the cornerstones of the entire framework.

## 2.3 Holographic Screen: A Two-Dimensional Information Window

### 2.3.1 Existence of the Holographic Screen

In each fiber $\mathbb{R}^9 = \mathbb{R}^3_{\mathcal{M}} \oplus \mathbb{R}^3_{\mathcal{C}} \oplus \mathbb{R}^3_{\mathcal{I}}$, there exists a **two-dimensional oriented subspace** $\Sigma$, called the **Holographic Screen**. The three $\mathbb{R}^3$ sectors project onto this two-dimensional plane.

This is the core mechanism of "nine dimensions becoming two":

```
     ℝ³_ℳ ──┐
             ├──→ ℝ²_Σ （Holographic Screen）
      ℝ³_𝒞 ──┤
             │
      ℝ³_ℐ ──┘
```

Each sector loses one dimension of information (because it projects from three dimensions onto two), but the **information from all three sectors combined** is completely encoded on $\Sigma$ — provided we know the projection angles.

### 2.3.2 Projection Intensity Angles

The key question: **What is the "intensity" of each sector's projection onto the Holographic Screen?**

This is described by the **Projection Intensity Angles** $\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3$. Intuitively:

- If $\theta_i = 90^\circ$, sector $i$ projects completely onto $\Sigma$ (orthogonal projection, zero information loss)
- If $\theta_i = 0^\circ$, sector $i$ does not project onto $\Sigma$ at all (parallel to the screen, information totally lost)
- For intermediate values, the projected area is proportional to $\sin\theta_i$

**Projection Area Element Ratios (GT-1.2.0.2)**

Let $P_{\mathcal{M}}, P_{\mathcal{C}}, P_{\mathcal{I}}$ be the linear projection maps from the three sectors onto the Holographic Screen $\Sigma$. Then the single-sector projection area elements satisfy:

$$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}}(U_{\mathcal{M}})) = \sin^2\theta_1 \cdot dA,$$
$$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{C}}(U_{\mathcal{C}})) = \sin^2\theta_2 \cdot dA,$$
$$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{I}}(U_{\mathcal{I}})) = \sin^2\theta_3 \cdot dA.$$

The area element for a two-sector joint projection is:

$$\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}} \times P_{\mathcal{C}}) = \sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2 \cdot dA$$

(the other two combinations are analogous).

*Explanation:* The square $\sin^2\theta_i$ comes from "project once, take area element" — when a three-dimensional volume element projects onto a two-dimensional plane, the area scaling factor is the sine of the projection angle.

## 2.4 Holographic Screen Encoding Condition

### 2.4.1 Why Exactly $90^\circ$?

The three Projection Intensity Angles $\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3$ are not free parameters. They satisfy a simple yet profound linear constraint:

**Holographic Screen Encoding Condition (GT-1.2.0.3)**

$$\theta_1 + \theta_2 + \theta_3 = 90^\circ, \qquad \theta_i \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ).$$

Why $90^\circ$? It can be understood through a geometric argument.

Consider the three angles $\theta_i$ in the range $[0^\circ, 90^\circ]$ and their sum $\Sigma = \theta_1 + \theta_2 + \theta_3$. If $\Sigma < 90^\circ$, the three sectors' projection directions are "too parallel," and the independent information of the three sectors cannot be distinguished on the Holographic Screen. If $\Sigma > 90^\circ$, the projection directions are "too divergent," and the information capacity of the Holographic Screen is insufficient to accommodate all the sectors' information.

$90^\circ$ is the "just right" value. More precisely, when $\Sigma = 90^\circ$, the dimension of the orthogonal complement space of the three sectors' projection directions in $\mathbb{R}^9$ is exactly 6, and the sum of the ranks of the three $\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ projections is 6, achieving "non-redundant, lossless" encoding.

## 2.5 Kähler Structure of the Holographic Screen

The Holographic Screen $\Sigma$ is not an arbitrary two-dimensional plane — it has a beautiful geometric structure: **$\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1 \cong S^2$**, equipped with the standard Fubini-Study metric and possessing a Kähler structure.

**Kähler Structure of the Holographic Screen (GT-1.2.0.4)**

1. **Complex structure $J$**: the standard complex structure of $\mathbb{C}P^1$;

**Why must it be a Kähler structure?** Because the Holographic Screen is the receiving end of the Tripartite Tangent Bundle "projection." The Kähler structure ensures that: (1) the complex structure $J$ allows $\mathbb{R}^2$ to be regarded as $\mathbb{C}$ (algebraic closure); (2) the symplectic form $\omega$ defines the area element (information capacity); (3) the three are self-consistently related via $g = \omega(\cdot, J\cdot)$.

## 2.7 Tangent Bundle Square Root Decomposition and Magic Numbers

### 2.7.1 Question: Why is the Degeneracy $2(k+1)^2$?

Before entering the holomorphic structure, we need to understand why the holomorphic line bundles on the Holographic Screen $\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$ have a specific dimension formula.

The dimension of the space of global holomorphic sections of the $k$-th tensor power line bundle $\mathcal{O}(k)$ on $\mathbb{C}P^1$ is $k+1$. But the degeneracy appearing in Geometric Theory is $2(k+1)^2$ — an extra factor of 2 and a square. This "excess" structure comes from the **Square Root Decomposition** induced by the Tripartite Tangent Bundle.

### 2.7.2 Square Root of the Holomorphic Tangent Bundle

**Tangent Bundle Square Root (GT-1.2.0.7)**

Let $T^{1,0}\Sigma$ be the holomorphic tangent bundle of $\Sigma$. When $\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$, $T^{1,0}\Sigma \cong \mathcal{O}(2)$. Its **square root** is:

$$K^{-1/2} \cong \mathcal{O}(1),$$

where $K$ is the canonical bundle.

The existence of this square root is not accidental — it corresponds to the existence of spin structures (on $\mathbb{C}P^1$ there are exactly two spin structures, corresponding to the two square roots of $\mathcal{O}(1)$).

### 2.7.3 Magic Number Theorem

**Magic Number Theorem (GT-1.2.0.8)**

On the Holographic Screen $\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$, the dimension of the holomorphic section space induced by the Tripartite Tangent Bundle is:

$$N(k) = 2(k+1)^2,$$

where $k = 0,1,2,\dots$ is a non-negative integer.

*Derivation outline:* Each sector of the Tripartite Tangent Bundle contributes an $\mathcal{O}(k)$ factor (from the linearization of the sector's projection onto the Holographic Screen). The three sectors give a direct sum → three-dimensional tensor product → the square comes from two independent directions (complex structure and real structure). $$137 - 124 = 13, \quad 124 - 98 = 26 = 2 \times 13.$$
