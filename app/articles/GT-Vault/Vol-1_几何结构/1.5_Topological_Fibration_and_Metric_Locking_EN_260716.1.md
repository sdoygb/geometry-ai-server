## §1.5.2 The Three-Dimensional Restriction: Why Work Only on One $S^3$?

Recall the [Tripartite Tangent Bundle Decomposition (GT-1.2.0.1)](./1.2_三分切丛与全息屏_CN_260713.1.md): on the constrained product sphere $M(a)$, the tangent bundle decomposes into three rank-3 sub-bundles:

$$TM(a) = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}, \quad \dim(M(a)) = 9$$

But we will now restrict attention to the $S^3$ factor $S^3_{\mathcal{M}} = S^3(a)$ of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector. Why?

**Because the fibration is an intrinsic property of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector** — the geometry of the $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{I}$ sectors will couple in through the normal bundle (the task of Chapter 1.6), but the topological structure of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector itself can be studied independently.

On $S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$, the tangent bundle decomposition becomes:

$$\mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I} = TS^3_{\mathcal{M}}, \quad \dim(\mathcal{M}) = \dim(\mathcal{C}) = \dim(\mathcal{I}) = 1.$$

**Note:** The three-dimensional decomposition on $S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ here, and the nine-dimensional decomposition on the nine-dimensional $M(a)$, are objects at different levels. They are connected via the nine-dimensional embedding, but must not be conflated.

## §1.5.3 Rank-1 Automatic Integrability

The first key observation: under the three-dimensional restriction, **$\mathcal{M}$ is a rank-1 sub-bundle**. This means its integrability is automatic.

**Rank-1 Automatic Frobenius Integrability (GT-1.5.0.1)**

Let $\mathcal{M} \subset TS^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ be a rank-1 sub-bundle (line bundle), locally spanned by a nowhere-vanishing vector field $X$. Then $\mathcal{M}$ is automatically Frobenius integrable — i.e., there exists an integral manifold (a curve) passing through every point.

**Proof:** This is a standard result in differential topology. For any smooth function $f$, $[X, fX] = (X \cdot f)X \in \mathcal{M}$, so $[\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{M}] \subset \mathcal{M}$ holds automatically. The Frobenius theorem therefore applies. $\square$

**Intuition:** A one-dimensional distribution is like a "direction field" — you can always walk out a curve along this direction. It is like following a stream down a mountain: the direction is given, you just follow it, and a path always emerges.

## §1.5.4 Left Invariance and the Leaf Space $S^2$

$S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ is an $SU(2)$ group manifold, with a natural left-multiplication action.

**Left Invariance of $\mathcal{M}$ (GT-1.5.0.2)**

$\mathcal{M} \subset TS^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ is invariant under the left-multiplication action of $SU(2)$. That is, for any $g \in SU(2)$ and $p \in S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$, the pushforward $(L_g)_*$ induced by left translation $L_g: S^3_{\mathcal{M}} \to S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ satisfies:

$$(L_g)_*(\mathcal{M}_p) = \mathcal{M}_{gp}.$$

**Proof:** $\mathcal{M}$ is a rank-1 sub-bundle of the tangent bundle of $S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$, originating from the first factor of the [Tripartite Tangent Bundle Decomposition (GT-1.2.0.1)](./1.2_三分切丛与全息屏_CN_260713.1.md), and the left-multiplication action of $S^3$ naturally preserves this decomposition. $\square$

**Integral curves are $U(1)$ orbits:** Left invariance means that the direction of $\mathcal{M}$ at each point is given by a left-invariant vector field of $SU(2)$. The integral curves of this vector field are precisely the orbits of $U(1)$ ($S^1$) on $S^3$.

**Leaf Space $\cong S^2$ (GT-1.5.0.3)**

The integral manifolds of $\mathcal{M}$ form a foliation of $S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$. The leaf space (the quotient space of leaves) is homeomorphic to $S^2$:

$$\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{M} \cong S^2.$$

**Proof:** $S^3_{\mathcal{M}}$ is foliated by $U(1)$ orbits. Each $U(1)$ orbit is an $S^1$, and the quotient space $S^3/U(1) \cong S^2$ is the classical Hopf fibration. $\square$

## §1.5.5 Hopf Fibration: From $S^3$ to $S^2$

### §1.5.5.1 What Is the Hopf Fibration?

The Hopf fibration is the fiber bundle $S^3 \to S^2$ with fiber $S^1$. It is the first example of a nontrivial fibration, and the geometric realization of the $SU(2)$ principal bundle $S^3 \to S^2$.

Explicit formula for the Hopf map $h: S^3 \to S^2$ (viewing $S^3$ as the unit sphere in $\mathbb{C}^2$):

$$h(z_1, z_2) = (2z_1\bar{z}_2, |z_1|^2 - |z_2|^2) \in S^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^3.$$

### §1.5.5.2 Euler Class Locking

**Euler Class $= \pm 1$ (GT-1.5.0.4)**

The Euler class of the Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^3 \to S^2$ is $\pm 1 \in H^2(S^2; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. This is equivalent to saying: this $S^1$ bundle is the unit circle bundle of the holomorphic line bundle $\mathcal{O}(-1)$ (or $\mathcal{O}(1)$).

**Proof:** Standard topological result. $\square$

**Topological Hopf Fibration (GT-1.5.0.5)**

For any $\theta_M \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$ of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector, the fibration induced by $\mathcal{M}$ is topologically equivalent to the Hopf fibration $S^3 \to S^2$.

**Proof:** By [Left Invariance (GT-1.5.0.2)](#GT-1.5.0.2) and [Leaf Space $\cong S^2$ (GT-1.5.0.3)](#GT-1.5.0.3), the leaf space is $S^2$ and the fiber is $S^1$. There are only two $S^1$ bundles over $S^3$ (distinguished by Euler class $\pm 1$), both of which are Hopf fibrations. $\square$

## §1.5.6 Metric Hopf Equivalence: $r_{\mathcal{M}} = r_\Sigma$

### §1.5.6.1 Two Constraint Lines

The locking of the metric comes from the intersection of two constraint lines:

1. **Spectral constraint line:** $\lambda_1 = 3/r_{\mathcal{M}}^2$ (from the [Inverse Solution Formula (GT-1.1.0.8)](./1.1_听音辨形：约束乘积球面的谱刚性_CN_260713.1.md))
2. **Geometric constraint line:** the ratio relation between $r_{\mathcal{M}}$ and the radius $r_\Sigma$ of the holographic screen $\Sigma$

### §1.5.6.2 Double Constraint Cross-Locking

**Spinor Ratio Locking (GT-1.5.0.6)**

In the Hopf fibration $S^3 \to S^2$, the spinor structure yields the ratio relation between $r_{\mathcal{M}}$ and the Hopf quotient space radius $r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}$:

$$\frac{r_{\mathcal{M}}}{r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}} = 2.$$

**Proof:** The Hopf fibration is a Riemannian submersion. In the standard Hopf fibration, if $S^3$ has radius 1, then the quotient space $S^3/S^1$ viewed as $S^2$ has an induced radius of $1/2$ under the Fubini-Study metric. Hence $r_{\mathcal{M}} / r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}} = 2$. This is a standard geometric fact of the Hopf fibration. $\square$

> **⚠️ Important Distinction:** Here $r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}$ is the **induced metric radius** of the Hopf quotient space (naturally inherited from the Riemannian submersion), which is a different quantity from the radius $r_\Sigma$ of the holographic screen $\Sigma$ in §1.5.6.2. The relation between the two requires an additional normalization condition — this is precisely what the next subsection addresses.

**Metric Locking Correspondence Rule (GT-1.5.0.7)**

$$r_{\mathcal{M}} = r_\Sigma.$$

**⚠️ Positioning Note (260713.1 amendment):** Earlier versions claimed that "the spectral constraint $\lambda_1=3/r_{\mathcal{M}}^2$ together with the spinor ratio $r_{\mathcal{M}}/r_\Sigma=2$ jointly lock $r_{\mathcal{M}}=r_\Sigma$" — this derivation is not mathematically valid. From $r_{\mathcal{M}}/r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}=2$ (GT-1.5.0.6) one only obtains $r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}=r_{\mathcal{M}}/2$, which does not directly yield $r_{\mathcal{M}}=r_\Sigma$. The missing link is: **the relation between the holographic screen $\Sigma$ radius $r_\Sigma$ and the Hopf quotient space radius $r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}$**.

**Current treatment:** $r_{\mathcal{M}} = r_\Sigma$ is proposed in this volume as a **constructive correspondence rule** (analogous to Vol-0's GT-0.2.0.9 "Concentric Sphere Realization Rule" and GT-0.2.0.8 "Area-Weight Correspondence"), motivated by:

1. **Dimensional consistency:** The $S^3$ of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector is the "source manifold" and the holographic screen $\Sigma$ is the "information-bearing surface" — it is a natural constructive choice that the two share the same characteristic scale within the geometric theory framework.
2. **Spectral self-consistency:** If $r_\Sigma \neq r_{\mathcal{M}}$, an independent scale-ratio parameter must be introduced. Taking $r_\Sigma = r_{\mathcal{M}}$ follows the principle of minimal free parameters.
3. **Subsequent verification:** The correctness of this correspondence rule is tested by the agreement of physical predictions (electron mass, coupling constants, etc.) in later volumes with experiment. If predictions disagree with experiment, the rule must be revised.

> **Open Problem:** The precise mapping relation between $r_\Sigma$ and $r_{S^2}^{\text{Hopf}}$ (i.e., the metric normalization condition of the holographic screen) needs to be rigorously derived from the holographic screen construction (Chapter 1.2) and the normal bundle geometry (Chapter 1.6). This derivation lies beyond the scope of Volume 1 and is left for future supplementation.

### §1.5.6.3 Geometric Meaning of This Correspondence Rule

If $r_{\mathcal{M}} = r_\Sigma$ holds, it means: **the scale of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector equals the scale of the holographic screen $\Sigma$.**

From the Hopf fibration perspective: the $S^3$ of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector projects via the Hopf map to the quotient space $S^2$ (radius $= r_{\mathcal{M}}/2$). The holographic screen $\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$ is topologically homeomorphic to this quotient space. $r_{\mathcal{M}} = r_\Sigma$ amounts to requiring that the metric normalization of the holographic screen matches the metric of the source manifold of the $\mathcal{M}$ sector — i.e., the effective information-bearing radius of $\Sigma$ is not the induced radius $r_{\mathcal{M}}/2$ of the quotient space, but $r_{\mathcal{M}}$ itself, the same as $\mathcal{M}$. The physical basis for this choice is: **the holographic screen carries not merely the information of the quotient space, but the information of the entire $S^3$ fibration**.

> **Note:** The above argument is heuristic. A rigorous derivation requires completion in the holographic screen construction (Chapter 1.2) and the normal bundle geometry (Chapter 1.6).
