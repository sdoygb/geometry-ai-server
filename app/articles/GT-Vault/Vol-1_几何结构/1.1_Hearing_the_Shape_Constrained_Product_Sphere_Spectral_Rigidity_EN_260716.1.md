## 1.1 Introduction: An Ancient Question

In 1966, Mark Kac posed a seemingly simple question in a famous paper:

**"Can one hear the shape of a drum?"**

Meaning: if you only know all the pitches (i.e., the eigenvalues of the Laplace operator) produced by a drum, can you uniquely determine the shape of its membrane?

Mathematically, this question is equivalent to: **Does the Laplace spectrum uniquely determine the isometry class of a Riemannian manifold?**

### 1.1.1 Why This Question Matters

This is not a mere puzzle. The eigenvalues of the Laplace operator correspond to the **resonant frequencies** of physical systems — from drumhead vibrations to the hydrogen atom spectrum, to the temperature fluctuations of the cosmic microwave background — all are governed by the same mathematical structure. If the spectrum could uniquely determine the geometry, it would mean "measure the spectrum and you know the shape": as long as you measure the frequencies accurately, you can reverse-engineer the geometric structure that produced them.

But reality is counterintuitive: **in general, you cannot.**

### 1.1.2 The Shock of Counterexamples

In 1992, Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert constructed the first pair of **isospectral but non-isometric** planar domains — two drums of different shapes that nevertheless produce exactly the same pitches. Since then, the "hearing the shape" problem has been proven to have no affirmative answer in the general case.

But wait — "in general" is not the same as "in all cases."

This paper studies a **highly constrained** special class of manifolds: the product of three spheres, with their radii locked in fixed proportions by group theory. Under this constraint, spectral rigidity unexpectedly holds. Moreover, only the **first three eigenvalues** suffice to uniquely determine the geometry.

This result is not a matter of luck, but has a deep mathematical reason: **the dimension of the moduli space is sufficiently low.**

## 1.2 Preliminaries: Starting from a Single Sphere

Before entering nine dimensions, let us first understand the one-sphere case.

### 1.2.1 The Spectrum on a Single $S^3$

$S^3(R)$ is the three-dimensional sphere of radius $R$. As a compact manifold, the spectrum of its Laplace–Beltrami operator is completely known.

**Fact 1.2.1 (Laplace Spectrum of $S^3(R)$)** Let $S^3(R)$ be equipped with the standard metric. The eigenvalues of its Laplace operator are

$$\lambda_p = \frac{p(p+2)}{R^2}, \qquad p = 0, 1, 2, \dots$$

where $p=0$ corresponds to constant functions (zero eigenvalue), and $p=1$ gives the first nonzero eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 3/R^2$.

**Why this formula?** The derivation proceeds as follows. Embed $S^3(R)$ into $\mathbb{R}^4$ as $\{x \in \mathbb{R}^4 : |x| = R\}$. Homogeneous harmonic polynomials of degree $p$ restricted to the sphere yield spherical harmonics. Separate the Laplacian of $\mathbb{R}^4$ into radial and angular parts:

$$\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4} = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2} + \frac{3}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r^2}\Delta_{S^3}.$$

For a $p$-th degree homogeneous function $f(r,\theta) = r^p Y(\theta)$, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4}f = 0$ gives $\Delta_{S^3}Y = -p(p+2)Y$. Scaling to radius $R$, the eigenvalues are $p(p+2)/R^2$.

**Key observation:** If we only know $\lambda_1 = 3/R^2$, can we uniquely determine $R$? Yes:

$$R = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_1}}.$$

One eigenvalue → one radius. One-to-one correspondence, unambiguous.

### 1.2.2 The Product of Three Spheres

Now consider the product manifold of three $S^3$:

$$M = S^3(a) \times S^3(b) \times S^3(c),$$

equipped with the product metric $g = g_a \oplus g_b \oplus g_c$. What is its Laplace spectrum?

**Fact 1.2.2 (Spectral Separation on Product Manifolds)** On a product manifold, the Laplace operator separates into the sum of the operators on each factor:

$$\Delta_g = \Delta_1 + \Delta_2 + \Delta_3,$$

where $\Delta_i$ acts on the $i$-th factor. Hence the eigenvalues are sums of the eigenvalues from each factor:

$$\lambda_{p,q,r} = \frac{p(p+2)}{a^2} + \frac{q(q+2)}{b^2} + \frac{r(r+2)}{c^2}, \qquad p,q,r \in \mathbb{N}_0.$$

$p=q=r=0$ corresponds to the zero eigenvalue (constant functions); nonzero eigenvalues begin at $p+q+r \geq 1$.

Now the question arises: how many eigenvalues are needed to uniquely determine the three radii $(a,b,c)$?

If we only know the **first two** nonzero eigenvalues ($3/a^2$ and $3/b^2$, respectively), we can solve for $a$ and $b$, but $c$ remains undetermined. **Two eigenvalues are not enough to determine three geometric parameters.**

This is the concrete manifestation of Kac's problem on product spheres: the spectral information is insufficient.

## 1.3 $S_3$ Group Theory: Reducing Three Parameters to One

Now we introduce the most crucial input of this paper: **the permutation symmetry of the tripartite tangent bundle**.

### 1.3.1 The Inevitability of the Three-Sector Structure

The fundamental setting of Geometric Theory is that the nine-dimensional tangent bundle $TM$ decomposes into the direct sum of three sectors:

$$TM = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}.$$

These three sectors are **completely equivalent** at the metric level — i.e., any permutation of sector labels induces an isometric automorphism.

**Uniqueness of the Permutation Group (GT-1.1.0.1)** {#GT-1.1.0.1}

Let $G$ be the sector permutation symmetry group of the tripartite tangent bundle. If $G$ acts faithfully and transitively on the three sectors, and contains pairwise transpositions of any two sectors, then $G \cong S_3$, and $S_3$ is the unique finite group satisfying these conditions.

*Proof*: A faithful permutation group on three objects must be a subgroup of $S_3$. Transitivity requires orbit size 3, hence $|G| \geq 3$. The transitive subgroups of $S_3$ are only $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (the cyclic group) and $S_3$ itself. If transpositions (odd permutations) are required, $A_3$ is insufficient, leaving $S_3$ as the unique possibility. ∎

Thus $S_3$ is the unique choice — not an assumption, but an inevitable consequence of "three-sector equivalence."

### 1.3.2 Two Key Numbers: $\Lambda = 3$, $k_0 = 2$

As a group, $S_3$ possesses two group-theoretic invariants directly relevant to spectral rigidity:

**Interlocking Function and Compactness Function (GT-1.1.0.2)** {#GT-1.1.0.2}

Let $G$ be a finite group. Define:
- **Interlocking Function** $\Lambda(G) := |\text{Conj}(G)|$ — the number of conjugacy classes of $G$ (equal to the number of irreducible complex representations);
- **Compactness Function** $k_0(G) := [G : N_{\max}]$ — the index of $G$ in its maximal normal subgroup.

**Group-Theoretic Functions of $S_3$ (GT-1.1.0.3)** {#GT-1.1.0.3}

For $G = S_3$:

$$\Lambda(S_3) = 3, \qquad k_0(S_3) = 2.$$

*Proof*: The conjugacy class decomposition of $S_3$ is: identity $\{e\}$ (1 element), transpositions $\{(12),(13),(23)\}$ (3 elements), 3-cycles $\{(123),(132)\}$ (2 elements), for a total of 3 conjugacy classes, hence $\Lambda(S_3) = 3$.

The normal subgroups of $S_3$ are only $\{e\}$ and $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$. The maximal normal subgroup is $A_3$, with index $[S_3 : A_3] = 6/3 = 2$, hence $k_0(S_3) = 2$. ∎

These two numbers $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ are not free parameters. If the tripartite tangent bundle were a four-sector structure (permutation group $S_4$), then $\Lambda(S_4)=5$ and $k_0(S_4)=2$ — which would be an entirely different theory.

### 1.3.3 From Group-Theoretic Weights to Geometric Proportions

$S_3$ possesses a normal subgroup chain:

$$\{e\} \lhd A_3 \lhd S_3.$$

The orders of the three layers are $|\{e\}| = 1$, $|A_3| = 3$, $|S_3| = 6$. Taking the reciprocals of the orders and normalizing:

$$\frac{1}{1} : \frac{1}{3} : \frac{1}{6} = 6 : 2 : 1.$$

This **6:2:1 weight** will determine the radius ratios of the three sphere factors. The specific rule: if a given layer is governed by residual symmetry $H$, then its characteristic length squared is proportional to $|H|^{-1}$ (equivalently, proportional to the weight $w_H = [S_3:H]$).

Taking the reciprocal square root (since characteristic length $\propto 1/\sqrt{\text{weight}}$), we obtain the radius ratios:

$$R_1 : R_2 : R_3 = 1 : \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} : \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}.$$

The radius ratios of the three spheres are locked.

## 1.4 Constrained Product Sphere (CPS)

We can now state the central definition of this chapter.

### 1.4.1 Definition

**Constrained Product Sphere (GT-1.1.0.4)** {#GT-1.1.0.4}

Let $a > 0$ be a global scale factor. The nine-dimensional closed manifold

$$M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{6}}\right)$$

equipped with the product metric $g = g_a \oplus g_{a/\sqrt{3}} \oplus g_{a/\sqrt{6}}$ is called a **Constrained Product Sphere** (CPS).

Write $b = a/\sqrt{3}$, $c = a/\sqrt{6}$. Then $a > b > c$ holds strictly.

**Origin of the locked proportions:** The ratios of the three factor radii $1 : 1/\sqrt{3} : 1/\sqrt{6}$ derive from the $S_3$ group-theoretic weights $6:2:1$. The parameters $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ are necessary outputs of $S_3$ group-theoretic functions, not arbitrary choices.

The parameter relations can be expressed compactly as:

$$b^2 = \frac{a^2}{\Lambda},\qquad c^2 = \frac{a^2}{\Lambda k_0}.$$

### 1.4.2 Moduli Space: One-Dimensional

The entire CPS class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ has only one free parameter: the global scale $a$. All geometric quantities (curvature, volume, spectrum) vary with $a$, but the proportional relations remain invariant.

**Parametrization of the Moduli Space (GT-1.1.0.5)** {#GT-1.1.0.5}

The moduli space of the CPS class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0, \infty)$, where $a$ is the unique continuous degree of freedom. Different $a$ correspond to distinct isometry classes.

*Proof*: Suppose $a \neq a'$. If $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ were isometric, their diameters and volumes would have to be equal. For the product metric:

$$\text{diam}(M(a)) = \pi\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2} = \pi a \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{\Lambda} + \frac{1}{\Lambda k_0}},$$

where $\sqrt{1 + 1/\Lambda + 1/(\Lambda k_0)}$ is a positive constant independent of $a$. Hence the diameter is a strictly monotonic function of $a$, so $a \neq a'$ yields distinct isometry classes. ∎

**Key insight**: The moduli space is **one-dimensional**. This means that only **one eigenvalue** is required to determine $a$. The second and third among the first three eigenvalues serve as consistency checks — they are not necessary for determining the geometry, but they provide a criterion for verifying "does this manifold indeed belong to the CPS class."

## 1.5 Explicit Computation of Laplace Eigenvalues

### 1.5.1 The First Three Eigenvalues

Using the spectral separation formula and the known eigenvalues of $S^3(R)$, we can compute directly.

**First Three Laplace Eigenvalues of CPS (GT-1.1.0.6)** {#GT-1.1.0.6}

For $M(a)$, the first three nonzero eigenvalues are:

| Mode $(p,q,r)$ | Eigenvalue Expression | Ratio to $\lambda_1$ |
|:---:|:---|:---:|
| $(1,0,0)$ | $3/a^2$ | $1$ |
| $(2,0,0)$ | $8/a^2$ | $8/3 \approx 2.667$ |
| $(0,1,0)$ | $3/b^2 = 3\Lambda/a^2$ | $\Lambda = 3$ |

*Proof*: From the spectral separation formula (Fact 1.2.2), the eigenvalues for each mode are:

- $(1,0,0)$: $1(1+2)/a^2 = 3/a^2$.
- $(2,0,0)$: $2(2+2)/a^2 = 8/a^2$.
- $(0,1,0)$: $1(1+2)/b^2 = 3/b^2 = 3\Lambda/a^2$.

We must verify that these three are indeed the **first three** nonzero eigenvalues. Candidate competing modes include $(0,0,1)$ (yielding $3/c^2 = 3\Lambda k_0/a^2 = 18/a^2$) and $(1,1,0)$ (yielding $3/a^2 + 3/b^2 = 3(\Lambda+1)/a^2 = 12/a^2$). Since $\Lambda = 3 > 8/3$, we have $3\Lambda/a^2 = 9/a^2 > 8/a^2$; and $k_0 = 2$, so $3\Lambda k_0/a^2 = 18/a^2$ and $3(\Lambda+1)/a^2 = 12/a^2$ are both greater than $9/a^2$. Hence the first three nonzero eigenvalues are strictly as tabulated above. ∎

### 1.5.2 Spectral Signature

**Spectral Signature (GT-1.1.0.7)** {#GT-1.1.0.7}

The ratios

$$\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1} = \frac{8}{3}, \qquad \frac{\lambda_3}{\lambda_1} = \Lambda = 3$$

are universal constants independent of $a$, called the **spectral signature** of the CPS class.

If you measure the Laplace spectrum of a manifold and find that the ratios of the first two nonzero eigenvalues are not $8/3$, then it is definitely **not** a CPS. The spectral signature is like an identity card — it is not "because this manifold exists, the ratios are certain numbers," but rather "because the ratios are these numbers, this manifold belongs to the CPS class."

### 1.5.3 Inverse Solution: From Spectrum to Geometry

**Inverse Solution Formula (GT-1.1.0.8)** {#GT-1.1.0.8}

Given the first three nonzero Laplace eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3$ of $M(a)$, the scale factors are uniquely determined:

$$a = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_1}}, \qquad b = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_3}}, \qquad c = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\Lambda k_0 \lambda_1}}.$$

**Self-Consistency Condition**: A triple of positive numbers $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ constitutes the first three eigenvalues of some CPS if and only if

$$\lambda_2 = \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda_3 = 3\lambda_1.$$

*Proof*: From $\lambda_1 = 3/a^2$ we directly solve $a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1}$. From $\lambda_3 = 3/b^2$ we solve $b = \sqrt{3/\lambda_3}$. And from $b^2 = a^2/\Lambda$, we have $\lambda_3 = 3\Lambda/a^2 = \Lambda \lambda_1$, hence $\lambda_3/\lambda_1 = \Lambda = 3$. Similarly $\lambda_2/\lambda_1 = 8/3$. ∎

## 1.6 The Spectral Rigidity Theorem

With all the groundwork laid, the central theorem emerges naturally.

### 1.6.1 Statement of the Theorem

**Spectral Rigidity of CPS (GT-1.1.0.9)** {#GT-1.1.0.9}

Let $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ be two Constrained Product Spheres. If their Laplace operators share the same **first three nonzero eigenvalues**, then $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric.

### 1.6.2 Proof

*Proof* proceeds in four steps.

**Step 1: The moduli space is uniquely parametrized by $a$.** By [Parametrization of the Moduli Space (GT-1.1.0.5)](#GT-1.1.0.5), the moduli space of the CPS class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ is $a \in (0,\infty)$. Different $a$ correspond to distinct isometry classes.

**Step 2: The first three eigenvalues determine $a$.** By the [Inverse Solution Formula (GT-1.1.0.8)](#GT-1.1.0.8), $\lambda_1 = 3/a^2$ uniquely determines $a$. The second and third eigenvalues provide a self-consistency check: if $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ have the same first three eigenvalues, then $a = a'$.

**Step 3: Topological uniqueness.** All CPS are diffeomorphic to $S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$, independent of $a$.

**Step 4: Isometry.** $a = a'$ implies identical radii $(a,b,c)$, hence identical product metric, and therefore $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric. ∎

### 1.6.3 Why Three Eigenvalues?

Note: in Step 2, only the **first eigenvalue** $\lambda_1$ is actually required to determine $a$ (since $a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1}$). So why do we still refer to "the first three"?

There are two reasons:

1. **Self-consistency check**: $\lambda_2$ and $\lambda_3$ provide a criterion for verifying "is this manifold indeed a CPS?" If $\lambda_2/\lambda_1 \neq 8/3$, then it does not belong to this manifold class at all — the premise of the spectral rigidity theorem does not hold.

2. **Moduli space dimension argument**: The moduli space dimension of the CPS class is $d=1$, and the necessary condition for spectral rigidity requires using at least $k \geq 1$ eigenvalues. Here we use $k=3$, far exceeding necessity, and hence more robust.

## 1.7 Dimension Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity

### 1.7.1 Why Does Spectral Rigidity Fail in General?

The isospectral non-isometric counterexample of Gordon–Webb–Wolpert reveals a brutal truth: **in general, even infinitely many eigenvalues are insufficient to determine the geometry.**

Why? Because the moduli space of a general class of manifolds is **infinite-dimensional**. The spectral map

$$\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k, \quad [g] \mapsto (\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k)$$

is a map from an infinite-dimensional space to a $k$-dimensional space — it cannot possibly be injective. This is the fundamental reason why spectral rigidity fails.

### 1.7.2 The Dimension Obstacle Theorem

**Dimension Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity (GT-1.1.0.10)** {#GT-1.1.0.10}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a $d$-dimensional moduli space (i.e., the manifold class is described by $d$ continuous parameters). If spectral rigidity (the first $k$ eigenvalues determine the isometry class) holds on $\mathcal{M}$, then necessarily $k \geq d$. When $k < d$, spectral rigidity inevitably fails.

*Proof*: The spectral map $\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k$ is a continuous map. If $\sigma_k$ is injective (i.e., spectral rigidity holds), then $\mathcal{M}$ can be embedded into $\mathbb{R}^k$, so $\dim \mathcal{M} = d \leq k$. If $d > k$, the continuous map $\sigma_k$ cannot be injective, and spectral rigidity cannot hold. ∎

This theorem provides a **purely topological obstacle** to spectral rigidity, completely independent of the specific geometry of the manifolds — it depends only on the dimension of the moduli space.

### 1.7.3 Application to CPS

Applying the dimension necessary condition to the CPS class: $d = 1$ (since the moduli space is parametrized by the single scale parameter $a$), spectral rigidity requires $k \geq 1$. We use $k = 3 \geq 1$, so the dimension obstacle does **not** prevent spectral rigidity from holding.

Indeed, $d=1$ is the **most favorable scenario** for spectral rigidity to hold — a single eigenvalue suffices to determine the entire isometry class. This is the deep reason why the CPS class can satisfy spectral rigidity.

### 1.7.4 Compatibility with Classical Counterexamples

**Inapplicability of Counterexamples (GT-1.1.0.11)** {#GT-1.1.0.11}

Sunada-type isospectral non-isometric constructions do not apply to the CPS class. The fundamental group $\pi_1(M(a)) = 0$ (trivial), and the moduli space dimension $d=1$ satisfies the dimension necessary condition $d \leq k$. Hence the counterexamples of Gordon–Webb–Wolpert and Sunada cannot be applied to the CPS class.

*Proof*: $S^3$ is simply connected, hence $\pi_1(M(a)) = \pi_1(S^3) \times \pi_1(S^3) \times \pi_1(S^3) = 0$. The moduli space dimension of the CPS class is $d=1$, and we use $k=3 \geq 1$ eigenvalues, satisfying the dimension necessary condition. Therefore all known isospectral non-isometric constructions are inapplicable. ∎

### 1.7.5 The Core Lesson of This Section

The spectral rigidity theorem tells us three things:

1. **More constraints, more spectral rigidity** — compress the moduli space from infinite-dimensional to one-dimensional, and spectral rigidity transitions from false to true.
2. **The magic of $S_3$ group theory** — it not only provides the spectral weight proportions, but also, via $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$, compresses the moduli space of a nine-dimensional manifold to one dimension.
3. **Spectral rigidity is not automatic** — it depends on specific geometric structure (the Constrained Product Sphere).
