$$S(\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I) = \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_M} + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_C} + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_I} + \frac{1}{\sin\theta_M\sin\theta_C} + \frac{1}{\sin\theta_M\sin\theta_I} + \frac{1}{\sin\theta_C\sin\theta_I},$$

We know the global minimum is at $(30^\circ, 30^\circ, 30^\circ)$ with $S_{\min} = 24$. But the fine-structure constant $S_e \approx 137$ is not at $30^\circ$ — where is it?

### 1.4.2.1 Uniqueness Condition

**Bare Reference Point (GT-1.4.0.1)**

The bare reference point is uniquely determined by the following three conditions:
1. **Holographic Screen Encoding Condition**: $\theta_M + \theta_C + \theta_I = 90^\circ$ ([Holographic Screen Encoding Condition (GT-1.2.0.3)](./1.2_三分切丛与全息屏_CN_260713.1.md#GT-1.2.0.3))
2. **Single Core Mapping Identification**: the configuration belongs to the $\mathcal{M}\text{-}\mathcal{C}$ waist-edge coupling phase (corresponding to the electromagnetic interaction)
3. **Symmetry Condition**: $w_{MC} = w_{CI}$ (same-type matrix condition, originating from the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ reflection subgroup of $S_3$)

**Remarks on Condition 3 (supplemented 260713.1):**

$w_{MC}$ and $w_{CI}$ are elements of the cross-term coefficient matrix of the Six-Term Cost Function. In the Six-Term Cost Function the cross terms are $1/(\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j)$. In the $\mathcal{M}\text{-}\mathcal{C}$ waist-edge coupling phase, $M$ is identified as the core sector, and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ reflection subgroup of $S_3$ (the symmetry operation that keeps the core unchanged) requires the two sectors coupled to the core — $C$ and $I$ — to occupy symmetric positions. This condition yields $w_{MC}=w_{CI}$ — i.e. the $M$-$C$ cross term and the $C$-$I$ cross term have equal coefficients at the bare reference point.

> **⚠️ Honest Annotation:** $w_{MC}=w_{CI}$ is a constructive condition — it ensures uniqueness of the solution and yields $\theta_M^0=57.93^\circ$, consistent with the electron angle. A rigorous derivation of this condition from $S_3$ representation theory would require introducing Clebsch-Gordan coefficient correspondences, which lies beyond the scope of Volume 1.

These three conditions form a simultaneous system. Its solution is unique; the numerical solution is:

$$\boxed{\theta_M^0 = 57.9300000000^\circ, \quad \theta_C^0 = 26.1593112467^\circ, \quad \theta_I^0 = 5.9106887533^\circ}.$$

Constraint verification: $57.93 + 26.1593112467 + 5.9106887533 = 90.0000000000^\circ$ ✅

The word "bare" carries two layers of meaning:

- **It is "bare"**: the primitive configuration solved directly from the Six-Term Cost Function and symmetry conditions, without any quantum corrections or higher-order perturbations.
- **It is a "reference"**: all subsequent corrections (seven-level expansion, higher-order percolation effects) are expanded around this point as the origin.

The integer part 137 agrees with $S_e = 137.035999084$. The fractional deviation $0.035999083$ is precisely the gap that the subsequent seven-level recursion is to fill.

## §1.4.3 Bowl Slope: First-Order Gradient

### 1.4.3.1 Gradients Along the Three Directions

At the bare reference point, compute the gradient of $S(\theta)$: $\nabla S = (\partial S/\partial\theta_M, \partial S/\partial\theta_C, \partial S/\partial\theta_I)$.

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial\theta_i} = -\frac{2\cos\theta_i}{\sin^3\theta_i} - \frac{\cos\theta_i}{\sin^2\theta_i}\left(\frac{1}{\sin\theta_j} + \frac{1}{\sin\theta_k}\right).$$

Among which $\partial S/\partial\theta_I$ dominates (contributing over 99.9%).

### 1.4.3.2 Intuitive Understanding: Information Field Dominance

The extreme non-uniformity of the gradient tells us: at the bare reference point, **the "hillside" in the information sector ($\theta_I$) direction is extraordinarily steep** — a slight deviation from $\theta_I^0$ causes an enormous change in $S$.

### 1.4.3.3 Effective Gradient Under Constraint

## §1.4.5 Decoupled Coordinate System $(\xi, \eta)$

### 1.4.5.1 Why New Coordinates?

The off-diagonal element (301.67) of the cross-section Hessian $H_\Sigma$ is non-zero, indicating that $\theta_M$ and $\theta_C$ are not "natural" coordinates on the constrained cross-section. We need a new coordinate pair $(\xi, \eta)$ that diagonalizes the Hessian.

**Decoupled Coordinates (GT-1.4.0.6)**

$$\begin{pmatrix} \xi \\ \eta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.9974 & -0.0718 \\ 0.0718 & 0.9974 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \theta_M - \theta_M^0 \\ \theta_C - \theta_C^0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

In these coordinates, the cross-section Hessian is diagonalized:

$$H_\Sigma^{\text{diag}} = \begin{pmatrix} 392.21 & 0 \\ 0 & 58760.77 \end{pmatrix}\ \text{rad}^{-2}.$$

- $\xi$ direction: **Soft Mode**, curvature 392.21 (corresponding to motion "along equipotential lines")
- $\eta$ direction: **Hard Mode**, curvature 58760.77 (corresponding to motion "perpendicular to equipotential lines")

### 1.4.5.2 Physical Meaning

$\xi$ and $\eta$ correspond to two distinct physical processes:
- **$\xi$ (Soft Mode)**: joint variation of the matter-causal sectors. Movement along $\xi$ changes $\theta_M$ and $\theta_C$ synchronously while $\theta_I$ remains nearly unchanged — corresponding to "fine-tuning of the electromagnetic interaction strength."
- **$\eta$ (Hard Mode)**: variation dominated by the information sector. Movement along $\eta$ causes $\theta_I$ to change dramatically — corresponding to "drastic variation of inter-sector coupling."

### 1.4.5.3 Origin of the Stiffness Ratio 150

**Stiffness Ratio (GT-1.4.0.7)**

$$\frac{\lambda_2^\Sigma}{\lambda_1^\Sigma} \approx 149.8 \approx 150.$$

This ratio is not a free parameter — it is uniquely determined by the second-order derivatives of the Six-Term Cost Function at the bare reference point. 150 is approximately $(k_0 \Lambda)^2 \times 4.17$, where $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$.
