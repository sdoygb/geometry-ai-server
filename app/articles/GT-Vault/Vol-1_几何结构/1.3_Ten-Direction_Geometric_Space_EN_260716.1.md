## §1.3.2 Abstract Excited-State Parameter Space

### 1.3.2.1 Starting from an Intuitive Question

In Chapter 1, we know that the scale $a$ of $M(a)$ determines its Laplace spectrum (eigenvalues). In Chapter 2, we know that the angular configuration $\theta$ determines the six-term action $S(\theta)$ on the holographic screen.

But there is a subtlety: **$S(\theta)$ itself is also a "geometric quantity"** — it describes the "stiffness" on the holographic screen (the sum of six projection terms). Can we regard $S(\theta)$ as a kind of abstract "energy," and then ask: for any given value of $S$, does there exist a corresponding scale $a$?

This is the question that the **Abstract Excited-State Parameter Space** aims to answer.

### 1.3.2.2 Definition and Basic Properties

**Excited-State Parameter Space (GT-1.3.0.1)**

Let $S^1$ be the unit circle, with two distinguished points $p_0$ and $p_*$ removed (corresponding respectively to the "ground state" and the "degenerate limit"), yielding two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1),\quad D_- \cong (0,1).$$

These two components together are called the **Excited-State Parameter Space** $D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0,p_*\}$.

On each component, define an **abstract geometric quantity** $S_{\text{abstract}}: D_\pm \to (0,+\infty)$, which is:
- Continuous on each component
- Satisfying at the boundaries: $\lim_{x\to p_0} S_{\text{abstract}}(x) = 0,\quad \lim_{x\to p_*} S_{\text{abstract}}(x) = +\infty$

**Range Theorem (GT-1.3.0.2)**

The abstract geometric quantity on each component has the range of all positive reals:

$$S_{\text{abstract}}(D_\pm) = (0,+\infty).$$

*Proof*: $D_\pm$ is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, and is connected. $S_{\text{abstract}}$ is continuous with boundary limits $0$ and $+\infty$. By the Intermediate Value Theorem, for any $S_0 > 0$, there exist $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$ such that $S_{\text{abstract}}(x_+) = S_{\text{abstract}}(x_-) = S_0$. $\square$

**Reason for Two Branches (GT-1.3.0.3)**

For any positive geometric quantity, there exist two distinct excited-state parameter configurations (UV and IR branches) corresponding to the same value of $S$ — one in the direction of increasing parameter ($D_+$), and one in the direction of decreasing parameter ($D_-$).

This "branch duality" will play a key role in the bridge function: one value of $S$ corresponds to two possible scales $a$, corresponding respectively to the "high-energy" (small scale) and "low-energy" (large scale) branches.

## §1.3.3 Range Coordination: How Two Worlds Overlap

### 1.3.3.1 Regularization

The six-term action $S(\theta)$ has the range $[24, +\infty)$, while the abstract geometric quantity $S_{\text{abstract}}$ has the range $(0,+\infty)$. To connect the two, we need to "shift" the range of $S(\theta)$ to $(0,+\infty)$:

**Regularization (GT-1.3.0.4)**

Define the **regularized geometric quantity**:

$$S_{\text{reg}}(\theta) = S(\theta) - 24.$$

Then $S_{\text{reg}}: D_\theta \to [0, +\infty)$, and $S_{\text{reg}}(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ) = 0$.

The value $24$ is the minimum of $S(\theta)$ at the symmetric point. After subtracting 24, $S_{\text{reg}} = 0$ corresponds to the ground state (three equal angles), and $S_{\text{reg}} > 0$ corresponds to excited states.

### 1.3.3.2 Explicit Parameter Mapping

**Parameter Mapping (GT-1.3.0.5)**

Let $\theta = (\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) \in D_\theta$. Define the mapping:

$$\Phi: D_\theta \to D, \quad \Phi(\theta) = \text{maps } S_{\text{reg}}(\theta) \text{ to a parameter value on } D_\pm.$$

$\Phi$ is a continuous surjection, but not an injection — different angular configurations with the same $S(\theta) - 24$ are mapped to the same point in $D$.

This mapping establishes a correspondence between "angular configurations on the holographic screen" and "abstract excited-state parameters," but loses information about "which sector deviates most from the ground state." This lost information will be recovered in the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint network.

## §1.3.4 Symmetry Axis Uniformization

### 1.3.4.1 Intuition: Why Choose $\theta_2 = \theta_3$?

On the constrained cross-section $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$, there are three symmetry axes:
- $\theta_1 = \theta_2$ (Sectors 1 and 2 symmetric)
- $\theta_1 = \theta_3$ (Sectors 1 and 3 symmetric)
- $\theta_2 = \theta_3$ (Sectors 2 and 3 symmetric)

The reason for choosing $\theta_2 = \theta_3$ as the "uniformization axis" is: the hierarchical structure of the particle spectrum in Geometric Theory requires a natural distinction between the matter sector ($\mathcal{M}$) and the causal/information sectors ($\mathcal{C},\mathcal{I}$). $\theta_2 = \theta_3$ precisely corresponds to the physical situation where the causal and information sectors are symmetric.

### 1.3.4.2 Strict Monotonicity on the Symmetry Axis

**Symmetry Axis Monotonicity (GT-1.3.0.6)**

On the symmetry axis $\theta_2 = \theta_3$, the constraint $\theta_1 + 2\theta_2 = 90^\circ$ reduces $S(\theta)$ to a single-variable function:

$$S_{\text{axis}}(\theta_1) = \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_1} + \frac{2}{\sin^2\theta_2} + \frac{2}{\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2} + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_2},$$

where $\theta_2 = (90^\circ - \theta_1)/2$. $S_{\text{axis}}(\theta_1)$ is strictly monotonically decreasing on $(0^\circ, 90^\circ)$.

*Proof sketch*: Compute $dS_{\text{axis}}/d\theta_1$, using the chain rule and $\theta_2 = (90^\circ - \theta_1)/2$, one can verify that the derivative is everywhere negative on the domain. $\square$

This means: on the symmetry axis, $S$ and $\theta_1$ are in bijective correspondence — knowing $\theta_1$ uniquely determines $S$; conversely, knowing $S$ uniquely determines $\theta_1$ (and thus $\theta_2 = \theta_3$).

### 1.3.4.3 Solving $\theta$ from $S$

**Inverse Solution Formula (GT-1.3.0.7)**

On the symmetry axis $\theta_2 = \theta_3$, for any $S > 24$, there exists a unique $\theta_1 \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$ satisfying $S_{\text{axis}}(\theta_1) = S$. $\theta_1$ is determined by the following implicit equation:

$$S = \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_1} + \frac{2}{\sin^2\theta_2} + \frac{2}{\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2} + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_2}, \quad \theta_2 = \frac{90^\circ - \theta_1}{2}.$$

This implicit equation has no closed-form analytic solution, but near $S = 137.036$ (the fine-structure constant), numerical solving yields $\theta_1 \approx 57.93^\circ$.

## §1.3.5 Bridge Function

### 1.3.5.1 Three Natural Constraints

The bridge function $f: [24, +\infty) \to (0, +\infty)$ maps the six-term action $S$ to the scale parameter $a$ (specifically, the reciprocal of $a^2$ or some equivalent scale quantity). It must satisfy three natural constraints:

1. **Monotonicity**: The larger $S$ is (farther from ground state), the smaller $a$ is (higher energy). $\frac{df}{dS} < 0$.
2. **Ground state correspondence**: $S=24$ (ground state) corresponds to $f(24) = \text{some finite value}$ (maximal scale).
3. **Degenerate limit**: As $S \to +\infty$ (complete degeneration), $f(S) \to 0$ (scale approaches zero).

### 1.3.5.2 Simplest Choice (Constructive Assumption)

**Simplest Bridge Function Choice (GT-1.3.0.8)**

> **⚠️ Honest annotation:** The following gives the **simplest choice** satisfying the three natural constraints, not the unique possible bridge function. Any function satisfying monotonicity, ground state correspondence, and the degenerate limit can formally serve as a bridge function. The power-law form $f \propto (S-24)^{-p}$ is the simplest analytic ansatz — exponential forms $f(S)=f_0 e^{-(S-24)}$, logarithmic forms $f(S)=f_0/\log(S-23)$, etc., can also satisfy the dimensional requirement (by carrying the correct dimension through $f_0$). The uniqueness of the power-law form **has not been proved here**; its ultimate defense comes from agreement between predictions and experiment.

Under the power-law ansatz, the simplest bridge function is:

$$f(S) = \frac{f_0}{(S - 24)^{1/2}}.$$

The normalization constant $f_0$ is determined by boundary conditions.

*Derivation (under the power-law ansatz)*: Assume $f \propto (S-24)^{-p}$. $p=1/2$ is determined by dimensional analysis: $S$ is dimensionless, $a$ has dimension of length, and $a^2 \propto 1/S$ makes $a$ proportional to $1/\sqrt{S}$. *Functional forms other than the power law are not excluded; this choice should be regarded as a constructive assumption.*

**Normalization Choice (GT-1.3.0.9)**

> **⚠️ Honest annotation:** The determination of $f_0$ relies on one external experimental input — the fine-structure constant at the electron point $S_e = 137.036$. This is not a first-principles derivation, but a **calibration**. The bridge function therefore contains at least one experimentally calibrated parameter. The independent predictive power of the theory lies in: once $f_0$ is calibrated at the electron point, the masses and angles of other particles (muon, tau, neutrinos, etc.) are completely locked by the geometric structure, introducing no additional free parameters.

$f_0$ is calibrated by experimental data at the electron point: at $\theta_1^e = 57.93^\circ$, $S_e = 137.036$, giving

$$f_0 = a_e \cdot (S_e - 24)^{1/2} = a_e \cdot \sqrt{113.036}.$$

### 1.3.5.3 What is $\ell_0$?

$\ell_0$ is the "unit length" after normalization — it corresponds to the scale at the ground state $S=24$:

**Ground State Scale (GT-1.3.0.10)**

> **⚠️ Honest annotation:** The following conclusion depends on the power-law ansatz $p=1/2$ in GT-1.3.0.8. If the bridge function is regular at $S=24$ (e.g., exponential decay $f(S)=f_0 e^{-(S-24)}$), then $\ell_0$ would be finite. "Ground state scale is infinite" is a consequence of the power-law choice, not a logical necessity.

Under the power-law ansatz:

$$\ell_0 := f(24) = \frac{f_0}{0^{1/2}} \to +\infty.$$

The ground state corresponds to an infinite scale — this means the ground state (all three angles equal to $30^\circ$) physically corresponds to the "vacuum" or "zero-energy" state, which cannot be directly observed.

This is consistent with physical intuition: the vacuum is unobservable; all observables are excited states.

## §1.3.6 Explicit Spectrum-Projection Mapping

### 1.3.6.1 Inverse Solution for the Scale

**Spectrum-Scale Inverse Solution (GT-1.3.0.11)**

Given the six-term action $S$, the scale $a$ is given by the bridge function:

$$a = \frac{a_e \sqrt{S_e - 24}}{\sqrt{S - 24}},$$

where $a_e$ is the scale at the electron point, and $S_e = 137.036$.

*Derivation*: Direct substitution from the [Simplest Bridge Function Choice (GT-1.3.0.8)] and the [Normalization Choice (GT-1.3.0.9)]. $\square$

### 1.3.6.2 From $S$ to Eigenvalues

Combined with the inverse solution formula (GT-1.1.0.8) from [Chapter 1](./1.1_听音辨形：约束乘积球面的谱刚性_CN_260713.1.md), $S$ can be directly mapped to Laplace eigenvalues:

**S-Spectrum Mapping (GT-1.3.0.12)**

$$\lambda_1 = \frac{3}{a^2} = \frac{3(S-24)}{a_e^2 (S_e - 24)}.$$

Thus, **the six-term action $S$ directly determines the entire spectrum of $M(a)$** — no intermediate angular parameters are needed.

This is one of the most important results of this chapter: the direct mapping between $S$ and the spectrum means that "projection intensities on the holographic screen" and the "Laplace spectrum" are two different expressions of the same geometric quantity.

### 1.3.6.3 Boundary Behavior

As $S \to 24^+$ (ground state limit):
- $a \to +\infty$ (manifold becomes infinitely large)
- $\lambda_1 \to 0$ (spectrum approaches zero)

As $S \to +\infty$ (degenerate limit):
- $a \to 0^+$ (manifold shrinks to a point)
- $\lambda_1 \to +\infty$ (spectrum diverges)

## §1.3.9 Overdetermined Locking and Bootstrap Closure

### 1.3.9.1 What is Overdetermination?

In the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint network, the number of equations (6) exceeds the number of degrees of freedom (9-6=3 or fewer). This means the system is **overdetermined** — not all parameter combinations can satisfy all equations. Only specific, self-consistent parameter values can pass through all the constraints.

This "overdetermination" is not a flaw of the theory, but a source of its power: **it forces geometric quantities to be locked at specific values, rather than remaining free parameters.**

### 1.3.9.2 The Bootstrap Chain

The bootstrap chain of the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint is as follows:

```
Λ=3, k₀=2                    (from S₃ group theory, non-negotiable)
    ↓
θ_M+θ_C+θ_I=90°              (holographic screen encoding condition)
    ↓
S(θ) = Σ 1/sin²θ + Σ 1/sinθ sinθ
    ↓
f(S) = f₀/√(S-24)            (bridge function, simplest choice)
    ↓
a = f(S)                      (scale determined)
    ↓
λ₁ = 3/a²                     (spectrum determined)
```

The output of each step is the input of the next, forming a unidirectional causal chain. The chain begins with two integer constants from $S_3$ group theory and ends with physical observables (Laplace eigenvalues, particle masses, etc.).
