## §1.6.1 Percolation Function $\Phi$: The Elliptic Equation

### 1.6.1.1 Why is a percolation function needed?

Recall the effective Hessian from Chapter 1.4:

$$H^{\text{eff}} = H^{\text{bare}} + \Phi \otimes H^{\text{bg\_proj}} + H^W$$

where $H^{\text{bare}} = \text{diag}(392.21, 58760.77)$ is the bare Hessian (Chapter 1.4), $H^{\text{bg\_proj}}$ is the projection of the $30^\circ$ background in $(\xi,\eta)$ coordinates (Chapter 1.3), and $H^W$ is the cross-sector coupling matrix (to be derived in §1.6.2). $\Phi$ is the **percolation function** — it describes how the $30^\circ$ background "percolates" onto the local cross-section.

Intuitively: the bare reference point found in Chapter 1.4 is a "bare" point — it does not include the percolation effect of the background upon it. When we project the geometric structure of the $30^\circ$ background (the global minimum of the six-term action, $S=24$) onto the local cross-section, it is equivalent to covering the surface of the "bowl" with a "percolation membrane." $\Phi$ is precisely the thickness distribution of this membrane.

### 1.6.1.2 From Joint Action Variation to the Elliptic Equation

Introduce the **joint action**:

$$S_{\text{joint}}[\text{total universe}] = S[\text{local sectors}] + S[\text{30° background}] + W[\text{coupling}]$$

where $S[\text{30° background}] = 24$ is a known constant (Chapter 1.3), and $W[\text{coupling}]$ is the cross-sector coupling term (derived in §1.6.2). Varying with respect to $\Phi$, $\delta S_{\text{joint}}/\delta\Phi = 0$, and expanding to second order in $\Phi$, we obtain the elliptic partial differential equation satisfied by the percolation function:

**Elliptic Equation for the Percolation Function (GT-1.6.0.1)**

$$L[\Phi] := -\left(\lambda_1^{\text{eff}}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2} + \lambda_2^{\text{eff}}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \eta^2}\right)\Phi + M^2(\xi,\eta)\Phi = Q(\xi,\eta)$$

Each term of this equation has a clear geometric meaning:

| Term | Symbol | Meaning | Source |
|:---|:---:|:---|:---:|
| Second derivative terms | $\lambda_{1,2}^{\text{eff}}$ | Effective soft/hard modes (391.05, 59324.3 rad$^{-2}$) | Chapter 1.4 Hessian + percolation correction |
| Mass term | $M^2$ | Normal curvature ($\geq 0$) | Curvature in the normal direction of the constraint manifold |
| Source term | $Q$ | Local projection of cross-sector coupling | Prefactors from §1.6.2 |

**Uniform ellipticity**: Since $\lambda_1^{\text{eff}}, \lambda_2^{\text{eff}} > 0$, the principal coefficient matrix $\text{diag}(\lambda_1^{\text{eff}}, \lambda_2^{\text{eff}})$ of the operator $L$ is positive definite; hence $L$ is a uniformly elliptic operator.

### 1.6.1.3 Non-Negativity of the Effective Mass Term $M^2$

$M^2(\xi,\eta)$ is the normal curvature of the joint action in the normal direction $n = (1,1,1)/\sqrt{3}$ (the direction perpendicular to the constraint manifold $\theta_M+\theta_C+\theta_I=90^\circ$). Since the second derivative of $S(\theta)$ in the normal direction is positive (the symmetry point is a global minimum), $M^2 \geq 0$ holds everywhere.

### 1.6.1.4 Embedding Map of the Source Term $Q$

$Q(\xi,\eta)$ is the projection of the cross-sector coupling matrix $H^W$ in $(\xi,\eta)$ coordinates. Its concrete form depends on the normal bundle geometry of §1.6.2.

### 1.6.1.5 Boundary Value and Green's Function

On the boundary $\partial\Sigma$ of the constrained cross-section $\Sigma$ ($\theta_i \to 0^+$ or $\theta_i \to 90^-$), the percolation function must satisfy the physical boundary condition: $\Phi|_{\partial\Sigma} = 0$. This is because at the boundary the sectors are either completely degenerate or completely projected, and no percolation effect exists.

**Existence of Green's Function (GT-1.6.0.1 cont.)**

The uniformly elliptic operator $L$ on $\Sigma$ possesses a unique Green's function $G(\xi,\eta;\xi',\eta')$, satisfying:

$$L G(\xi,\eta;\xi',\eta') = \delta(\xi-\xi')\delta(\eta-\eta').$$

The solution for $\Phi$ is expressed via the Green's function as:

$$\Phi(\xi,\eta) = \int_\Sigma G(\xi,\eta;\xi',\eta') Q(\xi',\eta')\, d\xi' d\eta'.$$

### 1.6.1.6 Spherical Harmonic Expansion and Super-Algebraic Decay

On $\Sigma \cong S^2$, the Green's function can be expanded as a series of spherical harmonics:

$$G(\theta,\phi;\theta',\phi') = \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty \sum_{m=-\ell}^\ell \frac{Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\phi)Y_{\ell m}^*(\theta',\phi')}{\lambda_1^{\text{eff}} \ell(\ell+1)/2 + \lambda_2^{\text{eff}} m^2/2 + M_0^2}.$$

Since $\lambda_2^{\text{eff}} \approx 150 \times \lambda_1^{\text{eff}}$, high-order $m$ modes (corresponding to high-frequency oscillations in the $\eta$ direction) are strongly suppressed. This leads to **super-algebraic decay**: the variation of $\Phi$ on $\Sigma$ is primarily controlled by low-frequency modes in the $\xi$ direction.

## §1.6.2 Cross-Sector Coupling $H^W$: The Prefactors

### 1.6.2.1 Problem: How do the three sectors couple?

The coupling term $H^W$ among the three sectors $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$ appears in the effective Hessian. Its origin lies in the geometry of the normal bundle $N\Sigma$ — the connection induced by $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$ in the orthogonal complement space of $\Sigma$.

The structure group of the normal bundle $N\Sigma$ is $\text{Spin}(8)$, the symmetry that naturally emerges from the sector structure of the tripartite tangent bundle in gauge theory.

### 1.6.2.2 Spin(8) Structure of the Normal Bundle $N\Sigma$

**Spin(8) Structure of the Normal Bundle (GT-1.6.0.2)**

On the constrained cross-section $\Sigma$, the normal bundle $N\Sigma$ of $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$ carries a Spin(8) structure. The dimension of Spin(8) is $\dim\text{Spin}(8) = 28$, and the **triality symmetry** (triple permutation symmetry) of its root system corresponds exactly to the three sectors of the tripartite tangent bundle.

*Remark*: Spin(8) is the only Spin group possessing triality (outer automorphism group $S_3$). This unusual algebraic property is the deep root of the tripartite tangent bundle symmetry.

### 1.6.2.3 Algebraic Relation between Spectral Residues and Prefactors

The cross-sector coupling matrix $H^W$ in $(\xi,\eta)$ coordinates takes the form $H^W = \text{diag}(a', b')$. $a'$ and $b'$ are the **prefactors** of cross-sector coupling, determined by the spectral residues of Spin(8):

$$a' = \frac{\text{Tr}_{\text{adj}}(R^2_{\text{spin}(8)})}{\text{Tr}_{\text{fund}}(R^2_{\text{spin}(8)})} \cdot \frac{\dim(N\Sigma)}{\dim(\Sigma)}$$

**Group-theoretic derivation (D₄ = Spin(8)):** Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(8)$ (type D₄), dual Coxeter number $g = 2n-2 = 6$. Casimir trace ratio $\text{Tr}_{\text{adj}}(R^2)/\text{Tr}_{\text{fund}}(R^2) = 2g = 12$, hence $a' = 12 \cdot \dim(N\Sigma)/\dim(\Sigma)$.
