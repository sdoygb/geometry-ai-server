# 2.1 Spectral Triple Construction

**Upgrading the geometric theory's Constrained Product Manifold $M(a) = S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$ to a real spectral triple in Connes' sense, as the starting point of the Dimensional Functor.**

## 2.1.1 Why a Spectral Triple?

Volume 0 established pure geometric structures on $M(a)$:

| Structure | Source | Output |
|:---|:---|:---:|
| Three Axioms + Six-Term Cost Function | Vol-0 ┬¦0.3 | Angles $(\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I)$, action $S(\theta)$ |
| Constrained Cross-Section Hessian | Vol-0 ┬¦0.5 | Soft/hard modes $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$, spectral gap $\Lambda_H$ |
| Product Sphere Spectral Rigidity | Vol-0 ┬¦0.6 | First three eigenvalues $\{0, \lambda_1, \lambda_2\}$ uniquely determine isometry |

But these structures can only answer "what are the ratios of geometric quantities" ŌĆö they are all **dimensionless**. To answer "what is the corresponding physical constant in meters / seconds / kilograms for this geometric quantity", we need a richer structure: **a functor capable of reading physical scales from geometric spectral data**.

Noncommutative geometry provides the standard tool for this functor: **the spectral triple** $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D, J, \gamma)$. The spectral data $\{(\lambda_n, \psi_n)\}$ of a spectral triple, via the Wodzicki Residue, Dixmier trace, and heat kernel asymptotic coefficients, can functorially reconstruct the manifold's **length scale, time scale, and mass scale** (Connes, 1994; Connes & Marcolli, 2008).

The sole task of this section: **to construct the correct spectral triple on $M(a)$, providing the starting point for all subsequent scale reconstructions.**

## 2.1.3 Clifford Algebra and Spinor Bundle

The tangent bundle $TM(a)$ of $M(a)$ is a nine-dimensional real vector bundle, with Clifford algebra:

$$\text{Cliff}(TM(a)) \cong Cl(9)$$

This is the Clifford algebra of the standard inner product on $\mathbb{R}^9$. The algebraic structure of $Cl(9)$ is:

| Property | Value | Remark |
|:---:|:---|:---:|
| Generators | $\{e_1, \ldots, e_9\}$ | Tripartite frame, $e_i^2 = 1$, $e_i e_j = -e_j e_i$ |
| Real dimension | $2^9 = 512$ | |
| Even part | $Cl^0(9) \cong Cl(8)$ | 8-dimensional Clifford algebra (isomorphic to $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$) |
| Irreducible real spinor rep. | $\dim_{\mathbb{R}} S = 16$ | $2^{\lfloor 9/2 \rfloor} = 16$ |
| Irreducible complex spinor rep. | $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} S = 16$ | $2^{9/2} = 16$ (9 is odd, unique irreducible representation) |

**Tripartite Tangent Bundle correspondence** ([Vol-1 Geometric Structure](../Vol-1_ÕćĀõĮĢń╗ōµ×ä/MOC.md)): the frame $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ corresponds to the M-Realm $M$-Sector, $\{e_4, e_5, e_6\}$ to the C-Realm $C$-Sector, $\{e_7, e_8, e_9\}$ to the I-Realm $I$-Sector.

The spinor bundle $S \to M(a)$ is given by the (unique) spinor representation of Spin(9), with $\text{rank}_{\mathbb{C}} S = 16$.

## 2.1.4 Item-by-Item Construction of the Spectral Triple

We now construct the real spectral triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D, J, \gamma)$ item by item.

### Algebra $\mathcal{A}$

$$\mathcal{A} = \overline{C^\infty(M(a)) \otimes Cl^0(9)}^{\|\cdot\|_{C^*}}$$

where:
- $C^\infty(M(a))$ is the algebra of smooth functions on $M(a)$
- $Cl^0(9) \cong Cl(8)$ is the even part of the Clifford algebra
- $\overline{\cdots}^{\|\cdot\|_{C^*}}$ denotes the $C^*$-norm closure

**Geometric meaning**: $\mathcal{A}$ contains two types of generators ŌĆö "position" (via functions) and "spinor structure" (via the Clifford even part), allowing the spectral triple to simultaneously encode the point geometry and spinor geometry of the manifold.

**Why $Cl^0(9)$?** Using $C^\infty(M(a))$ alone can only reconstruct point-set topology; incorporating $Cl^0(9)$ allows the algebra to distinguish spinor transformations across different sectors ŌĆö a prerequisite for the subsequent threefold scale (length/time/mass) to be read out from different sectors respectively.

### Hilbert Space $\mathcal{H}$

$$\mathcal{H} = L^2(M(a), S)$$

i.e., the space of square-integrable sections of the $Cl(9)$ spinor bundle $S$ over $M(a)$, equipped with the inner product:

$$\langle \psi, \varphi \rangle = \int_{M(a)} (\psi(x), \varphi(x))_{S_x} \, d\text{vol}_{M(a)}(x)$$

where $(\cdot, \cdot)_{S_x}$ is the standard Hermitian inner product on the spinor fiber. $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathcal{H} = \infty$ (countably infinite-dimensional).

### Dirac Operator $D$

In the tripartite frame $\{e_1, \ldots, e_9\}$, the Dirac operator is defined as:

$$D = \sum_{i=1}^{9} e_i \cdot \nabla_{e_i}$$

where $\nabla$ is the lift of the Levi-Civita connection on $M(a)$ to the spinor bundle. Since $M(a)$ is a compact spin manifold, $\nabla$ is uniquely determined by the Spin(9) structure and the standard metric.

**Key properties**:
- $D$ is self-adjoint: $D^\dagger = D$ (guaranteed by the metric compatibility of the spinor connection)
- $D$ is an elliptic operator, with purely discrete real spectrum ($M(a)$ compact)
- $\text{Ker } D = \{0\}$ (Dirac operators on odd-dimensional manifolds have no zero modes, guaranteed by $\hat{A}$-genus = 0 and the index theorem)

### Real Structure $J$

The real structure (charge conjugation operator) $J$ is defined as:

$$J = C \otimes J_M \otimes J_C \otimes J_I$$

where:
- $C$ is the standard charge conjugation (complex conjugation) on the spinor space
- $J_M, J_C, J_I$ are the quaternionic real structures of the three $S^3$ factors, respectively

**KO-dimension**: $9 \equiv 1 \pmod{8}$, hence $J^2 = +1$. This corresponds to the "real" type of real spectral triples.

### Chirality Operator $\gamma$

The chirality operator is defined as:

$$\gamma = \omega = e_1 e_2 \cdots e_9$$

satisfying $\gamma^2 = (+1)^{36} = +1$ (since $9 \times 8 / 2 = 36$ is even). $D$ anti-commutes with $\gamma$: $D\gamma = -\gamma D$.

## 2.1.5 Verification of Spectral Triple Properties

A spectral triple must satisfy Connes' seven axiomatic conditions (Connes, 1994, ┬¦VI.1; Connes & Marcolli, 2008, ┬¦1.8). We verify each below.

### (1) Compactness Condition: $(D^2 + 1)^{-1/2}$ is a compact operator

**Proof**: $M(a)$ is a compact Riemannian manifold, $D$ is an elliptic first-order self-adjoint differential operator. By elliptic regularity theory (see Gilkey, 1995, ┬¦1.9 in detail), the resolvent $(D - \lambda)^{-1}$ is a bounded operator from $L^2$ to $H^1$ (Sobolev space). Compactness of $M(a)$ ensures the embedding $H^1 \hookrightarrow L^2$ is compact. Hence the composition $(D - \lambda)^{-1}$ is a compact operator on $L^2$. Taking $\lambda = i$, then $(D^2 + 1)^{-1/2} = (D - i)^{-1}(D + i)(D^2 + 1)^{-1/2}$ is a composition of compact operators, and is therefore compact. $\square$

### (2) First-Order Condition: $[[D, a], Jb^*J^{-1}] = 0$ for all $a, b \in \mathcal{A}$

**Proof**: Take $a \in C^\infty(M(a))$. In a local frame:

$$[D, a] = \sum_{i=1}^{9} e_i \cdot \nabla_{e_i}(a \cdot) - a \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{9} e_i \cdot \nabla_{e_i} = \sum_{i=1}^{9} e_i \cdot (\partial_i a)$$

This is a zeroth-order operator (Clifford multiplication by the function gradient). For $b \in C^\infty(M(a))$, $Jb^*J^{-1} = [\text{complex conjugation} \cdot b \cdot \text{complex conjugation}^{-1}]$, which acts as pointwise left multiplication under the spinor inner product:

$$[[D, a], Jb^*J^{-1}] = \left[\sum_i e_i(\partial_i a), Jb^*J^{-1}\right] = 0$$

because $[D, a]$ is a pointwise operator (Clifford multiplication), which commutes with the pointwise left multiplication by $Jb^*J^{-1}$.

The generators of the $Cl^0(9)$ part act on $\mathcal{H}$ via Clifford multiplication. Since $Cl^0(9)$ and $\sum_i e_i(\partial_i a)$ lie in the same Clifford algebra, their commutator lies in $Cl(9)$. The complex conjugation action of $J$ makes $Jb^*J^{-1}$ the real version of $b$, commuting with the Clifford elements. Hence the first-order condition holds in full. $\square$

### (3) Regularity Condition: $\mathcal{A} \subset \bigcap_{k \geq 1} \text{Dom}(D^k)$

**Proof**: Elements of $C^\infty(M(a))$ retain smoothness under any power of $D$ ŌĆö since $D^k$ is a $k$-th order elliptic differential operator, it maps $C^\infty$ back to $C^\infty$. $Cl^0(9)$ is a finite-dimensional algebra ($\dim_{\mathbb{R}} = 128$), whose elements act on $\mathcal{H}$ as bounded Clifford multiplication operators; left-multiplying smooth functions preserves smoothness. Hence all elements of $\mathcal{A}$ belong to $\bigcap_{k \geq 1} \text{Dom}(D^k)$. $\square$

### (4) Boundedness Condition: $\mathcal{A}$ is faithfully represented as bounded operators on $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$

**Proof**: In two steps:
- **$C^\infty(M(a))$ part**: $f \in C^\infty(M(a))$ acts on $\mathcal{H} = L^2(M(a), S)$ via pointwise multiplication: $(M_f \psi)(x) = f(x)\psi(x)$. Since $M(a)$ is compact, $\|M_f\|_{\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})} = \|f\|_{L^\infty} < \infty$, hence $M_f$ is bounded.
- **$Cl^0(9)$ part**: $Cl^0(9) \cong M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ is a finite-dimensional $C^*$-algebra, whose elements act boundedly on the 16-dimensional spinor fiber, with the fiber norm uniform (compactness of $M(a)$ ensures uniform boundedness of the metric).

The tensor product representation $\pi(a \otimes b) = M_a \cdot \gamma(b)$ (where $\gamma(b)$ denotes Clifford right-multiplication) preserves boundedness. The representation is faithful: if $\pi(a \otimes b) = 0$, then for all $\psi \in \mathcal{H}$, $a(x) \gamma_x(b) \psi(x) = 0$ almost everywhere, from which $a=0$ and $b=0$ follow fiberwise. $\square$

### (5) Self-Adjointness and Real Structure Compatibility

Five sets of relations must be verified:

**ŌæĀ $D$ self-adjoint**: $D^\dagger = D$. Already explained in ┬¦2.1.4: metric compatibility of the Levi-Civita connection ensures that the spinor Dirac operator is symmetric under the $L^2$ inner product; ellipticity ensures its self-adjoint extension is unique and closed.

**ŌæĪ $J$ isometric**: $\langle J\psi, J\varphi \rangle = \overline{\langle \psi, \varphi \rangle}$. Guaranteed by the isometry of $C$ (complex conjugation) and the quaternionic real structures $J_M, J_C, J_I$ of each $S^3$ factor. All components are isometric maps, hence $J$ is a conjugate-linear isometry.

**Ōæó $J^2 = +1$**: Determined by KO-dimension $9 \equiv 1 \pmod{8}$. Concrete computation: $C^2 = 1$ (complex conjugation twice is the identity), quaternionic real structures satisfy $J_{S^3}^2 = 1$, hence $J^2 = C^2 \otimes J_M^2 \otimes J_C^2 \otimes J_I^2 = (+1) \otimes (+1) \otimes (+1) \otimes (+1) = +1$.

**ŌæŻ $JD = DJ$** (rather than anti-commuting): Real KO-dimension $1 \pmod{8}$ corresponds to $JD = DJ$. Verification: in the expression for $D$, $e_i \cdot \nabla_{e_i}$, $J$ acts by complex conjugation, $C$ commutes with $e_i$ (since $e_i$ are real frame elements), and $J_M, J_C, J_I$ commute with the $e_i$ of their respective sectors, hence $JD = DJ$.

**Ōæż $J\gamma = \gamma J$**: $\gamma = e_1 \cdots e_9$ is a product of real frame elements, $J$ is anti-linear, $J e_i J^{-1} = e_i$ (real frames are invariant), hence $J \gamma J^{-1} = \bar{\gamma} = \gamma$ ($\gamma$ is real), giving $J\gamma = \gamma J$.

### (6) Finite-Dimensionality Condition

Connes' finite-dimensionality condition requires: for any $a \in \mathcal{A}$, the operator $[D, a]$ is bounded, and $\langle D \rangle^{-n} a \langle D \rangle^{-n}$ is trace-class, where $n$ is the manifold dimension.

**Proof**:
- $[D, a] = \sum_i e_i(\partial_i a)$ is a zeroth-order operator, whose operator norm is bounded by $\|\nabla a\|_{L^\infty}$, hence bounded.
- Take $n = 9 = \dim M(a)$. The operator $\langle D \rangle^{-9} = (D^2 + 1)^{-9/2}$ is compact. By the Weyl spectral asymptotic formula (see ┬¦2.2.2 in detail), the eigenvalues of $D$ satisfy $\lambda_k \sim c k^{1/9}$, hence $\lambda_k^{-9} \sim c' k^{-1}$, whose sum converges, making it trace-class. Rigorously, the kernel function of $\langle D \rangle^{-9}$ on $M(a) \times M(a)$ is continuous ($9 > \dim M(a)/2$), so it is a trace-class operator. Boundedness of $\mathcal{A}$ preserves the trace-class property. $\square$

### (7) Orientability Condition

The orientability condition requires: there exists a Hochschild 9-cycle $c \in Z_9(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A})$ such that $\pi_D(c) = \gamma$.

**Verification**: $\gamma = e_1 \cdots e_9$. Construct the Hochschild 9-cycle:
$$c = \sum_{\sigma \in S_9} \text{sgn}(\sigma) \, x_{\sigma(1)} \cdots x_{\sigma(9)}$$

where $x_i$ are conformal coordinate functions in $\mathcal{A}$ corresponding to the frame directions $e_i$ (taking coordinates as standard spherical coordinates on $M(a)$). Since $M(a)$ is an oriented manifold (Vol-0 ┬¦0.3 Axiom 3 guarantees this), the representation $\pi_D(c)$ of this Hochschild 9-cycle yields $\gamma$. Concrete verification: $\pi_D(c) = [D, x_1] \cdots [D, x_9] + \text{permutation terms}$. Each $[D, x_i] = e_i \cdot d x_i/\partial x_i = e_i$ (in local coordinates), hence $\pi_D(c) \propto e_1 \cdots e_9 = \gamma$. $\square$

## 2.1.6 Spectral Data of the Spectral Triple

The core output of the spectral triple is the spectral data of the Dirac operator $D$, which serves as the numerical entry point for all subsequent scale reconstructions.

### 2.1.6.1 Product Structure of the Dirac Spectrum

Since $M(a) = S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$ is a product manifold, the spectrum of the Dirac operator $D$ is formed by the tensor product of the spectra of the Dirac operators on the three $S^3$ factors.

For the Dirac operator $D_{S^3_r}$ on a standard $S^3$ of radius $r$, its spectrum is (see Friedrich, 2000, ┬¦2.5 in detail):

$$\text{Spec}(D_{S^3_r}) = \left\{\pm\left(k + \frac{3}{2}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{r} \;\middle|\; k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\right\}$$

Multiplicity of each eigenvalue $\pm (k+3/2)/r$:

$$\text{mult}(k) = (k+1)(k+2)$$

For $D = D_{S^3_M} \otimes 1 \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes D_{S^3_C} \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes 1 \otimes D_{S^3_I}$ on $M(a)$, the eigenvalues are:

$$\lambda_{k_M, k_C, k_I, s_M, s_C, s_I} = s_M\left(k_M + \frac{3}{2}\right)\frac{1}{a} + s_C\left(k_C + \frac{3}{2}\right)\frac{\sqrt{3}}{a} + s_I\left(k_I + \frac{3}{2}\right)\frac{\sqrt{6}}{a}$$

where $s_M, s_C, s_I \in \{+1, -1\}$, $k_M, k_C, k_I \in \mathbb{N}_0$.

### 2.1.6.2 Lowest Non-Zero Eigenvalues

The M-Realm $S^3_M$ has the largest radius ($a$), and its first non-zero level contributes the dominant eigenvalue:

$$\lambda_{\min}^{(M)} = +\frac{3}{2a} \quad \text{(taking } k_M=0, s_M=+)$$

The C-Realm $S^3_C$ has radius $a/\sqrt{3}$, with lowest non-zero eigenvalue $+3\sqrt{3}/(2a)$. The I-Realm $S^3_I$ has radius $a/\sqrt{6}$, with lowest non-zero eigenvalue $+3\sqrt{6}/(2a)$.

**Soft mode correspondence**: $\lambda_1^{\text{eff}}$ corresponds to the effective weight of the $M$-Sector spectrum in the soft-mode direction, with its numerical value transmitted from the Hessian spectral data (see [2.2 Length Scale Reconstruction](./2.2_ķĢ┐Õ║”µĀćÕ║”ķćŹÕ╗║.md) ┬¦2.2.2 in detail).

## 2.1.7 Spectral Realization of the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint

**Existence and Uniqueness of the Spectral Triple (GT-2.1.0.2)**
The tripartite frame $\{e_1, \ldots, e_9\}$ corresponds to the nine elements $\{p_1, \ldots, p_9\}$. The M-Realm corresponds to $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$, the C-Realm to $\{e_4, e_5, e_6\}$, and the I-Realm to $\{e_7, e_8, e_9\}$. The first-order condition $[[D, a], Jb^*J^{-1}] = 0$ decomposes in local coordinates as:
- $M$-Sector gradients $\partial_{1,2,3}a$ ŌåÆ soft mode $\lambda_1^{\text{eff}}$
- $C \oplus I$-Sector gradients $\partial_{4,\ldots,9}a$ ŌåÆ hard mode $\lambda_2^{\text{eff}}$

The soft-to-hard mode ratio $\lambda_2^{\text{eff}} / \lambda_1^{\text{eff}} \approx 152.41$ corresponds to the stiffness ratio of the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint, locked by the Hessian block-diagonalization structure.

**Geometric meaning**: Within the spectral triple framework, the Nine-Element Mutual Constraint manifests as ŌĆö the nine directions are not of equal weight; rather, the 3 directions of the $M$-Sector and the 6 directions of the $C \oplus I$-Sector are orthogonally separated under the spinor inner product. This separation is precisely the geometric origin of $K$ in the mass scale reconstruction of ┬¦2.4 (the Dixmier trace of the $M$-Sector Dirac operator), and the source of the time scale $\chi_T$ in ┬¦2.3 (the heat kernel coefficient of the $C$-Sector).
