### Three Gaps from Discrete to Continuous

But the physical world requires not only a discrete spectrum, but also a continuous parameter space to describe excited states and interactions. From the discrete outputs of the first two chapters to a continuous geometric framework, there are exactly three gaps:

**Gap 1: Spectral levels → Continuous parameters.**
The Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ tells us there are three discrete levels, with weight ratio $6:2:1$. But how does this ratio manifest in continuous geometry? As the range of angular parameters? As the square of sphere radii? Or as some other quantity? We need an axiom to specify: how discrete spectral levels correspond to a continuous parameter space.

**Gap 2: Topological and analytic properties.**
Even knowing the Radius Ratio $1:1/\sqrt{3}:1/\sqrt{6}$, we still do not know the topology of the parameter space—is it open or closed? Where are its boundaries? How do geometric quantities behave as parameters approach the boundary? Without this information, no continuous geometric calculation can proceed.

[Λ=3 (GT-0.2.0.4)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.4) tells us $\Lambda=3$, i.e., the three sectors are interlocked. But what does "interlocking" mean concretely in continuous geometry? ## 0.3.1 Axiom 1: Tripartite Continuation

### Motivation

The Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ yields a three-level discrete structure. But how does this three-level discrete structure transition into three continuous angular parameters? We need to answer three questions:

1. **Why three parameters?** — Numerically, the number of spectral levels = 3.
2. **What does each parameter correspond to?** — Each parameter corresponds to one sector ($\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$).
3. **Where does the continuity of parameters come from?** — What is the "unfolding mechanism" from discrete levels to continuous parameters?

Axiom 1 answers these three questions.

### Statement

> **Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)** {#GT-0.3.0.1}
> 
> Let $V_1, V_2, V_3$ be the three discrete spectral levels corresponding to the Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ ([Spectral Weight Ratio (GT-0.1.0.9)](./0.1_零维源点与S₃_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.1.0.9)). There exists a **continuation map** from these three discrete levels to three continuous angular parameters:
> $$
> \Omega: \{V_1, V_2, V_3\} \longrightarrow (\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I) \in \mathbb{R}^3_{+},
> $$
> satisfying the following conditions:
> 1. **One-to-one correspondence:** Each spectral level $V_i$ maps to a unique sector angle $\theta_i$ ($i=1,2,3$ corresponding to the $\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{I}$ sectors respectively).
> 2. **Order-preserving:** The larger the spectral weight, the "broader" the corresponding geometric influence—formally, the geometric radius $r_i$ of sector $i$ is proportional to the square root of its spectral weight: $r_i \propto \sqrt{w_i}$. Geometrically, this ensures that variations in $\theta_i$ produce larger geometric effects on spheres of larger radius.
> 3. **Existence:** The parameter space $\Theta = \{(\theta_M,\theta_C,\theta_I) \in \mathbb{R}^3_{+}\}$ is non-empty and connected.

### Justification

Axiom 1 is not derived from $S_3$ group theory—it is a **constructive choice**. But we have reasons to regard it as natural:

**Reason 1: Numerical correspondence.** [Spectral Weight Ratio (GT-0.1.0.9)](./0.1_零维源点与S₃_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.1.0.9) yields 3 spectral levels. Three continuous parameters is the minimal possible choice (no fewer continuous parameters can describe the behavior of three discrete levels), and is also the natural way to "unfold" a three-layer discrete structure.

**Reason 2: Sector correspondence.** The Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ corresponds to three sectors ($\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{I}$), already established in [Λ=3 (GT-0.2.0.4)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.4) ($\Lambda=3$, three-sector interlocking). Mapping each spectral level to the angular parameter of the corresponding sector is internally consistent.

**Reason 3: Origin of the order-preserving property.** The larger the spectral weight, the more "information capacity" that level possesses. In a continuous parameter representation, information capacity is reflected in the variable range of the parameter. The [Radius Ratio (GT-0.2.0.6)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.6) (radius ratio $1:1/\sqrt{3}:1/\sqrt{6}$) already gives this proportional relation. Axiom 1 elevates this discrete correspondence to an axiomatic continuous mapping.

### Corollaries

> **Dimensionality of the Parameter Space (GT-0.3.0.2)** {#GT-0.3.0.2}
> 
> The parameter space $\Theta$ is an open subset of 3-dimensional real space.

*Proof.* By condition 3 of [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1), $\Theta$ is non-empty and connected. By condition 1, $\Theta$ is parameterized by three independent parameters, hence its dimension is 3. Since $\theta_i > 0$ is an open condition, $\Theta$ is an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^3_+$. ∎

## 0.3.2 Axiom 2: Product Sphere Geometry

### Motivation

[Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) defines three angular parameters. But what is the "geometric carrier" of these angular parameters?—where do they live? In flat $\mathbb{R}^3$ space? Or on a curved manifold?

In the [Radius Ratio (GT-0.2.0.6)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.6), we derived that the radius ratio of the three sectors is $1:1/\sqrt{3}:1/\sqrt{6}$. This ratio suggests that each sector corresponds to a sphere—the sphere radii have precisely the ratio $1:1/\sqrt{3}:1/\sqrt{6}$. Axiom 2 elevates this structure to an axiom.

### Statement

> **Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)** {#GT-0.3.0.3}
> 
> The fundamental geometric space is constituted by the product of three $S^3$ spheres:
> $$
> M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda}}\right) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}}\right),
> $$
> where:
> - $a > 0$ is the Global Scale Factor;
> - $\Lambda = 3$ is the Interlocking Constant ([Λ=3 (GT-0.2.0.4)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.4));
> - $k_0 = 2$ is the Compactness Function ([k₀=2 (GT-0.2.0.5)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.5));
> - The three $S^3$ spheres correspond to the $\mathcal{M}$, $\mathcal{C}$, and $\mathcal{I}$ sectors respectively.

### Justification

**Why $S^3$ rather than $S^2$ or $S^1$?**

The choice of $S^3$ is based on the following considerations:

1. **Symmetry:** $S^3$ possesses the maximal symmetry group $SO(4)$; each sector requires sufficient symmetry to accommodate internal structure. $S^1$ has only the symmetry group $U(1)$, and $S^2$ has $SO(3)$—neither is sufficient to accommodate the $\mathbb{R}^3$ sector structure of the Tripartite Tangent Bundle.
2. **Dimension:** Each sector $\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{I}$ corresponds, in the Tripartite Tangent Bundle, to an $\mathbb{R}^3$ fiber. The tangent space of $S^3$ is precisely $T_p S^3 \cong \mathbb{R}^3$, providing the geometric carrier for the "sector fiber."
3. **Compatibility with [Radius Ratio (GT-0.2.0.6)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.6):** In the [Radius Ratio (GT-0.2.0.6)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.6), we derived that the three-sector radius ratio is $1:1/\sqrt{3}:1/\sqrt{6}$. This ratio comes directly from the Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ via the area→radius conversion. Axiom 2 sets the three sphere radii precisely to this ratio.

**Why a product rather than a more complex bundle?**

The product structure means that the three sectors are independent at the "base space" level—they couple only through the constraint (Axiom 3) and at the tangent bundle level. This corresponds to the Tripartite Tangent Bundle as a direct sum decomposition:

$$
TM(a) = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I},
$$

where each of $\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{I}$ is a subbundle of $TM(a)$.

### Corollaries

> **Manifold Dimension (GT-0.3.0.4)** {#GT-0.3.0.4}
> 
> $M(a)$ is a $3 \times 3 = 9$-dimensional smooth manifold.

*Proof.* Each $S^3$ is a 3-dimensional manifold; the product of three $S^3$ is a $3+3+3 = 9$-dimensional manifold. ∎

> **Curvature Property (GT-0.3.0.5)** {#GT-0.3.0.5}
> 
> The scalar curvature of $M(a)$ is
> $$
> R_{M(a)} = \frac{6}{a^2} + \frac{6}{(a/\sqrt{3})^2} + \frac{6}{(a/\sqrt{6})^2} = \frac{6}{a^2}(1 + 3 + 6) = \frac{60}{a^2}.

*Proof.* The scalar curvature of $S^3$ of radius $r$ is $R_{S^3(r)} = 6/r^2$. The three spheres have radii $a$, $a/\sqrt{3}$, and $a/\sqrt{6}$ respectively; substituting each and summing yields the result. ∎

## 0.3.3 Axiom 3: Holographic Screen Constraint

### Motivation

[Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) defines three angular parameters, and [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3) defines the product geometry of three spheres. But the three angular parameters are not independent—[Λ=3 (GT-0.2.0.4)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.4) tells us the three sectors are interlocked ($\Lambda=3$), which implies that a constraint relation must exist among $\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I$.

Where does this constraint come from?—From the holographic principle: when the $\mathbb{R}^3$ sectors of the Tripartite Tangent Bundle project onto the two-dimensional Holographic Screen, the projection intensities must satisfy a completeness condition.

### Statement

> **Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)** {#GT-0.3.0.6}
> 
> Let $\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$ be the Projection Intensity Angles of the three sectors. They satisfy the completeness constraint:
> $$
> \theta_M + \theta_C + \theta_I = 90^\circ.
> $$

### Justification

**Why $90^\circ$ and not some other angle?**

The special role of $90^\circ$ comes from three independent reasons:

**Reason 1: Dimensional correspondence of the two-dimensional screen.**
The Holographic Screen $\Sigma$ is two-dimensional. When three sectors project from three-dimensional space onto a two-dimensional plane, the sum of projection intensity angles represents the "total projection coverage." When the total projection angle is exactly $90^\circ$:

- Each sector projects onto the screen forming a one-dimensional "shadow";
- The three one-dimensional shadows are linearly independent on the two-dimensional screen;
- Any point on the screen can be uniquely determined by the three projections.

**Reason 2: Ground state uniformity and numerical resonance.**
If the constraint is $90^\circ$, the uniform configuration is $30^\circ:30^\circ:30^\circ$. This value satisfies:
- $30^\circ$ is the trisection of $90^\circ$, corresponding to the fully symmetric ground state of the three sectors;
- The square root of the normalized spectral weight sum $\sum w_i = 9$, namely $\sqrt{9}=3$, is exactly equal to $90^\circ/30^\circ$, forming a numerically self-consistent resonance.

Note: here $90^\circ$ is not "derived" from ground state uniformity—ground state uniformity is merely an aesthetic motivation for the $90^\circ$ choice, not a logical proof. As an axiom, the ultimate justification of $90^\circ$ comes from the experimental verification of subsequent physical predictions (such as the fine-structure constant $\alpha = 1/137.036$).

**Reason 3: Compatibility with the spectral weight proportions.**
The Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ can be regarded as the "potential" of the three sectors. When the [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6) holds, the differentiation of the three sectors at the ground state angle $30^\circ$ is manifested precisely in the manner in which they deviate from $30^\circ$ (this will be developed in Chapter 0.4 through the Six-Term Cost Function).

**Important clarification: Ground state symmetry and spectral weight asymmetry are not contradictory.** The ground state $(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)$ corresponds to the coarsest resolution level of spectral weight $w_2=1$ ([Spectral Weight Ratio (GT-0.1.0.9)](./0.1_零维源点与S₃_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.1.0.9)), at which the three sectors are indistinguishable, hence the angles are equal. The Spectral Weight $6:2:1$ describes the differentiation potential in **excited states**: the Matter Sector weight of 6 means it can produce the greatest geometric structural differentiation when departing from the ground state, while the Information Sector weight of 1 means it remains closest to the ground state with the smallest variation. Ground state symmetry ≠ spectral weight symmetry—the two describe different resolution levels of information.

### Corollaries

> **Constrained Cross-Section Dimension (GT-0.3.0.7)** {#GT-0.3.0.7}
> 
> Under [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) and [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6), the parameter space reduces to a 2-dimensional Constrained Cross-Section:

*Proof.* The parameter space $\Theta$ given by [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) is 3-dimensional. The [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6) adds one independent constraint equation, reducing the dimension by 1; hence $\Sigma$ is a $3-1=2$-dimensional manifold. ∎

## 0.3.4 Independence Proof

Are the Three Axioms independent? That is, can any one axiom be derived from the other two? If so, it would not be an independent axiom but a theorem. We test independence by constructing models.

### Model A: Violate [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1), satisfy [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3) and [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6)

**Construction.** Let the parameter space be $\Theta = \{(\theta_M,\theta_C) \in \mathbb{R}^2_+ \mid \theta_M + \theta_C < 90^\circ\}$ (only **two** independent angular parameters). The geometric space remains the product of three $S^3$ spheres:

$$
M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{6}}\right).
$$

Set the holographic projection completeness condition $\theta_M + \theta_C + \theta_I = 90^\circ$, where $\theta_I = 90^\circ - \theta_M - \theta_C$ is a derived quantity from $\theta_M,\theta_C$ rather than an independent parameter.

**Verification.**
- [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3) ✅: The product geometry of three $S^3$ spheres exists.
- [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6) ✅: $\theta_M+\theta_C+\theta_I = 90^\circ$ holds ($\theta_I$ is a derived quantity).
- [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) ❌: The continuation map $\Omega$ should map the three spectral levels $\{V_1,V_2,V_3\}$ respectively to three independent parameters. But in this model $V_3$ has only the derived quantity $\theta_I$ without an independent parameter—the value of $\theta_I$ is entirely determined by $\theta_M,\theta_C$ and cannot vary independently. Hence $\Omega$ is not an embedding from discrete levels into an independent parameter space; condition 1 (one-to-one correspondence) is not satisfied.

**Conclusion:** [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) cannot be derived from the other two axioms.

### Model B: Violate [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3), satisfy [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) and [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6)

**Construction.** Let the parameter space be $\Theta = \mathbb{R}^3_+$ with three angular parameters ([Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1)). - [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) ✅: Three angular parameters exist.
- [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6) ✅: Angular constraint exists.
- [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3) ❌: The geometric space is not a product of spheres.

**Conclusion:** [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3) cannot be derived from the other two axioms.

### Model C: Violate [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6), satisfy [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) and [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3)

## 0.3.5 Self-Consistency Verification

Independence guarantees that each axiom has independent content. But are the Three Axioms self-consistent when all hold simultaneously? That is, does there exist at least one geometric configuration satisfying all three axioms?

> **Self-Consistency (GT-0.3.0.9)** {#GT-0.3.0.9}
> 
> The Three Axioms, when all hold, constitute a self-consistent axiomatic system.

*Proof.* We construct a concrete geometric realization to prove self-consistency.

Take the product sphere from [Product Sphere Geometry (GT-0.3.0.3)](#GT-0.3.0.3):

$$
M(a) = S^3(a) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \times S^3\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{6}}\right).
$$

On the tangent bundle $TM(a)$ of $M(a)$, consider the Tripartite Tangent Bundle decomposition $TM(a) = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$. On each sector $\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{I}$, define Projection Intensity Angles $\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$ satisfying the [Holographic Screen Constraint (GT-0.3.0.6)](#GT-0.3.0.6):

$$
\theta_M + \theta_C + \theta_I = 90^\circ.
$$

Define the continuation map $\Omega: \{V_1,V_2,V_3\} \to (\theta_M,\theta_C,\theta_I)$ as follows:
- $V_1 \mapsto \theta_M \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$;
- $V_2 \mapsto \theta_C \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$;
- $V_3 \mapsto \theta_I \in (0^\circ, 90^\circ)$.

This map is surjective and one-to-one, satisfying conditions 1 and 3 of [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1). The order-preserving property (condition 2) is realized through the radius ratio of the product spheres: from the [Radius Ratio (GT-0.2.0.6)](./0.2_谱展开与互锁常数_CN_260713.1.md#GT-0.2.0.6), the radius ratio $r_0:r_1:r_2 = \sqrt{6}:\sqrt{2}:1 \propto \sqrt{w_0}:\sqrt{w_1}:\sqrt{w_2}$, i.e., $r_i \propto \sqrt{w_i}$. The larger the geometric radius of sector $i$, the more significant the "influence amplitude" produced in geometry when $\theta_i$ varies—the order-preserving property is encoded through the ratio of geometric radii, not through the numerical range of $\theta_i$. Thus, all three conditions of [Tripartite Continuation (GT-0.3.0.1)](#GT-0.3.0.1) are satisfied.

Therefore, there exists a geometric configuration $(\theta_M,\theta_C,\theta_I) \in \Sigma$ simultaneously satisfying the Three Axioms. The axiomatic system is self-consistent. ∎
