## 0.2.2 Interlocking Constant $\Lambda(S_3)$

### 0.2.2.1 Conceptual Introduction

Spectral unfolding decomposes the symmetry of $S_3$ into a three-level hierarchy. But an important question arises: **how many "sectors" (mutually indistinguishable internal directions) does this symmetry actually correspond to?**

For any finite group $G$, the **number of conjugacy classes** gives the number of groups into which group elements are partitioned by permutation equivalence. This number determines how many "independent directions" the symmetry requires in its geometric realization—because elements belonging to the same conjugacy class can be transformed into one another via internal transformations and cannot be distinguished by any physical process.

We define:

**Interlocking Constant (GT-0.2.0.1)** {#GT-0.2.0.1}

Let $G$ be a finite group. The **Interlocking Constant** $\Lambda(G)$ of $G$ is the number of conjugacy classes of $G$:
$$
\Lambda(G) := \#\{\text{conjugacy classes of } G\}.
$$

The meaning of the term "interlocking" will emerge in subsequent chapters: $\Lambda(G)$ determines the number of sectors of the geometric structure, and the interlocking constraints among these sectors are precisely the source of quantitative predictions throughout Geometric Theory.

### 0.2.2.2 Definition and Computation of Conjugacy Classes

**Conjugacy Class (GT-0.2.0.2)** {#GT-0.2.0.2}

Let $G$ be a finite group. Two elements $a,b \in G$ are said to be **conjugate** (denoted $a \sim b$) if there exists $g \in G$ such that
$$
b = g^{-1} a g.
$$
Conjugacy is an equivalence relation on $G$. Each equivalence class is called a **conjugacy class**.

This means: if two elements can be transformed into each other by "rotating" them through elements of the group, they belong to the same conjugacy class.

We now compute for $S_3$. Recall the six elements of $S_3$:

$$
S_3 = \{e,\ (12),\ (13),\ (23),\ (123),\ (132)\},
$$

where:
- $e$ is the identity permutation
- $(12), (13), (23)$ are three transpositions (swapping two elements), each of order 2
- $(123), (132)$ are two 3-cycles, each of order 3

We check conjugacy relations element by element.

**Step 1: The conjugacy class of the identity $e$.**
For any $g \in S_3$,
$$
g^{-1} e g = g^{-1} g = e.
$$
Hence $e$ is conjugate only to itself. The identity $e$ alone forms a conjugacy class:
$$
\mathcal C_0 = \{e\}.
$$

**Step 2: Verify that the three transpositions belong to the same conjugacy class.**
Take $(12)$ and $(13)$, and check whether there exists $g$ such that $g^{-1}(12)g = (13)$.

Consider $g = (23)$:
$$
(23)^{-1}(12)(23).
$$
Note that the inverse of a transposition is itself: $(23)^{-1} = (23)$.
We need to compute $(23)(12)(23)$.

In permutation groups, conjugation has an intuitive geometric meaning: $g^{-1}(ij)g = (g(i)\ g(j))$. That is, $g$ simultaneously permutes both indices of $(ij)$.

Hence:
$$
(23)^{-1}(12)(23) = (23)(12)(23) = (13).
$$
So $(12)$ and $(13)$ are conjugate.

Similarly, using $g = (12)$:
$$
(12)^{-1}(13)(12) = (12)(13)(12) = (23).
$$
So $(13)$ and $(23)$ are conjugate.

By transitivity of the conjugacy relation, all three transpositions lie in the same conjugacy class:
$$
\mathcal C_1 = \{(12), (13), (23)\}.
$$

**Step 3: Verify that the two 3-cycles belong to the same conjugacy class.**
Take $(123)$ and $(132)$. Using $g = (12)$:
$$
g^{-1}(123)g = (12)(123)(12) = (132).
$$
Thus the two 3-cycles are conjugate:
$$
\mathcal C_2 = \{(123), (132)\}.
$$

**Step 4: Confirm no further merging.**
- The identity cannot be conjugate to any non-identity element (since conjugation preserves order: if $a \sim b$ then $|a| = |b|$, and $|e| = 1 \neq 2,3$)
- Transpositions have order 2, 3-cycles have order 3; different orders preclude conjugacy

Thus the three conjugacy classes are mutually disjoint and cover all six elements of $S_3$:
$$
S_3 = \mathcal C_0 \cup \mathcal C_1 \cup \mathcal C_2.
$$

**Summary table of results:**

| Conjugacy Class | Elements | Order | Size |
|:---:|:---|:---:|:---:|
| $\mathcal C_0$ | $\{e\}$ | 1 | 1 |
| $\mathcal C_1$ | $\{(12),(13),(23)\}$ | 2 | 3 |
| $\mathcal C_2$ | $\{(123),(132)\}$ | 3 | 2 |

### 0.2.2.3 Numerical Value of the Interlocking Constant

From [Interlocking Constant (GT-0.2.0.1)](#GT-0.2.0.1) and the above computation:

$$
\Lambda(S_3) = \#\{\mathcal C_0, \mathcal C_1, \mathcal C_2\} = 3.
$$

**Λ=3 (GT-0.2.0.4)** {#GT-0.2.0.4}

The Interlocking Constant $\Lambda(S_3) = 3$. This indicates that the $S_3$ symmetry corresponds to three independent geometric sectors.

**Why 3 and not some other number?** This value follows directly from the group structure of $S_3$:
- The identity always occupies its own class (1 element)
- The structure of the three transpositions forces them to merge into one class (3 elements)
- The structure of the two 3-cycles forces them to merge into another class (2 elements)
- No other possibilities exist

This means: **as soon as we accept that the Zero-Dimensional Source Point carries $S_3$ symmetry, $\Lambda = 3$ is forced.** This is a purely mathematical, non-negotiable result, not an assumption.

## 0.2.3 Compactness Function $k_0(S_3)$

### 0.2.3.1 Conceptual Introduction

Another important structural piece of information from spectral unfolding is: **how many levels does the hierarchy have?** Concretely: how many "rungs" are there between the finest level ($\{e\}$) and the coarsest level ($S_3$)?

As we have seen, the normal subgroup chain of $S_3$ is $\{e\} \lhd A_3 \lhd S_3$. The length of the chain (excluding the trivial level) is 2. But chain length is not a standard invariant in group theory; we need a more precise characterization.

For a finite group $G$, the index of its **maximal proper normal subgroup** gives the "first-level compression" factor in the spectral unfolding—i.e., by what factor information is compressed when going from the full group $S_3$ to the first non-trivial normal subgroup $A_3$. This factor determines the "stride length" of the hierarchy.

**Compactness Function (GT-0.2.0.3)** {#GT-0.2.0.3}

Let $G$ be a finite non-trivial group. The **Compactness Function** $k_0(G)$ of $G$ is the index of the maximal proper normal subgroup of $G$:
$$
k_0(G) := [G : N_{\max}],
$$
where $N_{\max}$ is the largest normal subgroup of $G$ properly contained in $G$ (i.e., the normal subgroup of largest order other than $G$ itself). If $G$ has no non-trivial normal subgroups (i.e., $G$ is simple), define $k_0(G) = 1$.

### 0.2.3.2 Finding the Maximal Proper Normal Subgroup of $S_3$

**Step 1: List all normal subgroups of $S_3$.**

This exhaustive enumeration was already carried out in §0.1.2 of Chapter 0.1. We briefly review the verification:

$S_3$ has six subgroups:
- Order 1: $\{e\}$
- Order 2: $\{e, (12)\}$, $\{e, (13)\}$, $\{e, (23)\}$
- Order 3: $A_3 = \{e, (123), (132)\}$
- Order 6: $S_3$ itself

Testing each subgroup for normality (i.e., whether $g^{-1}Hg = H$ for all $g \in S_3$):

- $\{e\}$: $g^{-1} e g = e$, hence $\{e\}$ is normal (trivial normal subgroup).
- Order-2 subgroups: Test $\{e, (12)\}$. Take $g = (123)$:
  $$
  (123)^{-1}(12)(123) = (132)(12)(123) = (23) \notin \{e, (12)\}.
  $$
  Hence $\{e, (12)\}$ is not normal. Symmetry likewise excludes $\{e, (13)\}$ and $\{e, (23)\}$. All three order-2 subgroups are non-normal.
- $A_3$: A subgroup of index 2 in $S_3$; any index-2 subgroup is necessarily normal. Hence $A_3$ is normal.
- $S_3$: The full group is always normal (trivial normal subgroup).

Thus the normal subgroups of $S_3$ are:
$$
\{\{e\},\ A_3,\ S_3\}.
$$

**Step 2: Identify the maximal proper normal subgroup.**

"Maximal proper normal subgroup" means the normal subgroup of largest order, other than $S_3$ itself.

Comparing $\{e\}$ (order 1) and $A_3$ (order 3):
- $|A_3| = 3 > 1 = |\{e\}|$

Hence $A_3$ is the maximal proper normal subgroup of $S_3$.

### 0.2.3.3 Computing the Index

The index $[S_3 : A_3]$ is given by Lagrange's Theorem:
$$
[S_3 : A_3] = \frac{|S_3|}{|A_3|} = \frac{6}{3} = 2.
$$

Therefore:
$$
k_0(S_3) = [S_3 : A_3] = 2.
$$

**k₀=2 (GT-0.2.0.5)** {#GT-0.2.0.5}

The Compactness Function $k_0(S_3) = 2$. This indicates that the spectral unfolding of $S_3$ symmetry possesses a two-level structure (even/odd dichotomy).

**Why 2?** Because the only non-trivial normal subgroup of $S_3$, namely $A_3$, has index 2—this is determined by the group structure of $S_3$ itself, with no room for choice.

### 0.2.4.1 Sector Significance of $\Lambda = 3$

The transition from $\Lambda = 3$ conjugacy classes to "sector count = 3" is not a pure group-theoretic theorem but a **correspondence postulate**.

In group representation theory, the number of conjugacy classes equals the number of irreducible representations (via character theory), but irreducible representations correspond to "independent modes of symmetry" and are not directly synonymous with "geometric sectors." Interpreting the number of conjugacy classes as the number of geometric sectors requires an additional, explicit correspondence postulate:

> **Sector-Conjugacy Class Correspondence (GT-0.2.0.7)** {#GT-0.2.0.7}
> 
> In Geometric Theory, the number of geometric sectors of a symmetry group $G$ is defined as the number of conjugacy classes $\Lambda(G)$ of $G$. That is, the number of independently distinguishable "directions" in the geometric realization equals the number of conjugacy classes in group theory.

This is a **postulate** (a correspondence rule) of the theory, not a conclusion that flows naturally from group theory. Its justification lies in the fact that elements of the same conjugacy class can be transformed into one another via conjugation and therefore cannot be distinguished by any internal transformation in the geometric realization—hence they should be regarded as a single sector. Elements belonging to different conjugacy classes have no such internal transformation pathway connecting them, and therefore correspond to distinct sectors.

3 conjugacy classes → 3 sectors. Each sector corresponds to an independent geometric "direction."

> **Important note:** If future experiments deviate from the physical predictions of Geometric Theory, this correspondence rule is among the first fundamental assumptions that should be re-examined.

$k_0 = 2$ is neither the "number of levels" (there are 2 steps) nor a uniform stride length (the stride is not constant: the first step compresses by a factor of 2, the second by a factor of 3). $k_0 = 2$ specifically represents: **the information compression ratio from the full group to the first-level normal subgroup**.

In subsequent geometric realizations, the significance of this value is: the first-level internal structure of the sectors has two "sub-levels"—i.e., the dichotomy between the outer layer (the 6-element information of $S_3$) and the inner layer (the 3-element information of $A_3$).

> **Explanation:** If the 6 elements of $S_3$ are regarded as the full symmetry, and the 3 elements of $A_3$ as the "even-permutation part," then $k_0=2$ means that the information is halved when going from the full symmetry to the even-permutation part—this is the outermost structural feature of the $S_3$ spectral unfolding.

## 0.2.5 From the Discrete to the Continuous: Area → Radius Mapping

### 0.2.5.1 Statement of the Problem

The current output of spectral unfolding is **discrete**: three integer weights $6, 2, 1$ and two integer constants $3, 2$. But the goal of Geometric Theory is to describe the physical world using **continuous geometric structures**. We therefore need a method to map these discrete numbers onto continuous geometric quantities.

This mapping cannot be arbitrarily specified—it must respect the internal logic of spectral unfolding. Concretely, we must answer: **what geometric quantity do the spectral weights $w_i$ correspond to?**

### 0.2.5.2 Information Capacity → Area (Core Assumption)

In [Spectral Hierarchy Space (GT-0.1.0.4)](./0.1_零维源点与S₃.md#GT-0.1.0.4), the spectral weights $w_i$ are interpreted as the dimensions of the $i$-th spectral hierarchy space. This dimension characterizes "how much independent information that level can carry."

We now need to "translate" this discrete integer information capacity into some quantity in continuous geometry. This translation is **not a derivation**; it is the stipulation of a correspondence rule.

The spectral weights $w_i$ are the **only discrete numbers** output by spectral unfolding. A geometric realization requires continuous measures (area, volume, etc.). The most natural correspondence is: a larger information capacity corresponds to a larger geometric measure.

> **Area-Weight Correspondence (GT-0.2.0.8)** {#GT-0.2.0.8}
> 
> In the geometric realization of spectral unfolding, the geometric cross-sectional area $A_i$ of the $i$-th level is proportional to its spectral weight $w_i$:
> $$
> A_i \propto w_i.
> $$

**This assumption cannot be derived from group theory.** It is a **correspondence rule** from discrete information capacity to continuous geometry. The spectral weights $w_i$ are integers (6, 2, 1), whereas geometric area is a continuous quantity. A mapping between the two necessarily requires an additional correspondence postulate.

**Why proportionality rather than some other functional form?** Because spectral unfolding itself provides no information about the functional form. In the absence of additional information, the most conservative choice is linear proportionality—it introduces the fewest free parameters (only one proportionality constant). Any non-linear mapping (such as $A_i \propto w_i^2$ or $A_i \propto \log w_i$) would introduce an additional exponent parameter that spectral unfolding cannot determine.

**The status of this assumption:** It belongs to the "core correspondence rules" of the theory, not to its mathematical theorems. If subsequent physical predictions disagree with experiment, this assumption is among the first to be scrutinized. Its ultimate correctness is established not by mathematical proof, but by the sustained agreement of the physical predictions it produces with experimental data.

### 0.2.5.3 Area → Radius (Core Assumption, Requiring Clarification)

In ordinary Euclidean geometry, the surface area of a $d$-dimensional sphere $S^{d-1}$ satisfies $A \propto r^{d-1}$. But there is a fundamental distinction here: **the embedding dimension of the geometric realization manifold is not determined by the dimension of the spectral hierarchy space.** The spectral hierarchy space $\mathcal H_i = L^2(S_3/H_i)$ is an abstract function space over a discrete quotient set—its dimension $w_i$ is understood in the information-theoretic sense (the number of independent basis functions), not as a geometric embedding dimension. The geometric realization space is an independent construction whose dimension is a degree of freedom of the theory.

If one mistakenly takes $\dim \mathcal H_0 = 6$ as the embedding dimension, area would satisfy $A \propto r^{5}$, yielding a radius ratio of $\sqrt[5]{6} : \sqrt[5]{2} : 1$, incompatible with subsequent physical predictions. This precisely illustrates that: **the dimension of the geometric realization space can only be determined by subsequent physical constraints, and cannot be naively inherited from the algebraic dimension of the spectral hierarchy space.**

**Crucial clarification (Commentary on GT-0.2.0.9):** The "area" here is a continuous geometric representation of information capacity—an independently constructed quantity unrelated to the embedding dimension of $\mathcal H_i$. It reflects the cross-sectional measure of "how much independent information this level can accommodate" in its geometric realization, not the hypersurface area of some pre-existing manifold. $\beta=2$ means that this measure scales according to the two-dimensional area law $A \propto r^2$.

Reasons for choosing $\beta=2$ (here $\beta$ is used in place of $n$, consistent with the notation of the sensitivity analysis in §0.2.6.5):

1. **Separation of geometric realization space from spectral hierarchy space.** The spectral hierarchy space is an algebraic object (a function space over a finite quotient set); the geometric realization space is the continuous manifold we aim to construct. The mapping between the two (namely, the Area-Weight Correspondence GT-0.2.0.8) has already introduced one degree of freedom. On top of that, the area-to-radius mapping exponent $\beta$ is a second, independent degree of freedom. $\beta=2$ corresponds to mapping information capacity to a characteristic radius via a two-dimensional area law—this is the most direct choice, since the ultimate physical spacetime is 3+1 dimensional and information-bearing surfaces should naturally be two-dimensional (e.g., holographic screens).

2. **Principle of Minimal Free Parameters.** If one writes $A \propto r^\beta$, then $\beta$ is a free parameter. Spectral unfolding itself provides no information about $\beta$. $\beta=2$ introduces the fewest additional assumptions—it corresponds in Euclidean geometry to the standard surface area formula $A = 4\pi r^2$ for a 2-sphere (up to a constant factor). Any choice $\beta \neq 2$ introduces an additional free parameter that spectral unfolding cannot determine.

3. **Subsequent verification.** Whether the choice $\beta=2$ is correct will be tested in subsequent chapters through the self-consistency of physical predictions (see Chapter 0.5, the Spectral Rigidity Theorem, and Volume 2, the Dimensional Bridge). If $\beta \neq 2$ leads to disagreement with experiment, this assumption must be revised. Section §0.2.6.5 provides a complete sensitivity analysis of the radius ratio under different $(\alpha,\beta)$ combinations.

> **Radius-Area Relation (GT-0.2.0.9)** {#GT-0.2.0.9}

- $\alpha=1,\beta=2$ yields $\sqrt{6}:\sqrt{2}:1 \approx 2.449:1.414:1$, which numerically lies between the spectral weight ratio $6:2:1$ and the uniform ratio $1:1:1$
- If $\alpha=1,\beta\to\infty$, the radius ratio tends to $1:1:1$ (complete information homogenization)
- If $\alpha\to\infty,\beta=2$, the radius ratio tends to $6:2:1$ (fully preserving the discrete structure)

**Conclusion:** The choice $(\alpha,\beta)=(1,2)$ adopted in this chapter is not the only possible choice, but it is the choice that **introduces the fewest free parameters** (zero additional parameters). All other choices introduce at least one free exponent parameter that spectral unfolding itself cannot determine. The correctness of $(1,2)$ ultimately depends on experimental tests of subsequent physical predictions.

## 0.2.8 Open Problems and Subsequent Directions

### Open Problems

1. **Is the exponent $\beta=2$ in the area-radius relation mandatory?** The [Radius-Area Relation (GT-0.2.0.9)](#GT-0.2.0.9) chooses $\beta=2$ based on the Principle of Minimal Free Parameters. However, if the spectral hierarchy spaces are embedded in a higher-dimensional space (e.g., if $\mathcal H_0$ is a 6-dimensional space), the area-radius relation could be $A \propto r^{5}$. This would lead to a different radius ratio ($\sqrt[5]{6} : \sqrt[5]{2} : 1$ instead of $\sqrt{6} : \sqrt{2} : 1$). The sensitivity analysis of §0.2.6.5 displays the consequences of different $(\alpha,\beta)$ combinations.

2. **Alternative realizations of the [Scale Ratio Implementation Rule (GT-0.2.0.10)](#GT-0.2.0.10).** Concentric spheres are not the only geometric realization of a three-level hierarchy. Do there exist other geometric realizations (such as ellipsoids, tori, etc.) that also satisfy $6:2:1$ and $\Lambda=3, k_0=2$? Would these alternatives lead to different physical predictions?

3. **Generalization of $\Lambda$ and $k_0$ to other groups.** This chapter computed these two constants only for $S_3$. If the theory needs to be extended to other symmetry groups (such as $S_4$ or other Cayley groups), $\Lambda$ and $k_0$ would take different values, leading to different spectral unfolding structures.
