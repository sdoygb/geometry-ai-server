## Chapter 2 Axiomatization of the Excited-State Parameter Space

### 2.1 Axiom 1 (Topology of the Parameter Space)

**Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** The excited-state parameter space $D$ is obtained from the one-dimensional compact connected manifold $S^1$ (the circle) by removing two distinct points $p_0$ and $p_*$:

$$D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}, \qquad p_0 \neq p_*$$

The two removed points are called the **vacuum point** ($p_0$) and the **degenerate point** ($p_*$). Consequently, $D$ consists of two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1), \qquad D_- \cong (0,1)$$

Each component is homeomorphic to an open interval. The parameter $x \in D_\pm$ labels the excited states.

**Lemma 2.1 (Topological Structure of the Components)** Let $S^1$ be the standard unit circle $\{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi \in [0,2\pi)\}$. Removing two points $p_0=e^{i\phi_0}$ and $p_*=e^{i\phi_*}$ (assume without loss of generality $0\leq \phi_0 < \phi_* < 2\pi$), the set $S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}$ has exactly two connected components:
$$D_+ = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_0 < \phi < \phi_*\}, \quad D_- = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_* < \phi < \phi_0+2\pi\}.$$
Each component, with the subspace topology inherited from $S^1$, is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0,1)$.

*Proof.* Consider the standard covering map $\pi: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $\pi(\phi)=e^{i\phi}$. On $\mathbb{R}$, $\pi^{-1}(S^1\setminus\{p_0,p_*\}) = \mathbb{R}\setminus\{\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi \mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}$. Each connected component of this set is an open interval $(\phi_0+2k\pi, \phi_*+2k\pi)$ or $(\phi_*+2k\pi, \phi_0+2(k+1)\pi)$. Since the restriction of $\pi$ to any interval of length $2\pi$ is a homeomorphism, the images of these open intervals in $S^1$ are precisely $D_+$ and $D_-$. Each open interval is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, hence $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$. $\square$

### 2.2 Axiom 2 (Analytic Properties of the Geometric Quantity)

**Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** On the parameter space $D$, there exists a geometric quantity $S: D \to (0,+\infty)$ satisfying:

1. **Continuity**: $S$ is continuous on each connected component $D_\pm$;
2. **Vacuum Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_0$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$;
3. **Degeneracy Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_*$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$.

**Remark 2.1** Axiom 2 does not require $S$ to have the same functional expression on $D_+$ and $D_-$, nor does it require $S$ to be continuous on all of $D$ ($p_0$ and $p_*$ are not in $D$). Thus $S$ may be different functions on the two independent components, so long as each satisfies the boundary conditions.

### 2.3 Range Theorem

**Theorem 2.1 (Range)** Under Axioms 1 and 2, the range of $S$ on each connected component $D_\pm$ is $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$.

*Proof.* By Axiom 1, $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is connected. By Axiom 2, $S$ is continuous on $D_\pm$, hence the image $S(D_\pm)$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e., an interval. By the vacuum limit, $\inf S(D_\pm) = 0$; by the degeneracy limit, $\sup S(D_\pm) = +\infty$. Since $p_0, p_* \notin D$, the endpoints $0$ and $+\infty$ are not attained. Therefore $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed Explanation:** The connectedness argument may be further expanded as follows. Let $D_+ \cong (0,1)$, and $S: D_+ \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Suppose $S(D_+)$ is not an interval; then there exist $\alpha < \beta < \gamma$ such that $\alpha, \gamma \in S(D_+)$ but $\beta \notin S(D_+)$. Set $U = S^{-1}((-\infty, \beta))$ and $V = S^{-1}((\beta, +\infty))$; then $U,V$ are disjoint open sets in $D_+$ with $U\cup V = D_+$, contradicting the connectedness of $D_+$. Hence $S(D_+)$ must be an interval. Combined with the boundary limits, this interval must be $(0,+\infty)$.

### 2.4 Existence Theorem and Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis

**Theorem 2.2 (Existence)** For any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, on each component $D_+$ and $D_-$ there exists at least one excited state $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$ such that $S(x_+) = S(x_-) = S_0$.

*Proof.* By Theorem 2.1, $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. For any $S_0 > 0$, clearly $S_0 \in (0, +\infty)$. Hence $S^{-1}(S_0) \cap D_\pm \neq \varnothing$. $\square$

**Definition 2.1' (Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis)** If $S$ is strictly monotonic on $D_\pm$, then for any $S_0>0$, the $x_+$ and $x_-$ in Theorem 2.2 are uniquely determined. Strict monotonicity is **not** a theorem derived from Axioms 1–2, but a **constructive hypothesis** of the framework. Its introduction is equivalent to requiring $S$ to constitute a global coordinate function on $D_\pm$.

*Proof.* A strictly monotonic continuous function is injective, hence the preimage is unique. $\square$

## Chapter 3 Constrained Product Spheres and Spectral Rigidity

### 3.0 Group-Theoretic Origin of the Interlocking Functions

**Lemma 3.0 (Rigidity of the Tripartite Tangent Bundle Permutation Group)** Let $TM = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$ be the tripartite tangent bundle, and $G$ its sector permutation symmetry group. If $G$ acts faithfully and transitively on the three sectors, and contains all pairwise transpositions, then $G \cong S_3$, and $S_3$ is the unique finite group satisfying these conditions.

*Proof.* A faithful permutation group on three objects must be a subgroup of $S_3$. Transitivity requires an orbit of size 3, hence $|G| \geq 3$. The transitive subgroups of $S_3$ are only $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (cyclic) and $S_3$ itself. If transpositions (odd permutations) are required, $A_3$ is insufficient; the only possibility is $S_3$. $\square$

**Definition 3.0 (Interlocking Function and Compactness Function)** Let $G$ be a finite group. Define:
- **Interlocking function** $\Lambda(G) := |\text{Conj}(G)|$ (the number of conjugacy classes, which by the fundamental theorem of representation theory equals the number of irreducible complex representations of $G$);
- **Compactness function** $k_0(G) := [G : N_{\max}]$ (the index of the maximal normal subgroup).

**Proposition 3.0** For the permutation symmetry group $G = S_3$ of the tripartite tangent bundle $TM = \mathcal{M} \oplus \mathcal{C} \oplus \mathcal{I}$:
$$\Lambda(S_3) = 3, \quad k_0(S_3) = 2.$$

*Proof.* The conjugacy class decomposition of $S_3$ is: identity $\{e\}$ (1 element), transpositions $\{(12),(13),(23)\}$ (3 elements), 3-cycles $\{(123),(132)\}$ (2 elements), totaling 3 conjugacy classes, hence $\Lambda(S_3)=|\text{Conj}(S_3)|=3$.

The normal subgroups of $S_3$ are only $\{e\}$ and $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (the alternating subgroup). The maximal normal subgroup is $A_3$, with index $[S_3 : A_3] = 6/3 = 2$, hence $k_0(S_3)=2$. $\square$

**Remark 3.0** By Lemma 3.0, $S_3$ is the unique choice for the tripartite tangent bundle permutation symmetry group. The interlocking constants $\Lambda=3$ and $k_0=2$ are not freely chosen parameters but rather the necessary values of the group-theoretic functions evaluated on $S_3$. The reason Tenfold Geometry locks $\Lambda=3$, $k_0=2$ is rooted in its group structure being $S_3$ (three sectors), and Lemma 3.0 has already proved the uniqueness of this group choice.

**Theorem 3.0' (Rigidity of Tripartite Tangent Bundle Sector Permutation)** Let $M = S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$ be equipped with the product metric, and suppose there exists a tangent bundle decomposition $TM = \mathcal{M}\oplus\mathcal{C}\oplus\mathcal{I}$ such that the three sectors are fully equivalent at the metric level (i.e., any permutation induces an isometric automorphism). Then:
1. The sector permutation group $G$ of this decomposition must contain all pairwise transpositions;
2. The finite group satisfying (1) is uniquely isomorphic to $S_3$.

Consequently, $\Lambda(G)=|\text{Conj}(S_3)|=3$ and $k_0(G)=[S_3:A_3]=2$ are necessary outputs of the group-theoretic functions.

*Proof.* (1) If the three sectors are metrically equivalent, then any transposition (e.g., $\mathcal{M}\leftrightarrow\mathcal{C}$) preserves the product metric and hence induces an isometric automorphism. Therefore all pairwise transpositions belong to $G$.

(2) By Lemma 3.0, a transitive permutation group containing transpositions can only be $S_3$. Hence $G\cong S_3$ is not an artificial choice but a necessary consequence of non-trivial permutations under the tri-equivalence structure. $\square$

**Proof.** On a product manifold, the Laplace operator separates as $\Delta_g = \Delta_1 + \Delta_2 + \Delta_3$, where $\Delta_i$ is the Laplace operator on each factor. It is known that on $S^3(R)$, the eigenvalue corresponding to spherical harmonics of degree $p$ is $p(p+2)/R^2$; hence the total eigenvalue is the sum of the three. $\square$

By separating the Euclidean Laplacian into radial and angular parts, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4} = \partial_r^2 + \frac{3}{r}\partial_r + \frac{1}{r^2}\Delta_{S^3}$. For a homogeneous function of degree $p$, $f(r,\theta)=r^p Y(\theta)$, $\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^4}f=0$ yields $\Delta_{S^3}Y = -p(p+2)Y$. ### 3.5 Recovering the Scale Factor from the First Three Eigenvalues

**Theorem 3.3 (Inversion Formula)** Given the first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues $\lambda_1^\Delta$, $\lambda_2^\Delta$, $\lambda_3^\Delta$ of $M(a)$, the scale factors can be uniquely recovered:

$$a = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_1^\Delta}}, \quad b = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\lambda_3^\Delta}}, \quad c = \frac{a}{\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\Lambda k_0 \cdot \lambda_1^\Delta}}.$$

**Self-Consistency Condition** A triple of positive numbers $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ are the first three non-zero eigenvalues of some $M(a)$ if and only if

$$\lambda_2 = \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda_3 = \Lambda(S_3) \lambda_1 = 3\lambda_1.$$

In this case $a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1}$.

**Proof.** By Theorem 3.2, $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2 \Rightarrow a = \sqrt{3/\lambda_1^\Delta}$; $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3/b^2 \Rightarrow b = \sqrt{3/\lambda_3^\Delta}$; by the ratio constraint of Definition 3.1, $c = a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$. The self-consistency condition follows directly from $\lambda_2^\Delta = 8/a^2$ and $\lambda_3^\Delta = 3\Lambda/a^2$. $\square$

**Lemma 3.1 (One-to-One Correspondence between Spectral Data and Geometry)** Under the premise that the interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$ are locked by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, there exists an explicit one-to-one map between the first three non-zero Laplace eigenvalues and the scale factors $(a,b,c)$. Given $\lambda_1^\Delta$, $\lambda_2^\Delta$, $\lambda_3^\Delta$, the triple $(a,b,c)$ can be uniquely recovered; conversely, given $(a,b,c)$, the first three eigenvalues can be uniquely computed.

### 3.6 Spectral Rigidity Theorem

**Theorem 3.4 (Spectral Rigidity of Constrained Product Spheres)** Let $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ be two constrained product spheres with the same interlocking constants $(\Lambda=\Lambda(S_3)=3, k_0=k_0(S_3)=2, \ell_0>0)$. If their Laplace–Beltrami operators share the same first three non-zero eigenvalues, then $M(a)$ and $M(a')$ are isometric.

**Proof.** By Proposition 3.2, the first non-zero Laplace eigenvalue is $\lambda_1^\Delta=3/a^2$. If $M(a')$ is isospectral to $M(a)$, then $\lambda_1^{\Delta}(a')=\lambda_1^{\Delta}(a)$, i.e., $3/a'^2=3/a^2$. Since $a,a'>0$, we obtain $a'=a$. By the ratio constraints of Definition 3.1, $b=a/\sqrt{\Lambda}$ and $c=a/\sqrt{\Lambda k_0}$, the scale triple $(a,b,c)$ is uniquely determined, hence $M(a')$ and $M(a)$ are isometric. $\square$

**Remark 3.1** The root of the spectral rigidity in this theorem is that the moduli space is parametrized by a single scale factor. Since the isometry classes of constrained product spheres satisfying Definition 3.1 with locked interlocking constants are uniquely labeled by $a$, and the first three Laplace eigenvalues (in fact, the first one alone suffices) uniquely determine $a$, two manifolds sharing the same first three eigenvalues must belong to the same isometry class.

**Remark 3.2 (Completeness of the First Eigenvalue)** In the single-parameter family $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ with locked interlocking constants, the moduli space is one-dimensional. The first non-zero eigenvalue $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2$ itself constitutes a complete coordinate for the moduli space (bijective with $a$). The second and third among the first three eigenvalues provide a self-consistency check (they must satisfy the ratios $8/3$ and $\Lambda$), and serve as the spectral signature of the class, used to determine whether a given manifold belongs to this class.

### 3.7 Compatibility with Classical Counterexamples

**Theorem 3.5** Sunada-type isospectral non-isometric counterexamples do not exist in the constrained product sphere class.

**Proof.** The Sunada method requires a non-trivial fundamental group and finite coverings. $M(a)$ is the product $S^3 \times S^3 \times S^3$. Since $S^3$ is simply connected, the product is also simply connected, with trivial fundamental group. Hence the Sunada method is inapplicable in this class. $\square$

### 3.8 Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of Riemannian manifolds and $\mathcal{M}$ its moduli space (the set of isometry classes). Assume $\mathcal{M}$ possesses a smooth manifold structure of dimension $d$. Define the spectral map $\sigma_k$ sending each isometry class to the vector of its first $k$ non-zero Laplace eigenvalues:

$$\sigma_k: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^k, \quad [g] \mapsto (\lambda_1^\Delta, \lambda_2^\Delta, \dots, \lambda_k^\Delta).$$

**Theorem 3.6 (Dimensional Necessary Condition for Spectral Rigidity)** Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the moduli space of a class of manifolds, of finite dimension $d$. If the first $k$ Laplace eigenvalues suffice to achieve spectral rigidity (i.e., $\sigma_k$ is injective), then necessarily $d \leq k$. Under the interlocking constants determined by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0, the moduli space of $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ has dimension $d=1$, so only $k\geq1$ is required to satisfy the necessary condition.

**Proof.** Suppose $d > k$. Since eigenvalues depend continuously on the metric [BBG94], the spectral map $\sigma_k$ is continuous. If $\sigma_k$ is injective, then $\sigma_k$ provides a continuous injection from the $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ into $\mathbb{R}^k$.

Take an open neighborhood $U \subset \mathcal{M}$ of any point in a local coordinate chart, such that $U$ is homeomorphic to an open set in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Consider the restriction $\sigma_k|_U: U \to \mathbb{R}^k$, which is a continuous injection. Let $i: \mathbb{R}^k \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^d$ be the standard inclusion map (padding with $d-k$ zero coordinates). Then the composite map

$$i \circ \sigma_k|_U: U \to \mathbb{R}^d$$

is a continuous injection from the open subset $U$ of $\mathbb{R}^d$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$ (both domain and codomain are $\mathbb{R}^d$).

By the **Brouwer Invariance of Domain Theorem** [Brouwer1912]: If $V \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is open and $f: V \to \mathbb{R}^d$ is a continuous injection, then $f(V)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Applying this to $V = U$ and $f = i \circ \sigma_k|_U$, we obtain that $(i \circ \sigma_k|_U)(U)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

But $(i \circ \sigma_k|_U)(U) = i(\sigma_k(U)) \subset i(\mathbb{R}^k) = \mathbb{R}^k \times \{0\}^{d-k}$, i.e., its image is contained in a $k$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^d$. When $k < d$, a $k$-dimensional subspace has empty interior in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (any $\epsilon$-ball necessarily contains components orthogonal to the subspace). Hence $(i \circ \sigma_k|_U)(U)$ cannot be open in $\mathbb{R}^d$ — a contradiction.

Thus the assumption $d > k$ is untenable, and we must have $d \leq k$. $\square$

**Remark 3.6a (Structure of the Proof)** The key technique in the above proof is to lift the $\mathbb{R}^k$-valued map to an $\mathbb{R}^d$-valued map via the standard inclusion, thereby satisfying the requirement of the Invariance of Domain Theorem that the domain and codomain have the same dimension. This technique is standard: the Invariance of Domain Theorem essentially states that $\mathbb{R}^d$ cannot be collapsed into a lower-dimensional subspace via a continuous injection.

**Remark 3.6b (Alternative Argument: Measure Theory)** One may also argue directly via measure theory: if $d > k$, the continuous injection $\sigma_k|_U$ maps the $d$-dimensional open set $U$ (of positive $d$-dimensional Lebesgue measure) into $\mathbb{R}^k$, and $\mathbb{R}^k$, as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^d$, has zero $d$-dimensional Lebesgue measure. However, a continuous injection need not preserve measure, so this alternative argument requires additional hypotheses (such as Lipschitz continuity). The topological argument (composite inclusion map + Invariance of Domain) is more rigorous and does not rely on measure-theoretic structure.

**Remark 3.6' (Dimension Counting Argument)** Theorem 3.6 can also be derived directly from standard differential-topological dimension counting: if $d>k$, then any open set $U$ of a $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ cannot be embedded into $\mathbb{R}^k$ via a continuous injection (since the topological dimension of $\mathbb{R}^k$ is $k<d$). Hence injectivity of the spectral map $\sigma_k$ requires $d\leq k$. The moduli space of the present constrained product sphere class has dimension $d=1$, so $k\geq 1$ satisfies the necessary condition; this paper uses $k=3$ to provide a self-consistency check and spectral signature.

**Corollary 3.2** In the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}_{a>0}$ (with interlocking constants locked by Theorem 3.0' and Proposition 3.0), the moduli space dimension is $d = 1$. Hence only $k \geq 1$ eigenvalue is required to satisfy the necessary condition $d \leq k$; this paper uses $k = 3$ eigenvalues, which not only satisfies the necessary condition but also provides a self-consistency check and spectral signature.

### 3.9 Compatibility with Classical Isospectral Counterexamples

**Theorem 3.7 (Inapplicability of Sunada-Type Counterexamples)** $\pi_1(M(a))=0$, so the Sunada method is inapplicable.

**Proof.** $\pi_1(S^3)=0$ ($S^3$ is simply connected). By the product formula for fundamental groups, $\pi_1(M(a))=\pi_1(S^3)\times\pi_1(S^3)\times\pi_1(S^3)=0$. The Sunada method requires the existence of a finite group $\Gamma$ acting freely on a manifold $\tilde{M}$ such that $M=\tilde{M}/\Gamma$ and $\Gamma$ possesses almost-conjugate but non-conjugate subgroups. Since $\pi_1(M(a))=0$, no non-trivial covering exists, so the Sunada construction cannot be implemented. $\square$

**Theorem 3.8 (Inapplicability of Milnor-Type Flat Torus Counterexamples)** $M(a)$ has positive sectional curvature (as a product of $S^3$ factors), is non-flat, so Milnor's 16-dimensional flat torus isospectral pairs do not apply to this class.

**Proof.** $S^3(R)$ equipped with the standard metric has sectional curvature $1/R^2>0$. The sectional curvature of a product manifold is non-negative (by O'Neill's formula), and is positive at least in certain directions. Hence $M(a)$ is non-flat. Milnor's isospectral flat torus counterexamples depend on special spectral properties of flat metrics (such as modular transformations of theta functions), which do not hold on positively curved manifolds. $\square$

**Theorem 3.9 (Inapplicability of Gordon–Webb–Wolpert-Type Counterexamples)** The construction of planar isospectral domain pairs relies on the isometry group of the Euclidean plane and boundary conditions of the wave equation. $M(a)$ is a closed manifold (without boundary), and its spectrum is the global spectrum of the Laplace–Beltrami operator, not the Dirichlet/Neumann spectrum of a planar domain, so this type of counterexample is inapplicable.

**Proof.** The Gordon–Webb–Wolpert counterexamples construct planar domains with identical Dirichlet (or Neumann) spectra but different shapes. This construction depends on:
1. The planar Euclidean geometry of the domains;
2. The specific form of the boundary conditions (Dirichlet or Neumann);
3. Discrete subgroups of the isometry group (e.g., Fuchsian groups).
$M(a)$ is a compact Riemannian manifold without boundary; its Laplace spectrum is the global spectrum and does not involve boundary conditions. Moreover, the curvature of $M(a)$ is positive, not Euclidean planar. Hence this type of counterexample is inapplicable. $\square$

**Remark 3.3** The above compatibility theorems show that the constrained product sphere class $\{M(a)\}$ is naturally immune to several mainstream directions of classical isospectral constructions, which enhances the robustness of Theorem 3.4.

### 4.1 Tripartite Tangent Bundle Decomposition

### 4.6 Definition of the Six-Term Action

### 4.7 Range Theorem and Strict Convexity

**Theorem 4.4 (Range Theorem and Strict Convexity).** Under Axiom 3's $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ$, on the domain
$$D_\theta=\{(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)\mid\theta_i>0,\;\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=90^\circ\},$$
the six-term action $S$ has range
$$S(D_\theta)=[24,+\infty).$$
Moreover, $S$ is a **strictly convex function** on $D_\theta$, and the symmetry point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$ is the unique global strict minimum.

*Proof.*

**(1) Value at the symmetry point.**
Consider the symmetry point $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=30^\circ$. Then $\sin30^\circ=1/2$, $\sin^2 30^\circ=1/4$, giving
$$\frac{1}{\sin^2 30^\circ}=4,\quad\frac{1}{\sin 30^\circ\sin 30^\circ}=4.$$
Sum of three single-field terms: $3\times4=12$. Sum of three cross terms: $3\times4=12$. Hence
$$S(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)=12+12=24.$$

**(2) Strict minimum proof—Lagrange multiplier method.**
Under the constraint $\theta_1+\theta_2+\theta_3=\pi/2$, use Lagrange multipliers to find the extremum of $S$. Set up the Lagrangian
$$\mathcal{L}(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3,\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_i} + \sum_{i<j} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_i\sin\theta_j} - \lambda\left(\sum_{i=1}^3 \theta_i - \frac{\pi}{2}\right).$$

Partial derivative with respect to $\theta_k$:
$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_k} = -\frac{2\cos\theta_k}{\sin^3\theta_k} - \sum_{j\neq k} \frac{\cos\theta_k}{\sin^2\theta_k\sin\theta_j} - \lambda = 0.$$

Factor out $-\cos\theta_k/\sin^2\theta_k$:
$$-\frac{\cos\theta_k}{\sin^2\theta_k}\left(\frac{2}{\sin\theta_k} + \sum_{j\neq k} \frac{1}{\sin\theta_j}\right) = \lambda.$$

By $S_3$ permutation symmetry, if $\theta_1=\theta_2=\theta_3=\pi/6$, then the above gives the same $\lambda$ for $k=1,2,3$, satisfying the Lagrange equations. Thus $(\pi/6,\pi/6,\pi/6)$ is the unique interior critical point.

**Step 2a: Hessian positive definiteness verification.** Compute the Hessian quadratic form of $S$ on the constraint tangent space $T_{\theta}D_\theta = \{v\in\mathbb{R}^3 \mid v_1+v_2+v_3=0\}$. Denote $s_i=\sin\theta_i$, $c_i=\cos\theta_i$.

For single-sector terms $f_i=1/s_i^2$: $\partial^2 f_i/\partial \theta_i^2 = (2+4c_i^2)/s_i^4$. At $30^\circ$: $\partial^2 f_i/\partial \theta_i^2 = (2+3)/(1/16) = 80$.

For two-sector terms $f_{ij}=1/(s_i s_j)$: $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i^2 = (1+c_i^2)/(s_i^3 s_j)$. At $30^\circ$: $s_i=s_j=1/2$, giving $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i^2 = (7/4)/(1/16) = 28$.

For mixed partials: $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i\partial \theta_j = c_i c_j/(s_i^2 s_j^2)$. At $30^\circ$: $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial \theta_i\partial \theta_j = (3/4)/(1/16) = 12$.

Thus the Hessian matrix entries are:
$$H_{ii} = 80 + 28 + 28 = 136,\quad H_{ij} = 12\quad(i\neq j).$$

For any $v\in T_{\theta}D_\theta$ (i.e., $\sum v_i=0$), the quadratic form
$$v^T H v = 136\sum v_i^2 + 24\sum_{i<j}v_iv_j = 124\sum v_i^2 > 0$$
(using $(\sum v_i)^2=0 \Rightarrow \sum_{i<j}v_iv_j = -\frac{1}{2}\sum v_i^2$). Hence $H$ is positive definite on the constraint tangent space, $S$ is strictly convex on $D_\theta$, and the symmetry point $30^\circ$ is the unique global strict minimum.

**(3) Unboundedness above.**
When any angle tends to $0^\circ$, the corresponding term $1/\sin^2\theta_i\to+\infty$, hence $S\to+\infty$. Thus the range is unbounded above.

**(4) Continuity conclusion.**
$S(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ is continuous on $D_\theta$. Together with (2) and (3) and the intermediate value theorem, we obtain $S(D_\theta)=[24,+\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed Computational Supplement (Complete Derivation of the Hessian Matrix):**

For single-sector terms $f_i(\theta_i) = \csc^2\theta_i$:
$$f_i' = -2\csc^2\theta_i\cot\theta_i, \quad f_i'' = 2\csc^2\theta_i(2\cot^2\theta_i + \csc^2\theta_i) = \frac{2+4\cos^2\theta_i}{\sin^4\theta_i}.$$
At $\theta_i=\pi/6$: $\sin\pi/6=1/2$, $\cos\pi/6=\sqrt{3}/2$, so $f_i'' = (2+4\cdot 3/4)/(1/16) = (2+3)\cdot 16 = 80$.

For two-sector terms $f_{ij}(\theta_i,\theta_j) = \csc\theta_i \csc\theta_j$:
$$\frac{\partial^2 f_{ij}}{\partial \theta_i^2} = \csc\theta_i \csc\theta_j (\cot^2\theta_i + \csc^2\theta_i) = \frac{1+\cos^2\theta_i}{\sin^3\theta_i \sin\theta_j}.$$
At $\pi/6$: $\partial^2 f_{ij}/\partial\theta_i^2 = (1+3/4)/(1/8 \cdot 1/2) = (7/4)/(1/16) = 28$.

$$\frac{\partial^2 f_{ij}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j} = \cot\theta_i \cot\theta_j \csc\theta_i \csc\theta_j = \frac{\cos\theta_i \cos\theta_j}{\sin^2\theta_i \sin^2\theta_j}.$$
At $\pi/6$: $= (\sqrt{3}/2)^2 / (1/16) = (3/4)/(1/16) = 12$.

Thus $H_{ii} = f_i'' + f_{ij}'' + f_{ik}'' = 80 + 28 + 28 = 136$ ($j,k\neq i$), and $H_{ij}=12$ ($i\neq j$).

For $v\in T_{\theta}D_\theta$, $v_3=-v_1-v_2$, then:
$$v^T H v = 136(v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2) + 24(v_1v_2+v_1v_3+v_2v_3).$$
From $v_1+v_2+v_3=0$, $v_1v_2+v_1v_3+v_2v_3 = -\frac{1}{2}(v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2)$. Hence:
$$v^T H v = (136-12)(v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2) = 124\|v\|^2 > 0.$$

### 4.8 Tangent Bundle Square Root Decomposition and the Algebraic Origin of the Magic Number Sequence

**Theorem 4.6 (Tangent Bundle Square Root Decomposition and Two-Sector Projection).** Under the constant-curvature Kähler triple $(g_\Sigma,J,\omega)$ of Additional Hypothesis 4.1, the holomorphic tangent bundle of the holographic screen $\Sigma\cong\mathbb{C}P^1$ satisfies the standard algebraic-geometric decomposition:

$$T\Sigma\cong\mathcal{O}(2)\cong\mathcal{O}(1)\otimes\mathcal{O}(1).$$

Furthermore, from the tripartite tangent bundle projection structure (Proposition 4.1), at the $S_3$ symmetry point $\theta_0=(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)$, there exist two kernel line bundles $L_{\mathcal{M}},L_{\mathcal{C}}\subset N\Sigma\otimes\mathbb{C}$ (coming from the kernels of the $\mathcal{M}$ and $\mathcal{C}$ sector projections, respectively) such that

$$L_{\mathcal{M}}\cong L_{\mathcal{C}}\cong\mathcal{O}(1),$$

and their tensor product is naturally isomorphic to $T\Sigma$:

$$L_{\mathcal{M}}\otimes L_{\mathcal{C}}\cong T\Sigma.$$

*Proof.* In three steps:

**Step 1:** The holomorphic tangent bundle of $\Sigma\cong\mathbb{C}P^1$ is $\mathcal{O}(2)$ (standard result), and $\mathcal{O}(2)=\mathcal{O}(1)^{\otimes 2}$.

**Step 2:** The identification of the kernel line bundles $L_{\mathcal{M}}, L_{\mathcal{C}}$ with $\mathcal{O}(1)$ is guaranteed by the following lemma.

**Lemma 4.8 (Chern Class of Kernel Line Bundles—Determination by Elimination).** Under the explicit construction of Theorem 4.3 at the symmetry point, suppose the projection maps $P_{\mathcal{M}},P_{\mathcal{C}},P_{\mathcal{I}}$ are lifted to smooth bundle maps on $\Sigma$ via $S_3$-equivariant extension, and the kernel line bundles $L_i = \ker(P_i) \otimes \mathbb{C}$ are equipped with holomorphic structures making $P_i$ holomorphic. Then $c_1(L_{\mathcal{M}}) = c_1(L_{\mathcal{C}}) = c_1(L_{\mathcal{I}}) = 1$, i.e., $L_i \cong \mathcal{O}(1)$.

*Proof.* On $\Sigma \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$, holomorphic line bundles are completely classified by the Chern number $m \in \mathbb{Z}$: $L \cong \mathcal{O}(m)$, $c_1 = m$. $S_3$ equivariance (Proposition 4.1) cyclically permutes the three sectors at the symmetry point, so the three kernel line bundles are equivalent as holomorphic line bundles; denote their common Chern number by $m$.

**Assertion 1 ($m \ge 0$).** At the symmetry point $p_0$, Theorem 4.3 gives $L_{\mathcal{M}}(p_0) = \text{span}_{\mathbb{C}}\{e_3\} \neq 0$. If $m < 0$, then $H^0(\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(m)) = 0$ (negative line bundles admit no nonvanishing global holomorphic sections). But $L_{\mathcal{M}}$, as a holomorphic subbundle of $\mathcal{M}|_\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{C}^3$ (a holomorphically trivial bundle), the inclusion map $L_{\mathcal{M}} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}^3$ is a nonzero holomorphic bundle homomorphism, which requires $L_{\mathcal{M}}$ to possess a nonzero global holomorphic section (take the projection of the constant section $e_3$ onto $L_{\mathcal{M}}$). Contradiction. Hence $m \ge 0$.

**Assertion 2 ($m \neq 0$).** If $m = 0$, then $L_{\mathcal{M}} \cong \mathcal{O}(0)$ is the trivial holomorphic line bundle, whose curvature form $\Omega = \bar{\partial}\partial \log |s|^2$ for any global nowhere-zero holomorphic section $s$ satisfies $\frac{i}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma \Omega = m = 0$. However, the explicit construction of Theorem 4.3 gives $|\det(P_{\mathcal{M}}|_{U_{\mathcal{M}}})| = 1/4$ at $p_0$. When $P_{\mathcal{M}}$ is extended to a bundle map on $\Sigma$, the area condition $\text{Area}(P_{\mathcal{M}}(U_{\mathcal{M}})) / dA = \sin^2 30^\circ = 1/4$ requires this ratio to be constant everywhere on $\Sigma$. If the kernel line bundle is trivial, then $U_{\mathcal{M}}$ (the 2-dimensional subbundle orthogonal to the kernel) is also trivial, and $P_{\mathcal{M}}|_{U_{\mathcal{M}}}$ is a bundle homomorphism between trivial bundles. As $T_p\Sigma$ rotates with $p$ over $S^2$, maintaining a constant determinant forces the matrix representation of this bundle homomorphism to rotate with $p$, which in turn forces the kernel direction to rotate with $p$—contradicting the constant section of a trivial bundle. More formally: $L_{\mathcal{M}}$ trivial $\implies$ existence of a global parallel kernel direction $\implies$ $U_{\mathcal{M}}$ is also a globally trivial subbundle $\implies$ $P_{\mathcal{M}}|_{U_{\mathcal{M}}}$ is a fixed matrix (in a global parallel frame) $\implies$ $|\det|$ constancy is automatic but the rotation of $T_p\Sigma$ cannot be compensated by a fixed matrix. Hence $m \neq 0$.

**Assertion 3 ($m \le 1$).** The three kernel line bundles $L_{\mathcal{M}} \oplus L_{\mathcal{C}} \oplus L_{\mathcal{I}}$ embed, as smooth complex bundles, into the complexified normal bundle $N\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}$ (real rank 7). On $\mathbb{C}P^1$, the Grothendieck splitting gives $N\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C} \cong \bigoplus_{j=1}^{7} \mathcal{O}(b_j)$, $\sum b_j = c_1(N\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C})$. Compute $c_1(N\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}) = c_1(TM(a)|_\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}) - c_1(T\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C})$. By the product structure $M(a) = S^2 \times S^2 \times S^2$, each $TS^2|_\Sigma$, as the restriction of the tangent bundle of $S^2$, has vanishing complexified Chern class, so $c_1(TM(a)|_\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}) = 0$. Also $c_1(T\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}) = c_1(\mathcal{O}(2)) + c_1(\mathcal{O}(-2)) = 0$. Hence $\sum b_j = 0$. If $m \ge 2$, the three kernel line bundles contribute a positive degree sum $3m \ge 6$, and the remaining 4 summands have degree sum $\le -6$. But deformations of $\Sigma$ within $M(a)$ are generated by the normal directions of the three $S^2$ factors, providing at least 3 linearly independent global normal sections—this requires $N\Sigma \otimes \mathbb{C}$ to have sufficiently many nonnegative-degree summands, which conflicts with only 4 summands remaining, most of which are negative. Hence $m \le 1$.

**Conclusion:** $0 \le m \le 1$ and $m \neq 0$, so $m = 1$. That is, $c_1(L_{\mathcal{M}}) = c_1(L_{\mathcal{C}}) = c_1(L_{\mathcal{I}}) = 1$, and $L_i \cong \mathcal{O}(1)$. $\square$

### 5.2 Explicit Parametrization Map

**Theorem 5.2 (Explicit Construction of the Parametrization Map).** Let $S_{\text{abstract}}: D_+\to(0,+\infty)$ be a strictly monotonically increasing continuous function satisfying $\lim_{x\to p_0}S_{\text{abstract}}(x)=0$ and $\lim_{x\to p_*}S_{\text{abstract}}(x)=+\infty$. Note: Axioms 1–2 only guarantee that $S$ is continuous with range $(0,+\infty)$, and do not imply monotonicity; **strict monotonicity** is introduced here as a constructive hypothesis, equivalent to requiring $S_{\text{abstract}}$ to be a global coordinate function on $D_+$. $$\tilde{S}(x)=S_{\text{abstract}}(x)+24.$$

Then there exists a unique continuous injection $\Phi: D_+\to D_\theta$ such that:

1. **Range locking**: $S(\Phi(x))=\tilde{S}(x)$;
2. **Gauge section**: $\Phi(x)$ lies on the symmetry axis
   $$\gamma=\{\theta\in D_{\text{ord}}\mid \theta_2=\theta_3\}$$
   of the ordered sector gauge $D_{\text{ord}}$;
3. **Boundary compatibility**: $\lim_{x\to p_0}\Phi(x)=\theta_0=(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)$, and $\lim_{x\to p_*}\Phi(x)\in\partial D_\theta$.

*Proof.* In three steps:

**Step 1: Strict convexity of level sets.** By Theorem 4.4, $S(\theta)$ is strictly convex on $D_\theta$, with unique global minimum at $\theta_0$, value $24$. For any $S_0>24$, the level set $L_{S_0}=\{\theta\in D_\theta\mid S(\theta)=S_0\}$ is a smooth strictly convex closed curve surrounding $\theta_0$. Strict convexity guarantees: any ray emanating from $\theta_0$ intersects $L_{S_0}$ at exactly one point.

**Step 2: Uniformization on the symmetry axis.** By Theorem 4.5, $S|_\gamma: \gamma\to[24,+\infty)$ is a strictly monotonically decreasing homeomorphism. Hence, for any $S_0\geq24$, there exists a unique $\theta^*(S_0)\in\gamma$ such that $S(\theta^*(S_0))=S_0$.

**Step 3: Construction of the map.** Define $\Phi(x)=\theta^*(\tilde{S}(x))$. By the strict monotonicity of $\tilde{S}$ and the continuity of $\theta^*$, $\Phi$ is continuous and injective. Boundary behavior follows directly from the limits of $\tilde{S}$ and the correspondence of $\theta^*$. $\square$

**Detailed Expansion:** Proof of strict convexity of level sets in Step 1. By Theorem 4.4, $S$ is strictly convex on $D_\theta$. For $S_0>24$, $L_{S_0}=S^{-1}(S_0)$ is a level set of $S$. Since $S$ is strictly convex, its level sets must be strictly convex closed curves (in two dimensions). Strict convexity implies that the curvature of the curve is everywhere nonzero and of the same sign, so a ray from the interior point $\theta_0$ intersects the curve exactly once.

Injectivity of $\Phi$ in Step 3: If $\Phi(x_1)=\Phi(x_2)$, then $\theta^*(\tilde{S}(x_1))=\theta^*(\tilde{S}(x_2))$. Since $\theta^*$ is a homeomorphism (bijection), $\tilde{S}(x_1)=\tilde{S}(x_2)$. Moreover, $\tilde{S}$ is strictly monotonic, so $x_1=x_2$. Continuity is guaranteed by composition of continuous functions.

### 5.4 Explicit Spectrum–Projection Map

Based on the bridge function canonical form (Definition 5.1' and Normalization Condition 5.1''), the first three nonzero Laplace–Beltrami eigenvalues are explicitly determined by the holographic screen angle configuration $\theta$ as:

$$\begin{aligned}
\lambda_1^\Delta(\theta) &= \frac{S(\theta) \pm \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{8\ell_0^2} \\[4pt]
\lambda_2^\Delta(\theta) &= \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1^\Delta(\theta) = \frac{S(\theta) \pm \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{3\ell_0^2} \\[4pt]
\lambda_3^\Delta(\theta) &= \Lambda \lambda_1^\Delta(\theta) = \frac{\Lambda\left(S(\theta) \pm \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}\right)}{8\ell_0^2}
\end{aligned}$$

where the $\pm$ signs correspond respectively to the UV branch ($a\le\ell_0$, $\lambda_1^\Delta$ takes the $+$ sign) and the IR branch ($a\ge\ell_0$, $\lambda_1^\Delta$ takes the $-$ sign).

**Derivation Outline**

**Step 1: Algebraic structure of the self-dual equation.**
Let $t = a^2/\ell_0^2 > 0$; the bridge equation becomes $S = 12(t + 1/t)$. By the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality, $t + 1/t \ge 2$, with equality iff $t=1$ (i.e., $a=\ell_0$). Hence $S \ge 24$, matching the holographic screen action range $[24,+\infty)$ of Theorem 4.4 **seamlessly**. The minimum $S=24$ corresponds to the ground state $a=\ell_0$.

For $S>24$, the equation becomes the quadratic $t^2 - (S/12)t + 1 = 0$, with discriminant $\Delta = (S/12)^2 - 4 > 0$. By Vieta's formulas, the product of the two roots is $t_1 t_2 = 1$, so the corresponding scale factors satisfy $a_{\text{UV}} \cdot a_{\text{IR}} = \ell_0^2$.

**Step 2: Explicit inverse solution for the scale factor.**
By the quadratic formula: $t = \frac{S \pm \sqrt{S^2 - 576}}{24}$. Hence
$$a_{\text{UV}}(\theta) = \ell_0\sqrt{\frac{S(\theta) - \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{24}},\quad a_{\text{IR}}(\theta) = \ell_0\sqrt{\frac{S(\theta) + \sqrt{S(\theta)^2 - 576}}{24}}.$$
For any $S\ge24$, $a$ exists and is unique (at the ground state $S=24$, the two branches coincide at $a=\ell_0$).

**Step 3: Explicit closure of the first three eigenvalues.**
By Proposition 3.2, the first three nonzero eigenvalues are uniquely determined by $a$ and maintain the fixed spectral signature: $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/a^2 = 3/(\ell_0^2 t)$, $\lambda_2^\Delta = \frac{8}{3}\lambda_1^\Delta$, $\lambda_3^\Delta = \Lambda \lambda_1^\Delta$. Substituting $t = \left(S \pm \sqrt{S^2-576}\right)/24$ yields the explicit formulas above.

**Detailed Derivation:** From $t = \frac{S \pm \sqrt{S^2-576}}{24}$, we obtain $\lambda_1^\Delta = 3/(\ell_0^2 t) = 72/(\ell_0^2 (S \pm \sqrt{S^2-576}))$. Rationalizing the denominator:
$$\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{72(S \mp \sqrt{S^2-576})}{\ell_0^2 (S^2 - (S^2-576))} = \frac{72(S \mp \sqrt{S^2-576})}{576\ell_0^2} = \frac{S \mp \sqrt{S^2-576}}{8\ell_0^2}.$$
The UV branch $a\le\ell_0$ corresponds to $t\le 1$, i.e., taking the smaller root $t = \frac{S - \sqrt{S^2-576}}{24}$, in which case $\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{S + \sqrt{S^2-576}}{8\ell_0^2}$ (upper sign). The IR branch corresponds to $t\ge 1$, taking the larger root, giving $\lambda_1^\Delta = \frac{S - \sqrt{S^2-576}}{8\ell_0^2}$ (lower sign).

**Remark 5.3 (Boundary Behavior and Self-Consistency of the Vacuum Limit).** The natural structure of the bridge equation automatically produces three layers of boundary behavior:

1. **Ground state (vacuum steady state):** When $\theta=(30^\circ,30^\circ,30^\circ)$, $S=24$, and the equation degenerates to the unique solution $a=\ell_0$.
2. **UV degenerate limit:** As the angle configuration tends to the boundary (any $\theta_i\to0^+$), $S(\theta)\to+\infty$, and the UV branch solution gives $a_{\text{UV}}\to0^+$, $\lambda_i^\Delta\to+\infty$.
3. **IR degenerate limit:** Under the same $S\to+\infty$, the IR branch gives $a_{\text{IR}}\to+\infty$, $\lambda_i^\Delta\to0^+$.

The regularized action $S_{\text{abstract}} = S(\theta) - 24 = 12\left(\frac{a}{\ell_0} - \frac{\ell_0}{a}\right)^2$ translates the holographic screen realization domain $[24,+\infty)$ to $(0,+\infty)$, **precisely corresponding** to the range of the abstract geometric quantity $S_{\text{abstract}}:D_\pm\to(0,+\infty)$ of Chapter 2.

### 5.5 Bridge Function Canonical Form (Constructive Concept)

**Theorem 5.3 (Bridge Function Canonical Form).** Let $S:(0,+\infty)\to[24,+\infty)$ be a real-analytic function possessing a Laurent expansion convergent on $(0,+\infty)$ (i.e., $S$ extends to a meromorphic function on $\mathbb{C}^*$, with poles possible only at $0$ and $\infty$), satisfying the following three conditions:

**(C1) Scale-reciprocal duality**: $S(a)=S(\ell_0^2/a)$ for all $a>0$;
**(C2) Ground state locking**: $S(\ell_0)=24$ is the unique global minimum, and $S''(\ell_0)>0$;
**(C3) Spectral asymptotic constraint**: as $a\to 0^+$, $S(a)\sim C/a^2$ ($C>0$ a constant).

Then $S$ must take the form $S(a)=c_2(a^2/\ell_0^2+\ell_0^2/a^2)+c_0$, with $c_2>0$, $2c_2+c_0=24$.

**Remark 5.3' (Status of the Analyticity Hypothesis).** The theorem's requirement that $S$ be meromorphic (Laurent expansion convergent) is a **framework analyticity hypothesis**, not derived from Axioms 1–3 or Proposition 3.0. Physically, the bridge function, as a map from geometric stiffness to spectral stiffness, has no a priori reason to be meromorphic. If non-meromorphic perturbations are allowed (e.g., $S(a) = 12(a^2/\ell_0^2+\ell_0^2/a^2) + \varepsilon(a^4/\ell_0^4+\ell_0^4/a^4)$ with $\varepsilon$ small), then $S$ still satisfies (C1)–(C3), but the canonical form theorem fails. Hence the analyticity hypothesis is a **fourth constraint condition** of the framework, alongside the three axioms.

*Proof.* Let $x=a/\ell_0$, $u=\ln x$. By (C1), $S(x)=S(1/x)$, so $S$ is an even analytic function of $u=\ln x$, and its Laurent expansion $S(x)=\sum c_n x^n$ satisfies $c_n=c_{-n}$. By (C3), $\lim_{x\to 0} x^2 S(x)=C<+\infty$. This requires $c_n=0$ for all $n<-2$. By duality $c_n=c_{-n}$, we obtain $c_n=0$ for all $n>2$. Hence $S(x)=c_{-2}x^{-2}+c_0+c_2x^2$, with $c_{-2}=c_2$. By (C2), $S(1)=2c_2+c_0=24$, and $S''(1)=8c_2>0$ requires $c_2>0$. $\square$

**Detailed Expansion:** Even analytic function property. Let $x=e^u$, then $S(x)=S(e^u)=\tilde{S}(u)$. From $S(x)=S(1/x)$, we have $\tilde{S}(u)=\tilde{S}(-u)$, so $\tilde{S}$ is an even function of $u$. Its Taylor expansion $\tilde{S}(u)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n u^{2n}$ corresponds to $S(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (\ln x)^{2n}$. On the other hand, in the Laurent expansion $S(x)=\sum c_n x^n$, the condition $S(x)=S(1/x)$ implies $\sum c_n x^n = \sum c_n x^{-n}$, hence $c_n=c_{-n}$.

The asymptotic constraint $\lim_{x\to 0} x^2 S(x)=C$ means $S(x)\sim C/x^2$ as $x\to 0$. In the Laurent expansion, $x^2 S(x) = \sum c_n x^{n+2}$. As $x\to 0$, if there existed $n<-2$ with $c_n\neq 0$, then $x^{n+2}\to\infty$, contradicting the finite limit $C$. Hence $c_n=0$ for all $n<-2$. By duality $c_n=c_{-n}$, we obtain $c_n=0$ for all $n>2$. Therefore $S(x)=c_{-2}x^{-2}+c_0+c_2x^2$ and $c_{-2}=c_2$.

From $S(1)=2c_2+c_0=24$ and $S''(1)=8c_2>0$, we obtain $c_2>0$. If we further assume $c_0=0$ (pure asymptotic behavior), then $c_2=12$.

**Remark 5.2' (Status of the Bridge Function Canonical Form).** Theorem 5.3 shows: within the function class satisfying duality, ground state locking, and spectral asymptotic constraints, the canonical form of the bridge function is the hyperbolic type $c_2(x^2+x^{-2})+c_0$. The precise numerical values $c_2=12$ and $c_0=0$ **cannot be derived from the above three conditions alone**; they require additional normalization input (e.g., requiring $c_0=0$ to ensure $S$ has pure reciprocal-symmetric asymptotic behavior as $a\to\infty$, and determining $c_2=12$ through numerical correspondence with the spectral eigenvalue $\lambda_1^\Delta=3/a^2$ at $a=\ell_0$). Hence the bridge function canonical form (Definition 5.1' and Normalization Condition 5.1'') is mathematically a **parametrized canonical form family**, whose precise coefficient $12$ belongs to the **definitional setting** of the framework, not a theorem output strictly emergent from the three axioms.
