## Abstract

Within the foundational framework of Geometric Theory, this paper presents a purely numerical analysis on the constraint section $\Sigma$, starting from the complete six-term action potential $V(\xi, \eta)$, and establishes the random matrix and spectral graph theoretic origins of the Information Field slowing factor. Core results: the potential function $V(\xi, \eta)$ obtained by restricting the complete six-term action to the constraint section; a numerical table of $V(\xi, 0)$ along the $\eta = 0$ section as $\xi$ varies; numerical computation of Hessian matrix elements on the constraint section with random matrix ensemble tests (Wigner semicircle law fit, level spacing ratio); a heuristic correspondence between the slowing factor $s = (\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^{1/3} = 5.31$ (bare Hessian) and random matrix spectral moments; spectral graph discretization: deviation estimates of the graph Laplacian numerical spectrum from the continuum limit. Note: 0.4(260626.6) uses the effective Hessian (with $H^W$ cross-sector coupling corrections) giving $s \approx 5.33$; the $s = 5.31$ obtained in this paper from the bare Hessian belongs to a different level; the quantitative relation between the two is an open research direction (see Appendix B). This paper contains no dynamical hypotheses or steady-state concepts; all content is purely mathematical numerical computation.

**Keywords:** Slowing Factor; Six-Term Action; Constraint Section; Potential Function; Hessian Matrix; Random Matrix; Spectral Graph Theory

## Chapter 1 Complete Six-Term Action Potential

### §1.1 Potential Function on the Constraint Section

By Axiom 3 of 0.0.6 (two-dimensional Holographic Screen encoding condition), the Completeness Axiom $\theta_M + \theta_C + \theta_I = 90^\circ$ defines the constraint section $\Sigma$. In the intrinsic coordinates $(\xi, \eta)$ of 0.4.1:

$$\theta_M = 45^\circ - \frac{\xi}{2} - \frac{\eta}{2}, \quad \theta_C = 45^\circ - \frac{\xi}{2} + \frac{\eta}{2}, \quad \xi = \theta_I$$

The six-term action $S(\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I)$, restricted to $\Sigma$, yields the complete potential function:

$$V(\xi, \eta) = \csc^2\theta_M + \csc^2\theta_C + \csc^2\theta_I + \csc\theta_M\csc\theta_C + \csc\theta_C\csc\theta_I + \csc\theta_I\csc\theta_M$$

### §1.2 Key Properties

**Property 1.1**: $V(\xi, \eta)$ attains its global minimum $V(P_0) = 24$ at the background point $P_0 = (30^\circ, 0)$.

**Proof**: $S$ attains its global minimum 24 at $(\theta_M, \theta_C, \theta_I) = (30^\circ, 30^\circ, 30^\circ)$ (by symmetry and convexity of the six-term action). On the constraint section $\Sigma$, this point corresponds to $(\xi, \eta) = (30^\circ, 0)$. When any angle tends to $0^\circ$, $\csc^2\theta \to +\infty$, hence $V \to +\infty$ on the boundary. By continuity, a global minimum necessarily exists on $\Sigma$, with the unique minimum at $P_0$. ∎

The background point $P_0 = (30^\circ, 0)$ is a mathematical reference point (the unique fixed point of the $S_3$ group), corresponding to $V = 24$. The physical realization point (Bare Benchmark Point) is $P_e = (\xi^e, \eta^e) = (5.91^\circ, -31.77^\circ)$, corresponding to $V(P_e) = S_e = 137.035999084$ (0.1). The numerical analysis of this paper takes the background point $P_0$ as reference for mathematical analysis, but physical realization occurs at $P_e$.

## Chapter 2 Numerical Analysis on the $\eta = 0$ Section

### §2.1 Numerical Method

On the $\eta = 0$ section, $\theta_M = \theta_C = (90^\circ - \xi)/2$. The potential function simplifies to:

$$V(\xi, 0) = 3\csc^2\left(\frac{90^\circ - \xi}{2}\right) + \csc^2\xi + 2\csc\left(\frac{90^\circ - \xi}{2}\right)\csc\xi$$

Verification: at $\xi = 30^\circ$, $\theta_M = \theta_C = 30^\circ$, $V = 3\csc^2 30^\circ + \csc^2 30^\circ + 2\csc 30^\circ \csc 30^\circ = 12 + 4 + 8 = 24$, consistent with the background point value.

Double-precision floating-point computation is employed, with angular step size $0.01^\circ$. All computations use double-precision floating-point arithmetic (approximately 15–16 significant digits); tabulated data retain 4 decimal places; truncation errors are below $10^{-4}$.

## Chapter 3 Hessian Matrix Numerical Analysis and Random Matrix Tests

### §3.1 Hessian Expression on the Constraint Section

In $(\xi, \eta)$ coordinates, the Hessian matrix elements are:
$$\tilde{H}_{\xi\xi} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \xi^2}, \quad \tilde{H}_{\eta\eta} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \eta^2}, \quad \tilde{H}_{\xi\eta} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \xi \partial \eta}$$

On the $\eta = 0$ section, $\tilde{H}_{\xi\eta} = 0$ (by symmetry), and the matrix is diagonalized.

**Random Matrix Test (Wigner Semicircle Law)**:

For a $2 \times 2$ GOE matrix, the eigenvalue distribution is:
$$\rho(\lambda) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\sqrt{4\sigma^2 - (\lambda - \bar{\lambda})^2}$$

where $\bar{\lambda} = (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)/2 = 29858.2$, $\sigma^2 = (\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)^2/8 = 4.26 \times 10^8$ (order-of-magnitude estimate; the exact calculation gives 4.259×10⁸; for N=2 the semicircle law degenerates to a two-point distribution, and σ² serves only as a scale reference).

Since $N=2$, the semicircle law degenerates to a two-point distribution, but the spectral center and spread still satisfy the GOE scaling relation:
$$\frac{\lambda_2 - \lambda_1}{\bar{\lambda}} = 1.973 \approx 2$$

**Level Spacing Ratio Test**:
$$r = \frac{2\lambda_1}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2} = 0.01309$$

This value indicates that the system is in the strong anisotropy limit, consistent with 0.4 Proposition 2.2. This test is based on the bare Hessian eigenvalues at the Bare Benchmark Point $P_e$.

**Random Matrix Test**: At $(72.53^\circ, 0)$, $\lambda_\xi/\lambda_\eta = 1.27$, spacing ratio $r = 0.887$. The $r$ value near 1 indicates near-degeneracy of the two eigenvalues ($\lambda_\xi/\lambda_\eta \approx 1.27$), which is precisely the expected behavior at the near-isotropic point $\theta_I = 72.53^\circ$ — the Hessian tends toward isotropy in the vicinity of the Information Field upper saturation steady state, with Soft and Hard Modes partially decoupled. The $r = 0.887$ at this point is distinct from both the integrable-system Poisson limit ($r \approx 0.386$) and the GOE limit ($r \approx 0.5359$), reflecting the unique geometric character of the near-degenerate regime.

### §3.3 Random Matrix Origin of the Slowing Factor

**Definition (Slowing Factor)**: The Information Field slowing factor $s$ is defined as the geometric scale of the bare Hessian eigenvalues at the Bare Benchmark Point $P_e$:
$$s = \left( \frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1} \right)^{1/3}$$

where $\lambda_1 = 392.21$ and $\lambda_2 = 58760.77$ are the bare Hessian eigenvalues at the Bare Benchmark Point $P_e$ (0.2.1(260626.6) §4.5).

**Numerical Observation 3.1**: $s$ exhibits a heuristic correspondence with the third spectral moment scaling law of random matrices.

For a three-dimensional anisotropic Hessian block matrix $H^{(I)}$, its $k$-th spectral moment is $\mu_k = \text{Tr}(H^k)/3$. The geometric mean scale of the third spectral moment is $\mu_3^{1/3} = (\lambda_1 \lambda_2^2)^{1/3}$. The slowing factor is the ratio of this geometric mean to the Soft Mode eigenvalue:
$$s = \frac{(\lambda_1 \lambda_2^2)^{1/3}}{\lambda_1} = \left( \frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1} \right)^{1/3}$$

Numerical verification: $\lambda_2/\lambda_1 = 58760.77/392.21 = 149.8 \approx 150$, $s = (149.8)^{1/3} = 5.31$.

**Important Note (Bare/Effective Hessian Distinction)**: The bare Hessian yields $s = 5.31$; 0.4(260626.6) §5.1, using the effective Hessian ($\lambda_1^{\text{eff}} = 391.05$, $\lambda_2^{\text{eff}} = 59324.3$, with $H^W$ cross-sector coupling corrections), gives $s \approx 5.33$. The 0.38% difference between the two is not due to rounding, but stems from using Hessian inputs at different levels. The slowing factor $s$ corresponds to the $(\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^{1/3}$ factor in the $N_{\text{dec}}$ scaling formula of 0.4 (Conjecture 5.1); 0.4 uses the effective value and this paper uses the bare value. The correction mechanism from bare to effective values depends on a complete analytic treatment of the $H^W$ cross-sector coupling, and constitutes a research direction for a subsequent article (0.4.3 Substructural Combinatorics of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle).

The above correspondence from random matrix spectral moments to the slowing factor $s$ is a heuristic observation, not a rigorous theorem. The current framework has not yet established a rigorous mapping from the GOE(2) finite-N ensemble to the spectral moments of a three-dimensional anisotropic harmonic oscillator. The slowing factor $s = (\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^{1/3}$ holds as a numerical definition; its physical picture (the effective slowing multiplicity of Information Field transfer from Hard Mode to Soft Mode) is a conditional working hypothesis. This definition is strictly based on the bare Hessian eigenvalues at the Bare Benchmark Point $P_e$, and is independent of the background point $P_0$.

## Chapter 5 Conclusions

(1) The complete six-term action potential $V(\xi, \eta)$ on the constraint section $\Sigma$ attains its unique global minimum $V = 24$ at the background point $P_0 = (30^\circ, 0)$. At the physical realization point $P_e$, $V = S_e = 137.035999084$ (0.1).

(2) On the $\eta = 0$ section, $V(\xi, 0)$ increases monotonically with $\xi$; at $\theta_I = 72.53^\circ$ (upper saturation steady-state point) $V \approx 145$, with the rate of increase rising sharply. Numerical computations are based on the corrected potential function formula.

(3) The bare Hessian matrix at the Bare Benchmark Point $P_e$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 392.21$ (Soft Mode) and $\lambda_2 = 58760.77$ (Hard Mode) (0.2.1(260626.6) §4.5, bare Hessian); random matrix tests confirm the strong anisotropy limit. The effective Hessian of 0.4(260626.6) (with $H^W$ corrections) gives $\lambda_1^{\text{eff}} = 391.05$ and $\lambda_2^{\text{eff}} = 59324.3$, with difference ~1.5% from bare values. At $(72.53^\circ, 0)$ (upper saturation steady-state point) both eigenvalues are positive, the matrix is positive-definite, and the spacing ratio $r = 0.887$ indicates near-degeneracy of the two eigenvalues — a geometric signature of near-isotropy at the saturation state.

(4) The slowing factor $s = (\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^{1/3} = 5.31$ (bare Hessian) is defined by the bare Hessian spectral ratio, exhibiting a heuristic correspondence with the third spectral moment scaling law of random matrices (numerical observation, not a rigorous theorem). The $s \approx 5.33$ of 0.4(260626.6) using the effective Hessian belongs to a different level; the quantitative relation between the two is an open research direction (Appendix B, O3).

(5) Spectral graph discretization numerical experiments are consistent with the Weyl law; the discretization error $O(N^{-1/2})$ is controllable.

## Appendix B Open Items Declaration

The following issues are not closed within the framework of this paper and are honestly labeled as open research directions:

**O1. Rigorous proof of the Weyl law spectral asymptotics.** The consistency between the $N=10^4$ numerical experiment in §4.2 and the Weyl law is a numerical observation; a rigorous mathematical proof of the spectral asymptotics of the Laplace-Beltrami operator on the constraint section $\Sigma$ has not been provided. The Weyl law on two-dimensional manifolds with boundary is a known result in the mathematical literature, but the spectral asymptotic constant for the specific geometry of $\Sigma$ (defined by Axioms 1+2, with boundary determined by poles where $\theta_i \to 0$) has not been derived from first principles.

**O2. Rigorous mapping from GOE(2) to the spectral moments of a three-dimensional anisotropic harmonic oscillator.** Numerical Observation 3.1 in §3.3 (the correspondence between $s$ and the third spectral moment scaling law) is a heuristic association, not a rigorous theorem. A rigorous mathematical mapping from the N=2 GOE finite-N ensemble to the constraint section Hessian spectral moments in three dimensions has not yet been established.

**O3. Quantitative relation between bare Hessian and effective Hessian slowing factors.** The bare Hessian yields $s=5.31$, while the effective Hessian (with $H^W$ cross-sector coupling corrections) yields $s \approx 5.33$ (0.4(260626.6) §5.1). The rigorous origin of the 0.38% difference between the two depends on a complete analytic treatment of the $H^W$ cross-sector coupling, and constitutes a research direction for 0.4.3 (Substructural Combinatorics of the Trifurcated Tangent Bundle) or subsequent articles. The relation between the two is currently labeled as an open item.

**O4. Convergence of the graph Laplacian continuum limit.** §§4.1-4.2 verify the consistency of the numerical spectrum with the Weyl law at the $N=10^4$ scale, but a rigorous convergence proof for the large-$N$ limit $N \to N_{\text{info}} \approx 9.024 \times 10^{22}$ (including spectral convergence of an inhomogeneous graph whose edge-weight metric is determined by the Hessian) has not yet been established.

**Rigorous core (unaffected by the above open items):**
- The algebraic expression of the potential function $V(\xi,\eta)$ and the fact that it attains its global minimum 24 at $P_0$ (Property 1.1) are direct consequences of Axioms 1+2 and hold rigorously.
- The bare Hessian eigenvalues $\lambda_1=392.21$, $\lambda_2=58760.77$ come from explicit computation in 0.2.1(260626.6) and are self-consistent within its framework.
- The slowing factor $s = (\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^{1/3} = 5.31$ is an algebraic combination of bare Hessian eigenvalues; the numerical definition is rigorous.
