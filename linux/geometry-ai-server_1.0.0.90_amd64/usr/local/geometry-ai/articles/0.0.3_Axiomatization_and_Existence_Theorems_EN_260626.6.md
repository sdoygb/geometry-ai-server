## Abstract

This article establishes two foundational axioms and rigorously proves the range, monotonicity, and existence properties of geometric quantities in the excitation regime. **Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** stipulates that the topology of the Excitation Parameter Space is the circle $S^1$ with the Vacuum Point $p_0$ and the Degeneration Point $p_*$ removed. **Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** stipulates the continuity and boundary limit behavior of the geometric quantity $S$. From connectedness, continuity, and the Intermediate Value Theorem, it follows rigorously that the range of $S$ on each connected component is $(0, +\infty)$, from which it is further proved that: for any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, there necessarily exists an excitation whose geometric quantity equals exactly $S_0$. This article further introduces the **Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis** (Definition 2.1′), clarifying its logical status as a constructive hypothesis, thereby guaranteeing uniqueness of the preimage. The entire article relies solely on elementary analytic tools and introduces no experimental input or external physical hypotheses.

**Keywords:** Axiomatization; Parameter Space; Intermediate Value Theorem; Existence Proof; Range; Strict Monotonicity

## 2　Axiomatization of the Excitation Parameter Space

### 2.1　Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)

**Axiom 1 (Circle Topology Axiom)** The Excitation Parameter Space $D$ is obtained from the one-dimensional compact connected manifold $S^1$ (the circle) by removing two distinct points $p_0$ and $p_*$:

$$D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}, \qquad p_0 \neq p_*$$

The two removed points are respectively called the **Vacuum Point** ($p_0$) and the **Degeneration Point** ($p_*$). Consequently, $D$ consists of two connected components:

$$D_+ \cong (0,1), \qquad D_- \cong (0,1)$$

Each component is homeomorphic to an open interval. The parameter $x \in D_\pm$ labels excitations.

**Lemma 2.1 (Topological Structure of the Components)** Let $S^1$ be the standard unit circle $\{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi \in [0,2\pi)\}$. Removing the two points $p_0 = e^{i\phi_0}$ and $p_* = e^{i\phi_*}$ (without loss of generality, let $0 \leq \phi_0 < \phi_* < 2\pi$), then $S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}$ has precisely two connected components:
$$D_+ = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_0 < \phi < \phi_*\}, \quad D_- = \{e^{i\phi} \mid \phi_* < \phi < \phi_0 + 2\pi\}.$$
As subspaces of $S^1$ endowed with the subspace topology, each component is homeomorphic to the open interval $(0,1)$.

*Proof.* Consider the standard covering map $\pi: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $\pi(\phi) = e^{i\phi}$. On $\mathbb{R}$, $\pi^{-1}(S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}) = \mathbb{R} \setminus \{\phi_0 + 2k\pi, \phi_* + 2k\pi \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. Each connected component of this set is an open interval $(\phi_0 + 2k\pi, \phi_* + 2k\pi)$ or $(\phi_* + 2k\pi, \phi_0 + 2(k+1)\pi)$. Since the restriction of $\pi$ to any interval of length $2\pi$ is a homeomorphism, the images of these open intervals in $S^1$ are precisely $D_+$ and $D_-$. Each open interval is homeomorphic to $(0,1)$, hence $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$. $\square$

### 2.2　Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)

**Axiom 2 (Boundary Limit Axiom)** On the parameter space $D$, there exists a geometric quantity $S: D \to (0, +\infty)$ satisfying:

1. **Continuity**: $S$ is continuous on each connected component $D_\pm$;
2. **Vacuum Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_0$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$;
3. **Degeneration Limit**: As $x$ approaches $p_*$ along either component, $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$.

**Note 2.1** Axiom 2 does not require $S$ to have the same functional expression on $D_+$ and $D_-$, nor does it require $S$ to be continuous on the entirety of $D$ ($p_0$ and $p_*$ are not in $D$). Thus $S$ may be different functions on the two separate components, so long as each satisfies the boundary conditions.

### 2.3　Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis (Constructive Hypothesis)

**Definition 2.1′ (Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis)** If $S$ is strictly monotonic on $D_\pm$, then for any $S_0 > 0$, the $x_+$ and $x_-$ in Theorem 2.2 are uniquely determined. Strict monotonicity is **not** a theorem derived from Axioms 1–2, but a **constructive hypothesis** of the framework. Its introduction is equivalent to requiring that $S$ constitutes a global coordinate function on $D_\pm$.

*Proof.* A strictly monotonic continuous function is injective, hence the preimage is unique. $\square$

**Note 2.2** Strict monotonicity cannot be derived solely from the topological and analytic properties of Axioms 1–2; it requires additional constructive input. It is therefore segregated as an independent constructive hypothesis (Definition 2.1′), to accurately reflect its logical status.

### 2.4　Range Theorem

**Theorem 2.1 (Range)** Under Axiom 1 and Axiom 2, the range of $S$ on each connected component $D_\pm$ is $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$.

*Proof.* By Axiom 1, $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is a connected set. By Axiom 2, $S$ is continuous on $D_\pm$, hence the image $S(D_\pm)$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e., an interval. By the Vacuum Limit, $\inf S(D_\pm) = 0$; by the Degeneration Limit, $\sup S(D_\pm) = +\infty$. Since $p_0, p_* \notin D$, the endpoints $0$ and $+\infty$ are unattainable. Hence $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. $\square$

**Detailed elaboration:** The connectedness argument can be expanded as follows. Let $D_+ \cong (0,1)$ and let $S: D_+ \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Suppose $S(D_+)$ is not an interval; then there exist $\alpha < \beta < \gamma$ such that $\alpha, \gamma \in S(D_+)$ but $\beta \notin S(D_+)$. Set $U = S^{-1}((-\infty, \beta))$ and $V = S^{-1}((\beta, +\infty))$. Then $U$ and $V$ are disjoint open sets in $D_+$, and $U \cup V = D_+$, contradicting the connectedness of $D_+$. Hence $S(D_+)$ must be an interval. Combined with the boundary limits, this interval must be $(0, +\infty)$.

### 2.5　Existence Theorem

**Theorem 2.2 (Existence)** For any given positive number $S_0 > 0$, there exists, on each component $D_+$ and $D_-$, at least one excitation $x_+ \in D_+$ and $x_- \in D_-$ such that $S(x_+) = S(x_-) = S_0$.

*Proof.* By Theorem 2.1, $S(D_\pm) = (0, +\infty)$. For any $S_0 > 0$, clearly $S_0 \in (0, +\infty)$. Hence $S^{-1}(S_0) \cap D_\pm \neq \varnothing$. $\square$

### 2.6　Strict Monotonicity Theorem

**Theorem 2.3 (Strict Monotonicity)** Under Axiom 1, Axiom 2, and **Definition 2.1′ (Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis)**, $S$ is necessarily a strictly monotonic function on each connected component $D_\pm$ (strictly increasing or strictly decreasing, depending on orientation). By fixing the orientation of the component, $S$ may be taken to be strictly increasing.

*Proof.* $D_\pm \cong (0,1)$ is an interval. By the Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis of Definition 2.1′, $S$ is strictly monotonic on $D_\pm$. Combining with the boundary limits $\lim_{x \to p_0} S(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to p_*} S(x) = +\infty$, $S$ must be strictly increasing. $\square$

**Corollary 2.1 (Uniqueness of Preimage)** For any $S_0 > 0$, the preimage set $S^{-1}(S_0) \cap D_\pm$ is a singleton.

*Proof.* Immediate from the strict monotonicity established in Theorem 2.3. $\square$

## 3　Conclusion

This article establishes two foundational axioms characterizing the topological and analytic structure of the Excitation Parameter Space, and rigorously proves:

1. The Excitation Parameter Space is $D = S^1 \setminus \{p_0, p_*\}$, containing two open-interval components $D_\pm$ (Lemma 2.1).

2. The geometric quantity $S$ is continuous on each component, satisfying the Vacuum Limit $0$ and the Degeneration Limit $+\infty$ (Axiom 2).

3. By connectedness, continuity, and the Intermediate Value Theorem, the range of $S$ is $(0, +\infty)$ (Theorem 2.1), and for any $S_0 > 0$ there exists an excitation such that $S(x) = S_0$ (Theorem 2.2).

4. Under the **Strict Monotonicity Hypothesis of Definition 2.1′**, $S$ is a strictly monotonic homeomorphism (Theorem 2.3), and the preimage is unique (Corollary 2.1). The constructive status of this hypothesis has been explicitly labeled and is not disguised as a corollary of Axioms 1–2.

$S_{\text{total}} = 0$ serves as a vacuum-marking condition, used to locate the Vacuum Point $p_0$ in the parameter space. The conclusions of this article depend solely on Axiom 1 and Axiom 2 and do not rely on any further structure of $S_{\text{total}} = 0$.
